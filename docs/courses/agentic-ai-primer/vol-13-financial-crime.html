01Part A · The landscape

Financial crime, briefly

Twelve volumes built the foundation; here it pays off. To see where agentic AI fits, start with the problem it's applied to — and why that problem has defeated conventional automation for decades.

Financial crime spans fraud (deceiving someone out of money or assets), money laundering (disguising illicit proceeds), terrorist financing, sanctions evasion, and market abuse. Institutions defend against it with layered controls — transaction monitoring, screening, customer due diligence, investigation, and reporting — staffed by large teams of analysts. The defining pain of this work is its alert volume and false-positive rate: rule-based systems flag enormous numbers of transactions, the overwhelming majority of which are legitimate, and humans must investigate each. It is expensive, slow, and demoralising, yet the genuine crimes hide in that flood.

Why this is the showcase

Financial crime is investigation-heavy, evidence-driven, judgement-laden, and buried in false positives — exactly the profile of work where agentic AI's ability to gather, reason over, and synthesise information at scale (Vols. 04–06) creates the most value, while its judgement limits (Vol. 02) keep humans on the decisions.

02Part A · The landscape

Why agentic AI fits

Earlier AI in financial crime was mostly predictive — models scoring transactions for risk. Useful, but it produced more alerts without helping work them. Agentic AI is different in kind: it can do the investigation, not just raise the flag.

The match is precise. Investigation is multi-step and path-dependent — exactly ReAct and planning (Vol. 06). It requires pulling evidence from many systems — exactly tools and retrieval (Vols. 04–05). It demands a written, defensible rationale — exactly grounded, structured generation (Vols. 03, 05). And it must stay within tight controls — exactly governed, human-overseen autonomy (Vols. 10–12). An agent can take an alert, gather the evidence a human would, assess it against known patterns, and produce a documented recommendation — turning a raw alert into a worked, decision-ready case.

Where it doesn't fit

Agentic AI does not replace the analyst's judgement on whether something is criminal, and it must not make the irreversible regulated decisions — filing, account closure, sanctions determinations — on its own (Vol. 01 §7). Its value is the hours of preparation before those decisions, not the decisions themselves. Overstating this is the fastest way to a compliance failure.

Part B · Defence
03Part B · Defence

Fraud detection & investigation

Fraud is where speed matters most — decisions in seconds, at huge volume — and where the division of labour between fast models and reasoning agents is clearest.

The real-time scoring of a transaction stays with fast, specialised models; an agent reasoning for several seconds can't sit in a card-authorisation path. Where agents transform fraud work is after the flag: investigating flagged cases, assembling the customer's history and the transaction's context, checking related accounts and devices, weighing the evidence against known fraud patterns, and producing a recommendation with its rationale. They also help fraud teams adapt — surfacing emerging patterns and drafting the analysis that informs new rules — compressing investigation from many minutes of manual work to a reviewed agent output.

The division of labour

Fast models detect in real time; agents investigate what's detected. The two are complementary, not competing — and recognising which job needs which (Vol. 06 §8) is the core design insight for applying AI to fraud.

04Part B · Defence

Transaction monitoring

Transaction monitoring is the engine of the false-positive problem: rule-based systems generate vast alert volumes, most of them noise, and the cost of working them dominates many financial-crime budgets. It is the single biggest target for agentic AI.

Agents attack it on two fronts. Alert triage: an agent reviews each alert, gathers the surrounding context, and assesses whether it warrants escalation — clearing obvious false positives with a documented rationale and routing genuine concerns to analysts with the evidence already assembled. Tuning support: agents analyse alert patterns and outcomes to help teams refine the rules and thresholds that generate the noise in the first place. The promise is to lift the routine triage burden so scarce analyst attention concentrates on the alerts that actually matter.

Watch out

An agent clearing alerts is making risk decisions, even when "only" closing false positives — a missed true positive is a serious failure with regulatory consequences. This demands rigorous evaluation (Vol. 09), tight monitoring for coverage drift (Vol. 09 §7), conservative thresholds for what an agent may auto-clear, and human oversight calibrated to the risk (Vol. 11 §3). Efficiency must never quietly erode detection.

05Part B · Defence

The agentic investigation

Bringing the patterns together: a worked picture of how a financial-crime investigation runs as a governed agentic workflow — the synthesis of the whole series applied to one case.

An alert, investigated
Plan
"Establish the customer, map the flagged activity, screen the counterparties, assess against laundering typologies, draft a recommendation." (Vol. 06 §4)
Gather
Retrieve the customer profile, transaction history, and prior cases; pull counterparty and device data via tools. (Vols. 04–05)
Reason
"Seven transfers to high-risk jurisdictions, structured below reporting thresholds, to counterparties with no commercial link — consistent with layering."
Screen
Check counterparties against sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media sources. (Vol. 14 §4)
Draft
Produce a structured case file: findings, evidence with citations, typology assessment, and a recommendation — every claim grounded. (Vols. 03, 05)
Reflect
Self-check the narrative against the evidence before handoff. (Vol. 06 §5)
Escalate
Route the decision-ready case to a human analyst, who decides and — if warranted — files. The agent never files alone. (Vol. 01 §7)

The agent has done the hours of gathering, cross-referencing, and drafting that precede a disposition — consistently, traceably, and at scale — while the judgement and the regulated action remain with the accountable human. This is the shape of agentic financial-crime work, and every step rests on an earlier volume.

Part C · Threat & control
06Part C · Threat & control

Agentic fraud — the offensive side

The uncomfortable truth: the same capabilities that strengthen defence also arm attackers. Criminals have the same agents, and agentic AI is reshaping the threat as much as the response.

The offensive uses are real and growing: highly personalised, scaled social-engineering and phishing; synthetic identities and deepfake media to defeat onboarding and verification; automated probing of controls; and agents that orchestrate multi-step fraud faster than human defenders can react. The asymmetry is stark — attackers face none of the governance, accuracy, or compliance constraints that (rightly) bind defenders, so they can move faster and more recklessly.

The implication

Defence can no longer assume a human-speed adversary. As attacks become agent-driven and automated, static rules and manual response fall behind; defenders need the adaptive, fast, scaled capabilities of agentic AI simply to keep pace. The arms race is now agent-versus-agent, and opting out is not a safe option.

07Part C · Threat & control

Governance & model risk

A financial-crime agent sits at the intersection of every control concern in this series: it touches the most sensitive data, influences regulated decisions, and is itself a target. Its governance is correspondingly demanding — and non-negotiable.

The requirements compound from earlier volumes. As a model influencing regulated outcomes, it falls under model risk management — validated, monitored, documented, independently challenged (Vol. 12 §4). As an agent with data access and actions, it needs least privilege, gated writes, and defence in depth (Vol. 10). Its decisions must be explainable and auditable, with every disposition grounded in cited evidence and fully traced (Vol. 12 §6). And a missed crime is a coverage failure with regulatory consequences, so evaluation and drift monitoring must prove sustained detection, not just efficiency (Vol. 09). The accountable owner answers for all of it.

The synthesis

This is why the engineering, evaluation, security, governance, and regulation volumes came first. A financial-crime agent is only responsible if it is grounded, traced, secured, validated, overseen, and owned simultaneously. None of those is optional, and together they are what make the efficiency gains safe to capture.

Glossary

Alert triage
Reviewing and prioritising monitoring alerts to clear false positives and escalate genuine concerns.
False positive
An alert that, on investigation, reflects legitimate activity — the dominant cost in monitoring.
Financial crime
Fraud, money laundering, terrorist financing, sanctions evasion, and market abuse.
Layering
A money-laundering stage that disguises the origin of funds through complex movements.
Synthetic identity
A fabricated identity combining real and false data, used to defraud — increasingly AI-generated.
Transaction monitoring
Screening transactions against rules and patterns to flag potential financial crime.
Typology
A known pattern or method of financial crime used to assess suspicious activity.
Agentic investigation
An agent gathering evidence, assessing it, and drafting a decision-ready case for human disposition.
