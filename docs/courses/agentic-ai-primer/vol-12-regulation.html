01Part A · The landscape

The regulatory map

Governance (Vol. 11) is what an organisation chooses; regulation is what authorities require — and getting it wrong carries fines, enforcement, and reputational damage. For agentic AI in financial services, the rules come from two directions at once, and you must satisfy both.

There is the horizontal layer of AI-specific regulation and standards that apply across industries — the EU AI Act, the NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001 — and the vertical layer of long-standing financial-services rules that already govern models, decisions, data, and conduct, and that apply to an AI system just as they would to any other. An agent in a bank sits in the overlap, bound by both. Mapping which rules bite for a given use case is the first compliance task.

The shape of it

Most obligations are risk-based: the higher the stakes of the use case, the heavier the requirements. This dovetails with the risk tiering of Vol. 11 §2 — tier a use case once, and you've largely scoped both your governance and your regulatory burden.

Watch out

Regulation here is fast-moving and jurisdiction-specific, and this primer is a snapshot. Treat everything below as orientation, not legal advice, and confirm current obligations with compliance and counsel for your jurisdictions before acting.

02Part A · The landscape

The EU AI Act

The EU AI Act is the most comprehensive AI law to date and a likely template for others, so it's worth understanding by structure even outside the EU. Its core idea is a risk-based tiering of AI systems with obligations scaling to risk.

TierTreatment
Unacceptable riskProhibited outright (e.g. social scoring of the kind the Act bans).
High riskPermitted with heavy obligations — risk management, data governance, documentation, transparency, human oversight, accuracy and security. Much creditworthiness and similar financial use falls here.
Limited riskTransparency obligations (e.g. telling people they're interacting with AI).
Minimal riskLargely unregulated.

The high-risk obligations read almost like a specification for everything earlier volumes described: documented risk management (Vol. 11), data governance (Vol. 05), transparency and explainability (§6), human oversight (Vol. 11 §3), and robustness and security (Vols. 09–10). Build an agent well by this series' standards, and you have already done much of what the Act demands.

Part B · Frameworks
03Part B · Frameworks

NIST AI RMF & ISO 42001

Where the AI Act is binding law, two frameworks are voluntary but widely adopted — and increasingly the way organisations demonstrate they are managing AI risk responsibly. They are the practical scaffolding for compliance.

NIST AI RMF
A risk-management process

Organised around four functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage — it's a flexible, widely-referenced process for identifying and treating AI risk. Maps directly onto the governance and evaluation of Vols. 09 and 11.

ISO/IEC 42001
A certifiable management system

The first AI management-system standard — an auditable, certifiable structure for governing AI across its lifecycle, analogous to ISO 27001 for security.

Neither is law, but both matter: regulators and clients increasingly expect alignment to them, and they translate high-level obligations into concrete, checkable practices. Adopting one gives an organisation a defensible, recognised answer to "how do you manage AI risk?"

04Part B · Frameworks

Model risk management

For financial institutions, the most directly applicable regime is one that predates modern AI entirely: model risk management (MRM). An agent that influences a regulated decision is, in the eyes of these rules, a model — and decades of MRM expectation apply.

The lineage runs from the US supervisory guidance SR 11-7 (and its successors in the evolving supervisory line) and equivalents elsewhere. The core expectations: models must be validated independently before use, monitored on an ongoing basis, thoroughly documented, and subject to independent challenge separate from the team that built them. Every one of these maps onto capabilities from earlier volumes — validation is evaluation against a golden set (Vol. 09 §3), monitoring is drift detection (Vol. 09 §7), the independent-challenge structure is the three lines of defence (Vol. 11 §5).

The key reframing

Banks should not treat agentic AI as an exotic new thing demanding a brand-new rulebook. It is, for regulatory purposes, a model — often a complex, autonomous, acting one — and it slots into the MRM frameworks institutions already run. The novelty is the autonomy and action, which extend MRM rather than escape it.

05Part B · Frameworks

Data & sector rules

Beyond AI-specific rules, agents are bound by the data-protection and sector regulations that already govern the information and decisions they touch. These often bite hardest in practice.

Data protection (GDPR and its global counterparts) governs personal data an agent processes — lawful basis, minimisation, purpose limitation — and includes provisions on automated decision-making and a right to explanation that bear directly on agentic decisions (§6). Sector rules add more: AML/CFT obligations (Vols. 13–14), consumer-protection and fair-lending rules, conduct and suitability requirements, and record-keeping mandates. An agent doesn't get a pass on any of these because it's AI — if a human doing the task would be bound, so is the agent, and the accountable owner (Vol. 11 §2) answers for it.

Watch out

Data-protection and confidentiality rules constrain the architecture, not just the policy: where data can be processed, which model endpoints it may reach, what must stay on-premise (Vol. 02 §10, Vol. 10 §5). Compliance here is designed into the system from the start — it cannot be retrofitted onto an agent already wired to send data anywhere.

Part C · Building for it
06Part C · Building for it

Explainability & audit

Two requirements recur across nearly every regime above: an organisation must be able to explain why an AI-influenced decision was made, and to reconstruct what happened after the fact. For agents, these are design requirements, not reporting afterthoughts.

Explainability is challenging because models are not transparent (Vol. 02 §8). The practical answer is not to open the model but to make the process explainable: ground decisions in retrieved, cited evidence (Vol. 05), capture the reasoning trace (Vol. 06), and structure the output so the basis of a decision is recorded as data, not buried in prose. Auditability is the observability of Vol. 09 §6 serving a regulatory purpose — a complete trace of inputs, decisions, tool calls, and the context shown, retained so a decision can be examined months later. The same instrumentation that lets engineers debug an agent lets compliance defend it.

The convergence

Explainability and auditability are where good engineering and regulatory compliance become the same activity. Grounding, tracing, and structured output — built for quality and reliability — are exactly what the regulator asks for. Build for one and you largely get the other.

07Part C · Building for it

A compliant agent

Pulling the series together: what does a regulation-ready agent actually look like? Strikingly, it is just an agent built well by everything taught so far — compliance is largely the by-product of good engineering and governance.

Grounded
Decisions from cited evidence

Answers tied to retrieved sources, not model memory (Vol. 05) — the basis of explainability and the defence against hallucination.

Traced
Every step recorded

Full observability of inputs, reasoning, tools, and outputs (Vol. 09) — the audit trail.

Overseen
Humans on the consequential

The right oversight model per risk tier (Vol. 11 §3); irreversible actions gated.

Validated & monitored
Tested before, watched after

Independent validation and ongoing monitoring per MRM (§4, Vol. 09).

Secured
Least privilege, controlled data

Defence in depth and data handling that meets confidentiality and residency rules (Vols. 05, 10).

Owned
A named accountable human

Clear ownership and documented governance across the lifecycle (Vol. 11).

The payoff of the whole series

There is no separate "compliance mode" to bolt on. An agent built to this series' standards — grounded, traced, overseen, validated, secured, owned — is, by construction, most of the way to compliant. That is the deepest reason the engineering disciplines came first.

08Part C · Building for it

Outlook

The regulatory picture is unsettled and moving fast — but the direction of travel is clear enough to design for, even as specifics shift.

Expect more AI-specific regulation globally, much of it echoing the EU's risk-based structure; growing regulatory attention to autonomous and acting systems specifically, where today's rules were mostly written for predictive models; and convergence of standards (NIST, ISO) into de-facto expectations. The robust strategy is not to chase each rule but to build on durable principles — accountability, transparency, oversight, validation, security — that nearly every regime shares. An organisation grounded in those adapts to new specifics with far less rework than one that engineered to the letter of a single regulation.
