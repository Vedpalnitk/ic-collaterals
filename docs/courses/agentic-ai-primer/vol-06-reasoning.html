01Part A · Single-step

The reasoning toolkit

An agent's intelligence is not just its model — it is how the model is prompted to think across steps. The same model, wrapped in a better reasoning pattern, solves problems it otherwise can't. This volume is the catalogue of those patterns and the judgement to choose among them.

The patterns form a progression. The simplest improve a single answer (chain-of-thought). The next interleave thinking with action (ReAct). Then come patterns that plan before acting (plan-and-execute), check their own work (reflection), and explore multiple paths (search). Each adds capability and cost; the skill is using the lightest pattern that the task actually needs.

Framing

These are not competing products — they are composable moves. A real agent might plan at the top level, use ReAct within each step, and reflect on the final output. Learn them individually, then combine.

02Part A · Single-step

Chain-of-thought & variants

The foundation of all agent reasoning: get the model to work through intermediate steps before committing to an answer. Spreading reasoning across more tokens, each conditioning the next, measurably raises accuracy on anything multi-step (Vol. 02 §5, Vol. 03 §6).

Several variants extend the idea. Zero-shot CoT simply asks the model to reason step by step. Few-shot CoT shows worked examples that include their reasoning, teaching the model the style of working you want. Decomposition breaks a task into explicit sub-questions tackled in turn. With reasoning models (Vol. 02 §5), much of this is internal by default — so on those models, lighter and more direct prompting often beats heavy "think step by step" scaffolding.

Watch out

Visible reasoning is an audit aid, not a correctness guarantee. A model can produce a fluent chain that's wrong at step two and carries the error confidently to the end. Use the reasoning to inspect and catch failures — never to certify the answer.

Part B · Acting & planning
03Part B · Acting & planning

ReAct in depth

ReAct — reason and act, interleaved — is the workhorse of production agents. It is the agent loop of Vol. 01 made explicit in the model's own output: a thought, an action, an observation, repeated. Its power is grounding: each step is informed by what the world actually returned, not by the model's assumptions.

ReAct on an alert
Thought
"I need the customer's recent activity."
Action
get_transactions(A-4471, 90)
Observation
7 transfers to high-risk jurisdictions in week 3.
Thought
"Unusual — are these counterparties sanctioned?"
Action
screen_counterparties([...]) → loop continues

ReAct shines where information is needed incrementally and the path can't be known in advance — investigation, research, troubleshooting. Its weakness is long-horizon coherence: with no overall plan, a ReAct agent can wander, repeat itself, or lose the thread on a complex multi-stage task. That is exactly the gap the next pattern fills.

04Part B · Acting & planning

Plan-and-execute

For complex, multi-stage tasks, the agent first drafts an explicit plan, then executes its steps — re-planning when reality diverges. This adds long-horizon coherence and makes the agent's intent inspectable before it acts.

Plan-and-execute: decide the route first, execute the steps, re-plan when needed.

The benefits are real for long tasks: less aimless wandering, a plan a human can review before any action runs (valuable when actions are costly or irreversible), and natural points to delegate steps to sub-agents (Vol. 07). The cost is rigidity — a plan made up front can be wrong — which is why good implementations re-plan as observations come in, blending planning with ReAct-style adaptivity.

05Part B · Acting & planning

Reflection & self-correction

Agents can review and improve their own work. In a reflection loop, a generator produces an answer, a critic (often the same model in a different role) evaluates it against the goal, and the generator revises — repeating until it passes or a budget is hit.

Reflection catches a meaningful share of errors that a single pass misses, especially on quality-critical output: a regulatory narrative, a piece of code, a complex analysis. A simple, effective version is built right into a prompt: "draft an answer, list every way it could be wrong, then give a corrected final version." The trade-off is cost — each reflection cycle is another full pass — so reserve it for output where a second look genuinely pays.

Pairs well with grounding

Reflection is strongest when the critic can check against something real — retrieved sources, tool results, a test that runs. "Critique against the evidence" beats "critique from intuition", because otherwise a confident model may simply re-affirm its own mistake.

06Part B · Acting & planning

Search & exploration

The most powerful — and most expensive — reasoning patterns don't follow one line of thought; they explore several and choose the best. Where chain-of-thought is a single path, search-based reasoning branches.

Patterns like tree-of-thought generate multiple candidate steps at each point, evaluate them, and pursue the most promising — backtracking from dead ends. Self-consistency samples several independent reasoning paths and takes the majority answer. These approaches lift accuracy on hard problems with many possible routes, at a steep cost in tokens and time. In practice they are reserved for high-value problems where correctness dominates and the compute is justified — and increasingly, much of this exploratory behaviour is what reasoning models do internally.

Watch out

Exploration cost grows fast — branching multiplies calls. Most enterprise tasks do not need tree search; a well-grounded ReAct or plan-and-execute agent is usually the right and far cheaper choice. Reach for search only when the problem genuinely has many paths and a wrong answer is expensive.

Part C · Choosing
07Part C · Choosing

Matching pattern to task

The practical skill is selection. Most tasks are well served by the simpler patterns; complexity should be added only when the task demands it.

Task shapePatternWhy
Single multi-step answerChain-of-thoughtCheap accuracy lift; no tools needed.
Needs live info, path unknownReActGrounds each step in real observations. The default for investigation.
Long, multi-stage, reviewablePlan-and-executeCoherence over the horizon; inspectable plan.
Quality-critical outputReflectionA second pass catches errors before they ship.
Many paths, high stakesSearchExplores alternatives — only when worth the cost.
Default

Start with ReAct plus solid grounding (Vol. 05). Add planning when the horizon is long, reflection when output quality is critical, and search only for genuinely branchy, high-value problems. Simpler is cheaper, faster, and easier to debug.

08Part C · Choosing

Cost, latency & reliability

Every reasoning pattern spends tokens, and tokens are time, money, and — through compounding (Vol. 01 §9) — risk. The pattern you choose is a cost and reliability decision as much as a capability one.

More reasoning means more tokens per step and often more steps, which raises both latency and spend, and gives small errors more places to accumulate. The countervailing truth is that the right reasoning reduces failures that would otherwise require expensive rework or human cleanup. The balance is found empirically: measure the accuracy each pattern buys against what it costs, per task, and choose the point where the curve flattens — not the most elaborate pattern available.

Per-step, not per-system

The highest-leverage move is to vary reasoning depth within an agent: a reasoning model and reflection on the hard, consequential step; a fast standard model and a single pass on the routine ones. Uniform sophistication wastes money on the easy steps and under-serves the hard ones.

09Part C · Choosing

Reasoning in financial crime

Financial-crime work is a near-ideal showcase for these patterns: investigations are multi-step, evidence arrives incrementally, and the output must be defensible — which maps cleanly onto ReAct for gathering, planning for structure, and reflection for the written record.

A typical agentic investigation (detailed in Vols. 13–14) blends them: a plan frames the investigation (establish identity, map activity, screen counterparties, assess against typologies); ReAct drives each stage as evidence surfaces and dictates the next query; and reflection reviews the drafted narrative against the evidence before it reaches a human — checking that every claim is supported, the regulated reason for hallucination control (Vol. 02 §7). The reasoning trace itself becomes part of the audit record.

Watch out

In regulated decisions, the appeal of visible reasoning can mislead. A clean chain-of-thought makes a wrong disposition look authoritative. Reasoning supports the human reviewer; it does not replace them, and it is never the basis on its own for an irreversible action (Vol. 01 §7).

Glossary

Chain-of-thought
Producing intermediate reasoning steps before an answer to improve multi-step accuracy.
Decomposition
Breaking a complex task into explicit sub-questions tackled in sequence.
Plan-and-execute
Drafting an explicit multi-step plan, then executing it and re-planning as needed.
ReAct
Interleaving reasoning and action — thought, action, observation, repeat — the default agent pattern.
Reflection
A generator–critic loop where an agent reviews and revises its own output before finalising.
Self-consistency
Sampling several reasoning paths and taking the majority answer.
Tree-of-thought
Branching exploration of multiple candidate reasoning steps, with evaluation and backtracking.
Inference-time compute
Computation spent while answering; reasoning-heavy patterns spend more of it.
