01Part A · The problem

Why models need memory

A model knows two things: what it absorbed in training (frozen, general, and dated) and what you place in its context right now. It does not know your policies, your customers, yesterday's transactions, or what it concluded five minutes ago. Memory and retrieval are how you supply all of that.

Two gaps drive everything in this volume. The knowledge gap: the model's training is generic and ends at a cutoff (Vol. 02 §4), so it lacks your private data and anything recent. The continuity gap: a model has no recall between calls, so without help an agent forgets what it just did. Retrieval closes the first; memory closes the second.

Takeaway

"What does the model know?" always reduces to "what's in its context?" (Vol. 03 §1). This volume is the discipline of getting the right external knowledge and the right past state into that context, at the right moment.

02Part A · The problem

Context vs. memory

The words get used loosely; the distinction is sharp and useful. Short-term context is the window you engineer per call. Long-term memory is the external store you read from and write to across calls.

Short-term
Working / session

The current conversation and task state, held in the context window. Fast, in front of the model — and wiped when the session ends.

Long-term · facts
Semantic memory

Durable knowledge: policies, customer preferences, prior conclusions. Stored externally, retrieved when relevant.

Long-term · events
Episodic memory

A record of what happened: past cases, prior runs, what was tried and how it went. Lets the agent learn across sessions.

The mechanics differ. Short-term memory is a context-engineering problem — selection, ordering, compaction (Vol. 03 §8–9). Long-term memory is a retrieval and storage problem — how to keep knowledge outside the window and pull back exactly the right slice. Most of this volume is the second, because that is where the heavy machinery lives.

Part B · Retrieval
03Part B · Retrieval

RAG fundamentals

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is the dominant way to give a model knowledge it wasn't trained on: fetch relevant material from a store and place it in the context, so the model answers from your data rather than its memory. It is the single most important technique for grounding (Vol. 02 §7).

RAG has two phases: index your knowledge once, then retrieve-and-augment on every query.

The flow is two-phase. Indexing happens once and ahead of time: documents are split into chunks, converted to embeddings (§4), and stored in a vector database. Querying happens per request: the question is embedded, the most similar chunks are retrieved, inserted into the context, and the model answers from them. The model's job becomes reading and synthesising provided sources rather than recalling from training.

Why it's the workhorse

RAG is cheaper and faster to update than fine-tuning, keeps a citable source for every claim (vital for audit), and lets you change the knowledge by changing the documents — no retraining. For adding facts, it is almost always the right lever (Vol. 02 §6).

04Part B · Retrieval

Embeddings & vector search

RAG rests on one idea: turning meaning into geometry. An embedding is a vector of numbers representing a piece of text, arranged so that similar meanings sit close together. This is what makes "find passages about this" a fast mathematical lookup rather than a keyword match.

Keyword search finds documents that share words; it misses "sanctions evasion" when the text says "circumventing restrictions." Embedding (semantic) search finds them by meaning, because both map to nearby vectors. A vector database stores these embeddings and answers "nearest neighbours to this query vector" quickly across millions of chunks.

Watch out

Semantic search returns the most similar chunks, not necessarily the correct ones — and it can confidently surface plausible-but-irrelevant text. In practice the strongest systems combine semantic and keyword search (hybrid retrieval) and often re-rank results, because pure vector similarity alone misses exact terms like names, IDs, and codes that matter enormously in financial crime.

05Part B · Retrieval

Chunking & indexing

The least glamorous part of RAG is the one that most often decides whether it works: how you split documents into chunks. Retrieval can only return what indexing made findable.

Chunks that are too large dilute relevance and waste context budget; too small, and they lose the surrounding meaning that makes them useful. Good chunking respects the document's natural structure — sections, clauses, paragraphs — so each chunk is a coherent, self-contained unit. Metadata (source, date, document type, section) stored alongside each chunk lets you filter retrieval ("only this customer's files", "only post-2024 policy") and, critically, cite the source.

The quiet truth

"My RAG gives bad answers" is, far more often than not, a retrieval problem, not a model problem: the right chunk wasn't retrieved, or wasn't findable because of how it was chunked. Fix indexing and retrieval before blaming the model — which is why §8 (evaluating retrieval) matters as much as the generation.

06Part B · Retrieval

Agentic & advanced RAG

Classic RAG retrieves once, up front, with the raw question. That works for simple lookups and breaks on hard, multi-part ones. The advances all move in one direction: making retrieval smarter and iterative — and ultimately handing it to the agent itself.

Several techniques improve a single retrieval: query rewriting (reformulating a vague question into a better search), re-ranking (a second pass that reorders candidates by true relevance), and hybrid search (combining semantic and keyword). Agentic RAG goes further and gives retrieval to the agent as a tool: it decides whether it needs to look something up, what to search for, can try again when results are thin, search multiple sources, and keep going until it has enough.

The shift

Retrieval stops being a fixed pre-step and becomes part of the reasoning loop (Vol. 06). The agent that investigates a financial-crime alert doesn't retrieve once — it pulls the profile, sees something odd, searches for the counterparties, finds a match, retrieves the related case. More capable on hard questions; more calls and more variability to manage.

07Part B · Retrieval

Structured & graph memory

Not all knowledge is unstructured text. Much of what matters in financial services is relational — who owns what, who transacted with whom, which entities connect — and that calls for memory beyond a pile of documents.

A knowledge graph stores entities and the relationships between them: customers, accounts, beneficial owners, counterparties, and the links among them. Where vector search answers "what text is similar to this?", a graph answers "what is connected to this, and how?" — exactly the question in network-based fraud and money-laundering analysis. Agents increasingly combine both: vector retrieval for narrative context, graph queries for relationships, structured database lookups for precise facts and figures.

Match the memory to the question

Use vector/RAG for "find relevant text", a structured database for "give me the exact value", and a knowledge graph for "show me the connections". An agent that can reach all three reasons over the full shape of the problem — text, facts, and relationships — rather than just one slice.

Part C · In practice
08Part C · In practice

Evaluating retrieval

Because most RAG failures are retrieval failures, you have to measure retrieval separately from generation. Evaluating only the final answer hides whether the model was given the right material in the first place.

What to measureThe question it answers
Retrieval relevanceDid we fetch the chunks that actually contain the answer? (Did the right evidence even reach the context?)
Groundedness / faithfulnessDoes the answer stick to the retrieved sources, or did the model add unsupported claims?
Answer relevanceGiven good evidence, did the answer address the question asked?
Citation accuracyDo the cited sources actually support the statements attributed to them?

Separating these tells you where to fix: poor relevance means an indexing/retrieval problem (§5–6); good relevance but poor groundedness means a prompting problem (Vol. 03) — the model isn't being held to its sources. This component-level discipline is the seed of full agent evaluation in Vol. 09.

09Part C · In practice

Memory & RAG in financial services

In a regulated setting, grounding is not just a quality technique — it is the mechanism that makes a model's output explainable and defensible. RAG, done well, is much of what makes agentic AI usable in a bank at all.

The benefits map directly onto the constraints from Vol. 02 §10. Auditability: every answer carries a citation to a source document, so a decision can be reconstructed and defended. Currency: knowledge updates by changing documents, not retraining, so policy and watchlist changes propagate immediately. Confidentiality: private data stays in controlled stores and enters the context only for the specific query — though that also means the retrieval layer itself becomes sensitive infrastructure with its own access controls.

Watch out

Retrieval is an attack surface too. A poisoned document in the knowledge store, or injected content returned by a retrieval tool, reaches the model as trusted context (Vol. 04 §7). And retrieval permissions must mirror data permissions — an agent must not retrieve a customer record the requesting user isn't entitled to see. Govern the store as carefully as the model.

Glossary

Agentic RAG
Retrieval handed to the agent as a tool, so it decides whether, what, and how often to retrieve.
Chunking
Splitting documents into retrievable units before embedding; quality strongly affects retrieval.
Embedding
A numeric vector representing text's meaning, such that similar meanings sit close together.
Episodic memory
A stored record of past events and runs, letting an agent learn across sessions.
Groundedness
The degree to which an answer is supported by its retrieved sources rather than invented.
Hybrid retrieval
Combining semantic (embedding) and keyword search to capture both meaning and exact terms.
Knowledge graph
A store of entities and their relationships, answering "what connects to what" rather than "what's similar".
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation: fetching relevant material into context so the model answers from your data.
Re-ranking
A second-pass reordering of retrieved candidates by true relevance to the query.
Semantic memory
Durable factual knowledge stored externally and retrieved when relevant.
Vector database
A store of embeddings supporting fast nearest-neighbour (similarity) search.
