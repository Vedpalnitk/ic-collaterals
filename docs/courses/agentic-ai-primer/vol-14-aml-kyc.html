01Part A · The lifecycle

The AML / KYC landscape

Vol. 13 covered financial crime broadly; this volume focuses on the compliance lifecycle that anti-money-laundering obligations impose — onboarding to filing — because that lifecycle is where most of the cost sits and where agentic AI has the clearest, most immediate return.

AML/KYC obligations require institutions to know who their customers are, understand their expected behaviour, monitor for deviations, and report suspicion. In practice this becomes a lifecycle: onboarding and CDD (verifying identity and assessing risk), screening (against sanctions, politically-exposed-person and adverse-media sources), ongoing monitoring (transactions and periodic review), alert disposition (working what monitoring flags), and reporting (filing suspicious-activity/transaction reports). Each stage is document-heavy, repetitive, and judgement-laden — and each is staffed by large, costly teams.

The pattern, restated

Across every stage the agentic value is the same: automate the gathering, checking, and drafting that precede a decision, at consistency and scale humans can't match, while the decision and the regulated filing stay with an accountable person. This volume is that pattern applied stage by stage.

02Part A · The lifecycle

Where agents help

Not every stage benefits equally. Mapping the lifecycle against the agentic strengths and limits established across the series shows where to invest first.

StageAgentic fit
Onboarding / CDDHigh — document gathering, verification, risk assessment, profile drafting (§3).
ScreeningHigh — adjudicating matches, especially noisy name and adverse-media hits (§4).
Alert triageHigh — the false-positive flood; investigate, clear, escalate (§5).
SAR / STR draftingMedium — draft the narrative from evidence; human approves and files (§6).
Final disposition / filingHuman — the regulated decision stays with an accountable person.

The green stages share a profile: high volume, well-defined, evidence-driven, and currently drowning teams in routine work — the sweet spot. The pattern, again, is high autonomy on preparation and a firm human line on the regulated act.

Part B · The workflow
03Part B · The workflow

KYC / CDD onboarding

Customer due diligence at onboarding is information-assembly at scale: collect documents, verify identity, understand the customer and their expected activity, and assign a risk rating. It is slow, manual, and a notorious source of onboarding friction — and a strong agentic fit.

An agent can orchestrate much of it: gather and check submitted documents, cross-reference against authoritative sources, pull and synthesise relevant external information about the customer and (for entities) their ownership structure, identify gaps and request what's missing, and draft a structured risk assessment with its supporting evidence. For lower-risk, clear-cut cases this can run at high autonomy with light review; for higher-risk cases (complex ownership, elevated-risk jurisdictions) it prepares the file and routes to a specialist. Either way, the analyst starts from an assembled, evidenced profile rather than a blank form.

Watch out

Onboarding decisions are regulated risk decisions. The agent assembles and assesses; the risk rating and the decision to onboard remain a controlled, accountable step, calibrated by risk tier (Vol. 11). And because onboarding handles sensitive identity data and ingests external content, the data-handling and injection controls of Vols. 05 and 10 apply with full force.

04Part B · The workflow

Screening

Screening checks customers and counterparties against sanctions lists, PEP databases, and adverse media. Its defining problem is the same false-positive flood as monitoring: fuzzy name-matching generates huge volumes of hits, the vast majority irrelevant, each needing human adjudication.

Agents are well-suited to the adjudication. For a potential match, an agent can gather the context a human would — comparing identifiers, dates, locations, and known details — to assess whether it's a true match or a coincidence, and document the reasoning. Adverse-media screening is an especially strong fit: it's fundamentally a reading-and-summarising task, where an agent reviews sources, judges relevance and severity, and produces a grounded summary far faster than manual review. The effect is to clear the noise with an audit trail and surface real matches with the analysis already done.

Watch out

Clearing a screening hit is a high-stakes call: dismissing a true sanctions match is a serious regulatory breach. Auto-clearing must be conservative and confidence-gated, true and borderline matches must go to humans, every adjudication must be grounded and traced (Vol. 12 §6), and coverage must be evaluated continuously (Vol. 09) — efficiency can never come at the cost of a missed match.

05Part B · The workflow

Alert triage & disposition

This is the heart of the operational burden and the marquee agentic use case: working the relentless stream of monitoring alerts, most of which are false positives, each currently demanding manual investigation.

The agentic investigation of Vol. 13 §5 applies directly. For each alert, the agent gathers the customer and transaction context, checks related accounts and counterparties, assesses the activity against expected behaviour and known typologies, and produces a disposition recommendation with grounded evidence. Clear false positives can be cleared with a documented rationale; genuine concerns are escalated to analysts with the case already built. The aim is to concentrate scarce analyst attention on the alerts that matter, with the routine investigation already done — and to make every disposition, cleared or escalated, fully auditable.

Why this is the priority

Alert triage combines the highest volume, the worst false-positive ratio, and the most repetitive investigation in the lifecycle — so it offers the largest efficiency gain and the strongest case for agentic AI. It is where most institutions should start, precisely because the burden and the fit are both greatest.

06Part B · The workflow

SAR / STR drafting

When suspicion is confirmed, the institution files a suspicious-activity (or transaction) report — a structured narrative laying out what was found and why it's suspicious. Drafting these is time-consuming and a textbook fit for grounded generation; filing them is a regulated act that stays human.

An agent that has investigated a case can draft the report: a clear, structured narrative pulling together the activity, the evidence, and the typology assessment, written to the expected format, with every assertion grounded in and citable to the case evidence (Vols. 03, 05). The analyst reviews, corrects, and approves — working from a complete draft rather than a blank page. This is genuinely valuable: narrative drafting is a major time sink, and quality and consistency often improve. But the human review is mandatory, not optional.

Watch out

A SAR is a legal filing; an unsupported or wrong claim has real consequences. This is precisely where hallucination control matters most (Vol. 02 §7): the agent must draft only from established evidence, every statement traceable to its source, and a human must verify before filing. The agent drafts; it never files.

Part C · Operating
07Part C · Operating

Governance & the human line

Across the whole lifecycle, one principle holds the design together: a clear, deliberate line between what the agent does autonomously and what requires a human. Drawing that line correctly is the core governance act of an AML/KYC deployment.

The line follows the consequence-and-reversibility logic of Vol. 01 §7 and the oversight models of Vol. 11 §3. Reversible, evidence-gathering, and drafting work runs at high autonomy. Risk decisions — onboarding approval, clearing a true match, dispositioning a genuine alert, filing a SAR — stay with accountable humans, with the agent preparing the ground. The whole lifecycle must be auditable: every gather, assessment, clearance, and draft traced and grounded so a regulator can reconstruct it (Vol. 12 §6). And the agents are models under MRM (Vol. 12 §4) — validated, monitored for coverage drift, and independently challenged.

The non-negotiable

Efficiency is the goal, but coverage is the constraint. An AML programme exists to catch financial crime; any agentic deployment that improves throughput while quietly degrading detection has failed, regardless of its productivity numbers. The human line and continuous coverage evaluation are what keep efficiency honest.

08Part C · Operating

A target operating model

Putting it together: what does an agentic AML/KYC function look like, and how does the work — and the workforce — change? The shift is from analysts doing investigation to analysts supervising and deciding.

In the target model, agents handle the first pass across the lifecycle — assembling onboarding files, adjudicating screening hits, triaging alerts, drafting reports — running on the centralised, governed platform of Vol. 11 §4. Analysts shift from manual investigators to reviewers, decision-makers, and supervisors of agentic work: handling escalations, deciding the consequential cases, and overseeing quality. Roles tilt toward judgement and oversight; headcount mixes shift toward higher-skilled review (the pyramid-to-diamond shift Vol. 15 §4 develops). The function becomes faster and more consistent, with humans concentrated where judgement and accountability genuinely require them.

Glossary

Adverse media
Negative news about a customer or counterparty, screened as a financial-crime risk signal.
AML / CFT
Anti-money-laundering / countering the financing of terrorism — the obligations driving the lifecycle.
CDD / EDD
Customer (and Enhanced) Due Diligence — assessing customer identity and risk at onboarding and review.
Disposition
The decision on an alert — cleared as a false positive, or escalated/reported.
KYC
Know Your Customer — verifying identity and understanding expected behaviour.
PEP
Politically Exposed Person — a category screened for elevated corruption risk.
SAR / STR
Suspicious Activity / Transaction Report — the regulated filing made when suspicion is confirmed.
Sanctions screening
Checking parties against sanctions lists; a false clear is a serious breach.

The future-state design of how the function's work, roles, and platform are organised.
