01Part A · Foundations

What AI governance is

Everything so far has been about building agents that work. Governance is about an organisation being able to deploy them responsibly, at scale, and answer for what they do. It is the bridge from "we built an agent" to "we can run hundreds of agents and stand behind every one."

AI governance is the set of policies, roles, processes, and controls through which an organisation decides what AI it will build, ensures those systems are safe and compliant, and holds people accountable for outcomes. It is not a committee that says no; done well, it is the machinery that lets an organisation say yes confidently — because the guardrails, ownership, and oversight are in place. Without it, agentic AI is either reckless or stuck.

Governance vs. regulation

Governance is what an organisation chooses to do to manage its own AI risk; regulation (Vol. 12) is what external authorities require. Good governance usually exceeds regulation and makes compliance a by-product. This volume is the internal machinery; the next is the external mandate.

02Part A · Foundations

Risk tiering & accountability

Not all AI carries the same risk, and treating it uniformly is the fastest route to either paralysis or recklessness. The foundation of practical governance is risk tiering: proportioning oversight to the stakes of each use case.

The dimensions echo the autonomy logic of Vol. 01 §7: how consequential and reversible are the agent's actions; how sensitive the data; how much human oversight sits in the loop; and how exposed it is to customers or regulation. A low-risk internal drafting assistant warrants light governance; an agent that influences a lending or financial-crime decision warrants the full apparatus — validation, monitoring, documented oversight, named ownership. Tiering directs scarce governance effort where the risk actually is.

The principle that anchors it all

For every AI system, a specific, named human or role is accountable for its behaviour and outcomes. AI does not absorb accountability — it never has and cannot. Tiering decides how much oversight an accountable owner must exercise, but it never removes the owner. "The model decided" is not a defence anyone can offer.

Part B · The operating model
03Part B · The operating model

Human-oversight models

"Keep a human in the loop" is the right instinct stated too vaguely. Effective governance is precise about which oversight model applies where, because the three models differ sharply in protection and in cost.

Strongest
Human-in-the-loop

A person approves each consequential action before it happens. Maximum control; necessary for high-stakes, irreversible steps (filing, closing, paying). Slowest.

Balanced
Human-on-the-loop

The agent acts; a person monitors and can intervene or override. Suits higher-volume, moderate-risk work where pre-approving everything is impractical.

Lightest
Human-out-of-the-loop

The agent acts autonomously, reviewed after the fact. Only for low-risk, reversible, well-evaluated tasks.

The model follows the risk tier (§2): in-the-loop for the consequential, on-the-loop for the routine-but-watched, out-of-the-loop only where a mistake is cheap and reversible. The art is calibration — too much oversight destroys the efficiency that justified the agent; too little exposes the organisation to its actions.

04Part B · The operating model

The agentic operating model

As an organisation moves from one agent to many, a structural question dominates: who builds and runs them? The pattern that has emerged as the durable answer is centralised platform, federated use — balancing control and speed instead of sacrificing one for the other.

Centralised platform, federated use — the operating model that scales agentic AI safely.

A central team owns the shared, risk-bearing layer — the platform, approved models, guardrails, evaluation and observability tooling, the MCP/tool gateway, and the governance policy itself (the technology realisation in Vol. 08 §7). Business functions, who know their domains, build and operate use-case agents on that platform. The centre sets the rails; the edges move fast within them. This avoids both the bottleneck of all-central and the chaos of all-federated.

05Part B · The operating model

Roles, RACI & ownership

Accountability that isn't assigned to named roles is just a sentiment. Governance turns the principle of §2 into an operating reality by defining who does what across an agent's life.

RoleResponsibility
Business / product ownerAccountable for the use case, its outcomes, and the value it delivers — the named owner.
Platform / engineeringBuilds and runs the agent on the shared platform; responsible for technical reliability.
Risk & complianceValidates against policy and regulation; sets risk tier and required controls (Vol. 12).
Model risk / validationIndependent challenge and validation of high-risk agents.
Operations / oversightThe humans in or on the loop; monitor, intervene, and handle escalations.

The separation of building from validating matters especially in financial services: the team that builds an agent should not be the sole judge of whether it's safe to deploy. Independent challenge — the "three lines of defence" familiar from banking risk management — applies directly to agentic AI.

06Part B · The operating model

Lifecycle governance

Governance is not a launch gate you pass once; it spans an agent's whole life, from the idea to its retirement. Risk enters at every stage, so control must too.

The governed lifecycle
Intake
Use case proposed, risk-tiered, approved to build. Required controls set up front.
Build
Developed on the platform with guardrails, evals, and observability baked in (Vols. 08–09).
Validate
Independent review against policy and regulation; deployment gated on passing.
Deploy
Released with the right oversight model (§3), limits, and a kill switch.
Monitor
Live quality, drift, and incidents tracked (Vol. 09 §7); periodic re-validation.
Retire
Decommissioned cleanly when superseded; access and data handled.

Each stage has an owner and a gate. The discipline mirrors model-risk management (Vol. 12 §4) — unsurprising, since a regulated agent is a model under management. Building this lifecycle once, as a platform capability, is what lets an organisation govern many agents without re-inventing oversight each time.

Part C · Making it real
07Part C · Making it real

Policy, standards & guardrails

Governance becomes real when it is encoded — in written policy people follow and in technical guardrails the platform enforces. Principles that live only in a slide deck change nothing.

Two layers work together. Policy and standards state the rules: what AI may and may not be used for, data-handling requirements, the risk-tiering scheme, mandatory controls per tier, and approval processes. Technical guardrails enforce what they can automatically — permission limits, approval gates, input/output validation, egress controls, logging (Vols. 04, 10) — so compliance doesn't depend solely on people remembering. The strongest governance pushes as much policy as possible into the platform, where it's enforced by default rather than by diligence.

Make the safe path the easy path

If governance is friction bolted on at the end, teams route around it. If the central platform makes the compliant way the default and easiest way — pre-approved models, built-in guardrails, automatic logging — governance scales with adoption instead of fighting it.

08Part C · Making it real

Governance in financial services

Financial institutions don't start from zero — they have decades of governance: model risk management, three lines of defence, operational-risk frameworks. The task is not to invent agentic AI governance but to extend what exists to a new kind of system.

The fit is strong. An agent influencing a regulated decision is a model under existing model-risk regimes (Vol. 12 §4), so validation, monitoring, and documentation already apply. The three-lines structure already separates building from independent risk oversight. What's new is the autonomy and action — an agent does things, not just scores them — which is exactly why the autonomy tiering (§2), oversight models (§3), and security controls (Vol. 10) are the additions banks must layer onto their established frameworks. Extend, don't replace.
