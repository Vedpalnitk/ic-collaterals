Chapter I · Foundations

Introduction & purpose.

What trade surveillance is, why it exists, and how it differs from the AML transaction monitoring sitting next to it on the compliance floor.

§ 01 · Foundations

The continuous review of orders, trades and talk.

A definition of trade surveillance, the question it asks, and the deliverable it produces.

Trade surveillance (TS) is the continuous, automated review of orders, trades, quotes, cancellations, communications, and market data to detect activity that may constitute market abuse — broadly, insider dealing (trading on material non-public information) and market manipulation (trying to move or distort market prices, volumes, or impressions for advantage). Every regulated broker-dealer, investment bank, asset manager, exchange, MTF, and many trading venues operate a TS function.

The TS control does not try to prove a crime. It tries to identify activity that warrants escalation to compliance and, if appropriate, a regulatory filing — a STOR (Suspicious Transaction and Order Report) in the EU/UK, an analogous SAR/Form 1661/equivalent in other jurisdictions. The output of TS is an alert, never a conclusion.

Mental model

Trade surveillance asks two different questions in parallel: "Did this person trade on information they shouldn't have had?" (insider dealing) and "Did this trading pattern try to distort the market?" (manipulation). Different scenarios, different evidence, different investigation paths — but one platform and one team.

TS vs AML transaction monitoring

Both are post-event compliance controls running over transaction data, but they target different harms:

Chapter II · Foundations

Regulatory landscape.

The principal regimes mandating trade surveillance, the obligations they impose, and the reporting outputs they expect.

§ 02 · Why this exists

The rulebook every surveillance program is built against.

Three blocs, three reporting outputs, one operating expectation: every regulator-catalogued typology should have a corresponding scenario.

European Union & United Kingdom

  • Market Abuse Regulation (MAR, Regulation 596/2014) — the cornerstone EU framework defining insider dealing, unlawful disclosure, and market manipulation, and imposing the obligation under Article 16 to detect and report suspicious orders and transactions via STOR. Onshored into UK law post-Brexit as UK MAR.
  • MiFID II / MiFIR — transparency, reporting, record-keeping, and best-execution rules. Article 16(7) imposes the requirement to record telephone and electronic communications relating to transactions.
  • ESMA Guidelines on MAR — operational guidance including indicators of market manipulation set out in MAR Annex I and ESMA Q&As.

United States

  • Securities Exchange Act of 1934, especially Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5 — the foundational antifraud and anti-manipulation provisions.
  • Dodd-Frank Act §747 — explicit prohibition of spoofing and disruptive trading practices in commodities and futures markets, enforced by the CFTC.
  • FINRA rules — Rule 3110 (Supervision), Rule 4511 (Books and Records), and a deep body of guidance on supervisory systems and trade surveillance obligations.
  • SEC regulations — Regulation NMS, Reg SHO (short selling), Rule 15c3-5 (market access), and the broad antifraud authority.
  • CAT (Consolidated Audit Trail) — the SEC's comprehensive audit trail capturing every order event across US equities and options markets; reshapes how regulators detect manipulation and how firms construct their own surveillance evidence.

Asia-Pacific

  • Singapore — Securities and Futures Act (SFA); MAS guidelines on market conduct.
  • Hong Kong — Securities and Futures Ordinance (SFO) Parts XIII and XIV; SFC Market Misconduct Tribunal.
  • Australia — ASIC Market Integrity Rules; Corporations Act provisions on insider trading and market manipulation.
  • Japan — Financial Instruments and Exchange Act (FIEA); SESC enforcement.
  • India — SEBI PFUTP (Prohibition of Fraudulent and Unfair Trade Practices) Regulations; insider-trading regulations under SEBI Act.

The reporting obligations TS feeds

  • STOR (EU/UK)

    Suspicious Transaction and Order Report filed under MAR Article 16 with the relevant national competent authority (FCA, BaFin, AMF, etc.) when a firm has reasonable suspicion of insider dealing or market manipulation. Confidential; the firm must not tip off the subject.

  • SAR (US)

    Broker-dealers file SARs with FinCEN on certain suspicious activity per BSA. Many market-abuse escalations also flow through internal compliance to the SEC, FINRA, or CFTC as referrals.

  • Internal & exchange-level

    Exchanges (e.g. NYSE, LSE, SGX) also run their own surveillance and refer to regulators. Internal escalations often trigger disciplinary action, trader suspension, and Personal Account Dealing investigations.

Why this matters for surveillance design

Regulators publish indicative lists of manipulation behaviors (MAR Annex I; ESMA Q&As; the CFTC Anti-Spoofing Guidance) and expect firms to implement detection for each. A pattern catalogued by ESMA with no corresponding scenario in your TS platform is a coverage gap — and that gap is what enforcement actions over the last decade have repeatedly punished, from the LIBOR and FX fixing cases through to multiple individual spoofing prosecutions in US futures markets.

Chapter III · Taxonomy & concepts

Taxonomy of market abuse.

Two broad families with sub-types. Knowing the taxonomy makes every scenario, every regulatory paper, and every vendor pitch instantly more legible.

§ 03 · What TS is detecting

Two families, one boundary.

Insider dealing exploits the market through information asymmetry; manipulation distorts the market for everyone. Most regimes also reach the attempts and the talk around the trade.

  • Family 01

    Insider dealing

    Trading, or recommending another to trade, while in possession of material non-public information (MNPI) — and the related offences of unlawful disclosure (tipping) and information barrier breach (Chinese wall failure). The act is the misuse of the information asymmetry; the trade itself need not be otherwise abnormal.

  • Family 02

    Market manipulation

    Trading or order activity that gives or is likely to give false or misleading signals as to supply, demand, or price of a financial instrument — or that secures the price at an artificial level. Examples: spoofing, layering, wash trades, marking the close, benchmark manipulation, pump-and-dump.

  • Boundary

    Attempted & inchoate

    Most regimes also capture attempts, recklessness, and aiding. An unexecuted spoof order that was placed with no intent to trade is still manipulation under MAR. A trader who forwards MNPI without trading themselves is still in breach. Surveillance must therefore detect intent patterns, not just executed trades.

A useful frame

Manipulation distorts the market for everyone; insider dealing exploits the market through information asymmetry. Different scenarios target each. A single trader can commit both simultaneously (e.g. spoof to trigger a stop run while also holding MNPI on an upcoming announcement) — your surveillance must be able to see and link both signals.

Chapter IV · Taxonomy & concepts

The surveillance lifecycle.

Seven stages every TS platform implements, from raw market data to escalation or close-out.

§ 04 · How TS actually works

From the tape to the STOR.

The processing pipeline, plus the four engineering problems that decide whether the pipeline produces usable alerts or unprovable noise.

  1. 01
    Data capture
    Orders, executions, cancels, quotes, market data, e-comms, voice.
  2. 02
    Normalization
    Time-sync to microseconds; map venue-specific schemas to common model.
  3. 03
    Enrichment
    Reference data, watch/restricted lists, trader/desk hierarchies, news.
  4. 04
    Detection
    Run scenarios, behavioral models, statistical and ML detectors.
  5. 05
    Alerting
    Score, dedupe, group by trader/desk/instrument; route to investigators.
  6. 06
    Investigation
    Replay order book, link e-comms, build the timeline, evidence pack.
  7. 07
    STOR / close
    File STOR, escalate, discipline trader, or close with rationale.

The non-obvious hard parts

  • Time synchronization. MiFID II requires venue-side timestamps accurate to 100µs for latency-sensitive trading. Your TS platform must reconcile multiple sources without drift, or replay order-book reconstructions become unusable as evidence.
  • Order-book reconstruction. To prove spoofing you need to replay the book exactly as the venue saw it, in order, including every cancel. Most TS programs build or licence a tick-level replay engine for this.
  • Linking trading to communications. A wash trade is rarely interesting alone; a wash trade with a Bloomberg chat thirty seconds before that says "now" is a case. Joining the trading record to the e-comms / voice record is the single biggest analytical leap.
  • False positives. Many real trading behaviors look like manipulation (cancelled hedge orders, fast quoting on news, large block executions). Calibrating scenarios to the firm's actual trading is essential and ongoing.

Chapter V · Taxonomy & concepts

Core concepts & vocabulary.

The concepts that recur across every regulation, every scenario, and every vendor demo.

§ 05 · Vocabulary you need

Three vocabularies stacked on top of one another.

Market microstructure on the bottom, information & control in the middle, detection on top. Almost every TS conversation reaches into all three.

Market microstructure

Order book
The venue's live list of resting buy and sell orders at each price level. Most manipulation scenarios are defined in terms of what activity does to the order book, not just what was executed.
Bid / Ask / Spread
The best buy price (bid), the best sell price (ask), and the difference. Manipulation often targets the spread or the best bid/ask specifically because that's what reference prices and trading algos key off.
NBBO (US) / EBBO (EU)
National (European) Best Bid and Offer — the best available price across all lit venues for a given instrument. Manipulating the NBBO is far more damaging than manipulating a single venue.
Quote-to-trade ratio
Number of orders/quotes a trader places per trade executed. Extremely high ratios are a screening signal for spoofing, layering, and quote stuffing.
Latency & co-location
Time delay between event and observation. Co-located HFTs operate in microseconds; investigators must be able to slice activity at the same resolution.

Information & control concepts

MNPI (Material Non-Public Information)
Information that (a) a reasonable investor would consider important and (b) has not been disseminated publicly. The "material" and "non-public" threshold definitions are the heart of insider-dealing law.
Information barrier ("Chinese wall")
The organizational, physical, and technical separation between business areas that hold MNPI (M&A advisory, ECM, DCM, lending research) and those that trade (sales & trading, prop). A breach is misconduct in itself, regardless of whether trading occurred.
Watch list / Restricted list
The watch list is confidential and lists names/instruments where the firm has MNPI; used to monitor trading. The restricted list is generally distributed and prohibits certain trading activity. Both feed the surveillance system.
Control room
The compliance function that maintains the watch and restricted lists and clears employee trades and research dissemination. The control room is the lifeblood of insider-dealing surveillance.
Wall-crossing
The deliberate, recorded act of bringing someone "over the wall" into MNPI for a specific transaction (usually pre-deal soundings). Wall-crossing logs are critical evidence in any insider-dealing investigation.
Personal Account Dealing (PAD)
Trading activity by employees in their personal capacity. Tightly governed: pre-clearance, holding periods, prohibited lists. PAD violations are a top finding in employee-conduct surveillance.

Detection-side concepts

Scenario
An implemented detection pattern targeting one or more manipulation/insider typologies. Vendor platforms ship 30–100 out-of-the-box scenarios; firms tune them to their data.
Alert / Case
The output of a fired scenario. Becomes a case in the investigations workflow once acknowledged.
False positive (FP) / True positive (TP)
The dominant operational metric. Most trade-surveillance alerts (typically 90–98%) are closed as false positives — legitimate trading that looks like a typology. The goal is to lower FP rate without missing TPs.
STOR (Suspicious Transaction and Order Report)
The MAR Article 16 filing. Confidential, time-bound, addressed to the national competent authority.
Trade reconstruction
The reproduction of the full lifecycle of an order or trade: every order event, every venue route, every linked communication, every market data tick around it. Required by FINRA and increasingly expected in MAR-jurisdiction investigations.
Lexicon
A curated list of trigger phrases used in e-comms / voice surveillance ("front-run", "pump it", "guarantee", "off the record"). Most lexicons today are starting points for ML-based context classifiers rather than the sole detection mechanism.

Chapter VI · Detection

Detection approaches.

Six families of techniques, ordered roughly by adoption maturity. Real platforms blend all of them.

§ 06 · How detection is built

From rules through machine learning to cross-modal context.

No single technique covers the field. A defensible program layers them — and audits each layer independently.

1 · Rules & threshold scenarios

Deterministic conditions evaluated over order, trade, and quote events: cancel_rate > 95% AND order_size > 5× avg AND duration < 500ms. Easy to explain, auditable, regulator-recognized. Weak against creative manipulators and prone to noise.

2 · Statistical & peer-group analysis

Compares trader behaviour to peers in the same desk, region, or strategy bucket — flag the trader whose cancel ratio or pre-news P&L is several standard deviations out of line. Heart of behavioral PAD and P&L review surveillance.

3 · Sequence & pattern matching

Manipulation is often a sequence: order placed → market moves → opposing order filled → first order cancelled. Engines like complex event processing (CEP) and stateful stream processing detect these sequences in real-time or end-of-day.

4 · Behavioral profiling

Build a baseline of normal behavior per trader, desk, or algorithm; alert on material deviation. Useful for catching newly developed strategies that may shade into manipulation, and for change-of-conduct detection after personnel moves.

5 · Machine learning

Supervised models trained on historical TP/FP labels are increasingly used to (a) score alerts for investigator prioritization, (b) suppress likely-FPs, and (c) cluster similar cases. NLP and transformer models dominate communications surveillance. Explainability is mandatory under model risk management.

6 · Communications & cross-modal analytics

Joining trade events to e-comms (chat, email), voice transcripts, and even calendar/wall-crossing data is now the leading frontier. The signal is rarely in the trade alone — context lives in the conversation that preceded or followed.

How these layer

A typical tier-1 stack runs: (a) statistical/rule scenarios for regulatory coverage, (b) sequence detectors for manipulation typologies, (c) behavioral models for change/deviation, (d) ML triage on top to prioritize, and (e) an e-comms / voice surveillance layer joined to trading events for context. Each layer is independently auditable.

Chapter VII · Detection

Market manipulation scenarios.

The canonical manipulation typologies every TS platform must cover. Each card shows the typology, the detection pattern that targets it, and the primary risk.

§ 07 · The work itself — part 1

Sixteen typologies, four families.

Order-book manipulation, wash and pre-arranged trading, price and close-time manipulation, and benchmark/supply/concealment patterns — each with its own detection fingerprint.

Order-book manipulation

  • Spoofing

    P1
    Non-bona-fide orders
    Typology
    Placing one or more large orders on one side of the book with no intent to execute, to create false impression of supply/demand and induce others to trade — then cancelling the non-bona-fide orders after the genuine smaller order on the other side gets filled at a favourable price.
    Detection pattern
    Large resting order on one side + opposing small fill + rapid cancellation of the large order; very high cancel-to-execute ratio; pattern repeating across instruments or sessions.
    Primary risk
    Explicitly prohibited under Dodd-Frank §747, MAR Article 12, and CFTC anti-spoofing rules; multiple criminal convictions in US futures.

  • Layering

    P1
    Multi-level non-genuine orders
    Typology
    Multiple non-genuine orders placed at successive price levels on one side of the book to amplify a misleading impression of demand or supply, then cancelled after the opposing genuine order fills.
    Detection pattern
    Stacked orders at adjacent price levels with similar timestamps; rapid cancellation post-fill on opposite side; persistent imbalance signature on the trader's activity.
    Primary risk
    Same regulatory framework as spoofing; a defining typology in equities HFT manipulation.

  • Quote stuffing

    P2
    Order-flow flooding
    Typology
    High-volume submission and immediate cancellation of orders to slow down or confuse competing market participants and their algorithms, typically near the NBBO.
    Detection pattern
    Extreme message rate from a single participant; near-zero execution ratio; correlation with latency spikes at the venue.
    Primary risk
    Market disruption; abuse of co-location advantage; subject to CFTC and venue enforcement.

  • Momentum ignition

    P2
    Triggering a price move to exit
    Typology
    Series of orders designed to start or accelerate a short-term price move, drawing in other market participants, so the originator can exit a pre-positioned trade at favourable prices.
    Detection pattern
    Aggressive sweep of multiple price levels followed by counter-trade within seconds; correlated with a pre-existing position; pattern recurring on the same instrument.
    Primary risk
    False supply/demand signal under MAR Annex I; HFT-typical typology.

Wash, mirror & pre-arranged trading

  • Wash trades

    P1
    No change in beneficial ownership
    Typology
    Trades where the buyer and seller are the same person, the same legal entity, or beneficially connected — producing no real change of economic ownership but creating apparent trading volume or a misleading price print.
    Detection pattern
    Buyer and seller resolve to the same UBO/account family; matched price and quantity; opposing orders within tight time windows from related identifiers (account, IP, device).
    Primary risk
    Explicit MAR Annex I typology; extremely common as a precursor signal in crypto and small-cap markets.

  • Painting the tape

    P2
    Collusive volume creation
    Typology
    A series of trades between two or more colluding parties intended to give the false impression of active trading or rising/falling interest in a security.
    Detection pattern
    Repeated executions between the same counterparty pair over a short window, with no apparent economic purpose; volume disproportionate to typical interest in the instrument.
    Primary risk
    Misleading volume signal; classic precursor to a pump-and-dump.

  • Pre-arranged trading

    P3
    Off-market deal printed on-market
    Typology
    Parties agree the terms of a trade privately and then enter matching orders to "execute" the deal on the venue — producing a market print without genuine price discovery.
    Detection pattern
    Matched orders placed within milliseconds at identical price and size by unrelated counterparties; recurring pairing; off-VWAP execution timestamps.
    Primary risk
    Distorts price discovery; circumvents block-trading rules; often pairs with mismarking risk.

  • Cross-trade abuse

    P2
    Improper internal crosses
    Typology
    A broker crosses two clients' orders (or a client and its own house book) at off-market prices, benefiting one party at the expense of another or shifting losses between accounts.
    Detection pattern
    Cross executed outside NBBO at the time of trade; deviation from venue-published reference price; recurring pairings between specific account combinations.
    Primary risk
    Client harm; fiduciary breach; FINRA Rule 5320 (front-running) and best-execution violations.

Price & close-time manipulation

  • Marking the close

    P1
    Distorting the closing print
    Typology
    Trading at or near the end of the session designed to move the closing price of a financial instrument — often used to value portfolios, settle derivatives, or influence index calculations.
    Detection pattern
    Aggressive orders in the final minutes/seconds of trading vs typical activity; price impact disproportionate to volume; trader holds a related position whose mark depends on the close.
    Primary risk
    Index and derivative valuation distortion; explicit MAR Annex I typology; high-impact for benchmark contracts.

  • Marking the open

    P2
    Distorting the opening print
    Typology
    Mirror of marking the close — trades or pre-open orders structured to move the opening auction print to a favourable level, often before a related instrument's settlement.
    Detection pattern
    Outsized pre-open orders relative to trader's history; orders cancelled or reduced immediately after open; related position with sensitivity to the open price.
    Primary risk
    Auction-price distortion; gaming of cross-venue arbitrage and ETF NAVs.

  • Ramping / bear raiding

    P2
    Sustained directional pressure
    Typology
    Deliberate, sustained buying (ramping) or selling (bear raiding) of an instrument to push its price up or down without a fundamental basis — often combined with coordinated communications.
    Detection pattern
    Outsized one-directional flow over an extended window; price impact persistent rather than reverting; correlation with e-comms or social-media chatter.
    Primary risk
    False price signal; market-integrity damage; particular concern in small-cap and emerging-markets equities.

  • Pump and dump

    P1
    Hype-and-exit scheme
    Typology
    Promoters acquire a position, then drive up price and interest through misleading communications (social media, chat rooms, Telegram, paid promotion) — exiting positions into the resulting retail rally.
    Detection pattern
    Concentrated positioning by linked accounts; correlated social-media volume spike; rapid post-rally exit; venue-side small-cap or crypto context.
    Primary risk
    Retail investor harm; the dominant manipulation typology in crypto and penny-stock markets; rising in social-driven equities post-2021.

Benchmark, supply & concealment

  • Benchmark manipulation

    P1
    LIBOR, FX fix, gold, oil
    Typology
    Submissions or trading near the fixing window deliberately calibrated to move a benchmark (LIBOR, EURIBOR, the WM/Reuters 4pm FX fix, gold fix, oil price assessments) for the benefit of related positions.
    Detection pattern
    Trading concentration in the fix window; submission patterns inconsistent with the firm's actual cost of funding; e-comms references to "the fix" or coordinating with rival firms.
    Primary risk
    Resulted in multi-billion-dollar global fines (LIBOR 2012–2016, FX 2014–2017) and individual criminal convictions; reshaped surveillance industry post-2014.

  • Cornering / squeezing

    P2
    Control of deliverable supply
    Typology
    Acquiring a dominant position in the deliverable supply of an instrument (commodity, bond issue, stock-loan inventory) to control settlement price and force counterparties to settle at distorted levels.
    Detection pattern
    Concentrated long position vs available float; rising borrow costs in the instrument; venue concentration metrics breached.
    Primary risk
    Settlement distortion; classic commodity-market typology (the historical Hunt silver corner being the canonical example).

  • Front-running

    P1
    Trading ahead of a client order
    Typology
    A broker, trader, or affiliated account trades for their own benefit ahead of a known pending client order (or block, research recommendation, or index reconstitution) that will move price.
    Detection pattern
    Personal-account or house-book trade in the same instrument within minutes of a client order; consistent profitable pattern; matching trade direction.
    Primary risk
    FINRA Rule 5270 and MAR violations; client fiduciary breach; pre-research-publication trading is a major sub-category.

  • Mismarking

    P2
    Hidden P&L manipulation
    Typology
    Trader records inventory at unrepresentative prices to overstate P&L, hide losses, or smooth earnings — typically in illiquid OTC products where independent price observation is hard.
    Detection pattern
    EOD marks materially deviating from independent price verification (IPV); persistent positive/negative bias; large mark adjustments by specific traders.
    Primary risk
    Misstated financial results; precursor to large hidden losses (the 2008 Soc Gen Kerviel and 2012 JPMorgan London Whale cases involved mismarking).

  • Improper allocation

    P2
    Cherry-picking trades
    Typology
    A trader allocates winning trades to favoured accounts (personal, related, fee-paying) and losing trades to others, typically using a "house" or "error" account as the staging point.
    Detection pattern
    Statistically improbable P&L outperformance by certain accounts; time gap between execution and allocation; recurring use of error account as intermediate.
    Primary risk
    SEC and FINRA enforcement; client trust failure; common in PM & alternative-asset settings.

Chapter VIII · Detection

Insider dealing scenarios.

The scenarios targeting trading on, and disclosure of, material non-public information. Smaller scenario set than manipulation, but each one is regulator-sensitive.

§ 08 · The work itself — part 2

Where the trade is normal but the information was not.

Detection lives less in the order book and more in the join between trading, watch-list status, communications and timing.

  • Trading ahead of material news

    P1
    MNPI-driven positioning
    Typology
    A trader (employee, client, or related account) takes a position in an instrument shortly before a price-sensitive announcement — earnings surprise, M&A, regulatory action, clinical trial result — that moves the price in the expected direction.
    Detection pattern
    Position established within a defined pre-announcement window; abnormal trade size vs trader history; profitable post-announcement; correlation with watch-list or restricted-list status of the issuer.
    Primary risk
    Core insider-dealing offence under MAR Article 8, Section 10(b)/Rule 10b-5, and equivalents.

  • Information barrier breach

    P1
    Chinese-wall failure
    Typology
    Sales & trading, prop, or research activity in an instrument the firm has MNPI on through its private side (advisory, lending, ECM/DCM, restructuring).
    Detection pattern
    Trading on the public side in instruments currently on the firm watch list maintained by the control room; trade timing aligned with private-side milestones.
    Primary risk
    Direct regulatory violation; firm-wide governance failure; cited in major SEC and FCA enforcement actions.

  • Unlawful disclosure ("tipping")

    P1
    Passing MNPI to a tippee
    Typology
    An employee passes MNPI to another person (colleague, friend, family member, external party) who then trades — or who simply receives the information regardless of trading.
    Detection pattern
    E-comms / voice mentioning a restricted-list instrument outside authorized channels; calls/messages between insider and external account immediately preceding their trade; lexicon hits combined with timing.
    Primary risk
    Criminal offence in most jurisdictions even without trading by the discloser; a sub-typology heavily reliant on communications surveillance.

  • Pre-research-publication trading

    P2
    Front-running internal research
    Typology
    Sales, trading, or PAD activity ahead of an internal analyst's upgrade/downgrade or initiation report that materially moves price upon dissemination.
    Detection pattern
    Trades within hours/days before research publication, in the direction of the recommendation; profitability vs base rate; trader's access to draft research.
    Primary risk
    FINRA Rule 5270 violation; reputation risk; client fairness failure.

  • Personal account dealing breach

    P2
    Employee PAD violations
    Typology
    Employee trades in their personal account without pre-clearance, during a restricted period, in a restricted instrument, or below mandatory holding periods. Often combined with insider risk if the instrument is on the firm watch list.
    Detection pattern
    PAD broker feed reconciled to employee trade pre-clearance records; restricted-list match; instrument hold period violation.
    Primary risk
    Conduct breach; precursor to more serious insider-dealing exposure.

  • Wall-crossing failure

    P3
    MNPI handling control breakdown
    Typology
    An employee or external party is brought across the wall to receive MNPI (e.g. for a pre-deal sounding) but the wall-crossing is not logged, the recipient is not bound, or the recipient subsequently trades.
    Detection pattern
    Wall-crossing register reconciled against e-comms and call records; trades by named individuals while their wall-crossing was active.
    Primary risk
    MAR Article 11 obligations; firm-side governance failure; precursor to leakage.

Chapter IX · Worked cases & data

Worked scenario walkthroughs.

Three concrete examples showing how typology maps to detection logic, what the alert looks like, and how an investigator should think about it.

§ 09 · From signal to alert

Three patterns, three lessons.

A spoofing case in futures; a wash-trade case in crypto; an insider-dealing case ahead of M&A. The first two are caught firm-internally; the third tells you why some cases never are.

Walkthrough 01 · Spoofing in E-mini futures

Trader B repeatedly cancels a large sell order after a small buy fills.

Setup. Trader B operates a proprietary book trading E-mini S&P 500 futures. Average order size historically 5–10 contracts; cancel rate ~30%.

Activity over a 4-minute window:

  1. 09:14:02 — Sell order for 250 contracts placed two ticks above the market
  2. 09:14:05 — Buy order for 8 contracts placed at the market, filled immediately
  3. 09:14:06 — The 250-contract sell order cancelled
  4. Pattern repeats 27 times over 8 trading sessions; each time the small buy fills profitably

Detection logic that fires. Spoofing scenario: alerts when (a) a single order > 5× trader's typical size is placed and cancelled within 30 seconds, (b) an opposing smaller order is filled in the window between placement and cancellation, and (c) the large order's execution ratio is below 5%, with a minimum count of 5 such patterns over 5 trading days.

Why it works. No individual cancel is suspicious — cancellation is normal market behaviour. The fingerprint is the sequence: large resting order, opposing small fill, immediate cancellation, and recurrence. Investigators reconstruct the order book at each event, build the price impact chart, and pull the trader's e-comms for the period — looking for explicit acknowledgement of the strategy.
Walkthrough 02 · Wash trades in an illiquid OTC token

Two related accounts trade with each other 14 times in one afternoon.

Setup. An institutional trading platform offers a small-cap crypto token. Two accounts — A and B — both opened in the same week, same legal entity declared, similar IP fingerprints during onboarding.

Activity over a 6-hour window:

  1. 14 trades between accounts A and B, each ~$80,000 in notional
  2. Price drifts 8% upward as published market data over the same window
  3. External retail volume in the token spikes 4× as the price trend continues
  4. A and B exit their joint position into the retail flow over the following day

Detection logic that fires. Wash-trade scenario: alerts when (a) buyer and seller share KYC attributes (legal entity, address, beneficial owner, device fingerprint, or persistent IP) and (b) the executed price + quantity match within tight tolerance and (c) the pattern repeats > 3 times within a defined window.

Why it works. The wash signal lives at the relationship between accounts, not in either account alone. Without KYC-driven entity resolution your scenario will miss this — and in crypto markets the entity-resolution layer is often the limiting factor on detection quality. The follow-up case likely also touches AML (the apparent price drift may have been part of a pump-and-dump).
Walkthrough 03 · Insider dealing ahead of M&A announcement

A junior analyst's friend buys deep out-of-the-money calls on a target three days before announcement.

Setup. The firm is advising Acquirer Inc on the acquisition of Target Co. The mandate is added to the watch list at 09:00 on Monday; public announcement is scheduled for Thursday 18:00.

Signals available:

  1. Monday 17:30 — A junior analyst on the deal team has 4 short Bloomberg chats with a personal contact (no firm relationship)
  2. Tuesday 10:15 — That contact opens a fresh brokerage account at another broker (visible only later via SAR data-sharing)
  3. Tuesday 14:20 — The contact buys 800 of the cheapest Target Co weekly calls, struck 12% out-of-the-money
  4. Thursday 18:00 — Announcement; Target Co shares jump 26%
  5. Thursday 18:05 — The calls expire deep in-the-money; contact realizes ~ $310k profit

Detection logic that fires. Two scenarios fire in combination: (a) communications-link scenario alerts on personal-contact chats during a watch-list deal window from a deal-team member; (b) insider-pattern scenario at the regulator side (or post-incident at the firm) alerts on out-of-the-money option positioning ahead of a price-moving event in a watch-list instrument.

Why it works (and where it fails). The firm sees the e-comms signal but not the external trade — that side appears only when the regulator or another broker's surveillance flags it. This is why regulator-side surveillance (CAT, MAR public-side data) plays a critical role insider-dealing investigations cannot rely on firm-internal surveillance alone, and why robust wall-crossing logs, deal-team lists, and PAD discipline are the firm's first lines of defence.

Chapter X · Worked cases & data

Communications surveillance.

The body of practice covering email, chat (Bloomberg, Symphony, MS Teams), voice, video, and SMS surveillance — typically run by a sister team and platform but increasingly converging with trade surveillance.

§ 10 · The other half of surveillance

The trade record shows what; the chat record shows why.

Almost every major case of the last fifteen years turned on the talk around the trade. Modern programs are fusing the two records.

The trade record showed what happened; the communications record showed intent. Modern surveillance increasingly seeks to fuse the two. A mental model

Why it matters

Almost every major market-abuse enforcement action of the last 15 years — LIBOR, FX fixing, ISDAFIX, US Treasury auction collusion, multiple individual spoofing cases — pivoted on evidence drawn from chat rooms and recorded phone lines. The trade record showed what happened; the communications record showed intent. Modern surveillance increasingly seeks to fuse the two.

The data sources

  • Email — corporate email (Exchange/Outlook, Google Workspace) and any approved external channels. The original e-comms data type and still the highest-volume.
  • Chat — Bloomberg chat and IB, Symphony, Refinitiv Eikon Messenger, Microsoft Teams, Slack, WhatsApp/Signal where approved or banned (the unapproved-channels enforcement wave 2022–2024 produced ~$3bn+ in US fines).
  • Voice — turret lines, mobile, conference bridges. Transcribed via speech-to-text and then searchable via NLP.
  • Video — increasingly important post-Covid; Zoom, Teams, Webex recordings.
  • SMS / messaging — particularly relevant after the off-channel comms enforcement wave.

Detection approaches

  • Lexicon-based

    Search for curated trigger phrases — "front-run", "don't tell", "between us", "off the record", "do me a favour", "the cartel", "I've got the order". Still the regulatory baseline but FP-heavy in isolation. Lexicons evolve constantly based on emerging cases.

  • NLP / ML classifiers

    Trained models classify communications by intent and context — sentiment, role identification, topic clustering, anomaly detection. Major vendors (Behavox, Shield, Symphony AI, Theta Lake) have shifted heavily here; transformer-based classifiers now lead lexicon-only approaches by significant margins.

  • Entity & network analytics

    Who is talking to whom, how often, with whom externally — surfacing new communication patterns and unusual external contacts. Critical for tipping and collusion detection.

  • Trade-comms correlation

    Tie an alerted trade to communications in a tight window — calls, chats, emails involving the trader, the desk, and external parties. The defining frontier of unified surveillance.

Off-channel communications

Between 2022 and 2024 the SEC, CFTC, and other regulators imposed more than USD 3bn in fines on global banks for staff using WhatsApp, iMessage, and personal text channels to discuss business — bypassing the firm's record-keeping. Off-channel comms is now a top supervisory focus, and TS programs are expected to surface evidence of off-channel use (mentions in monitored channels of "let's take this to WhatsApp", device-level signals where MDM permits).

Chapter XI · Worked cases & data

Cross-product & cross-venue surveillance.

Modern manipulation routinely crosses instruments, venues, and asset classes. So must the surveillance.

§ 11 · Next-level surveillance

Four ways the picture is broken across boundaries.

Cross-product, cross-venue, cross-asset-class, and cross-firm. Each one is invisible to a surveillance program scoped to a single instrument or venue.

Cross-product manipulation

A trader influences the price of one instrument by trading another correlated instrument. Classic examples: moving the underlying equity price into option-expiry to make in-the-money options pay out; pushing the cash bond around a CDS auction date; influencing the futures price near settlement to mark a related swap. Detection requires correlated-instrument awareness — the platform must know that this option is written on this underlying and that this futures contract settles to this index.

Cross-venue manipulation

Placing the spoof on Venue A to move the NBBO, then taking the favourable execution on Venue B. Each venue sees only half of the picture. Detection requires consolidated tape data — in the US, the Consolidated Audit Trail (CAT) gives regulators this view; firms must build their own consolidated view from their order routing record and venue feeds.

Cross-asset-class

Increasingly common: equity activity used to move related ETF flows; crypto-spot activity used to move crypto-futures positions; spot-FX activity in support of FX option positions. Surveillance platforms must cover the full set of instruments traded by a single trader or desk to catch these.

Cross-firm collusion

The hardest pattern of all — multiple firms colluding (the "cartel" chat rooms in the FX-fixing cases, ISDAFIX, US Treasury auctions). No single firm sees the full picture; detection has historically depended on whistleblowers, internal e-comms hits, or regulator-side cross-firm data. Industry consortia (e.g. SteelEye Connect, vendor benchmarking) are exploring federated approaches but adoption is early.

Chapter XII · Operations

Mitigation & tuning.

The repertoire used to make a TS program defensible and operationally manageable — same lever set as TM, but the calibration mechanics differ.

§ 12 · Making TS actually work

The seven levers a defensible program pulls.

Threshold tuning, segmentation, typology coverage, replay discipline, governed suppression, backtesting, and independent model validation. None is optional.

Threshold & parameter tuning

Scenario thresholds (cancel ratio, time window, size multiplier, price-impact threshold) must reflect the firm's actual trading. Tuning uses above-the-line / below-the-line sampling — pull alerts just above and just below threshold, manually review, and adjust to maximize TP density without losing recall.

Trader segmentation

A market maker is supposed to quote both sides aggressively and cancel often; a low-touch execution algo is supposed to trade quietly. Applying the same spoofing threshold to both produces either over-alerting on market makers or under-detection on the algo. Segment by role, strategy, instrument class.

Scenario library to typology map

Maintain a live map of MAR Annex I / ESMA Q&A typologies / CFTC anti-spoofing examples to your implemented scenarios. Gaps become risk findings; closing them becomes the workplan.

Replay & reconstruction discipline

Every alert investigation should be able to produce a full order-book reconstruction at the relevant time stamps, plus a linked communications view. Firms that cannot do this fail FINRA / FCA examinations.

Suppression with governance

Known benign patterns can be suppressed (e.g. cancel-heavy market-maker quoting that meets defined criteria) — but every suppression must be approved, logged, reviewed periodically, and unwound when conditions change. Regulators ask exactly that.

Backtesting against past cases

When a public enforcement action emerges (a spoofing conviction, a new manipulation typology), run the new detection logic over the firm's own historical data. Many programs catch dormant cases this way; it is also regulator-expected diligence.

Independent model validation

Under SR 11-7 (US) and equivalent model-risk frameworks, every scenario and ML model must be independently validated for design, data quality, threshold appropriateness, and ongoing performance — usually by a Model Risk team outside the development line.

A regulatory trap

Raising thresholds simply to reduce alert volume is one of the most-common findings in FCA and FINRA supervisory reviews. Any threshold movement must be supported by ATL/BTL evidence, documented in the model inventory, approved by model governance, and re-tested afterwards. "We were drowning" is not a defence.

Chapter XIII · Operations

Investigation & STOR filing.

Most of the cost in a TS program is in this stage. The technology helps but doesn't replace the human judgement.

§ 13 · What happens after an alert

Three tiers of judgement, one piece of paper.

L1 triage, L2 deep-dive, L3 sign-off — culminating in a STOR narrative the regulator can act on without re-doing the firm's work.

Investigator hierarchy

  • L1 — Surveillance analyst

    Triages alerts; validates data; reviews trader and desk context; pulls quick order-book replay and relevant chats. Most alerts close here with a documented rationale.

  • L2 — Senior investigator

    Handles escalations. Deep-dives on order-book reconstruction, cross-product/cross-venue analysis, full communications review, trader interviews if needed. Produces the draft STOR narrative.

  • L3 — Compliance officer / senior management

    Reviews STOR filings, takes firm-disciplinary decisions, manages regulator relationships. In the UK and similar regimes the firm's Money Laundering Reporting Officer may also be the conduit for STOR-style filings.

The investigator's toolkit

  • Order-book replay — tick-by-tick reconstruction of the venue book at the relevant times, with the trader's events highlighted.
  • Communications timeline — every chat, email, call relevant to the trader, the desk, and the instrument, joined to the trade record on a single time axis.
  • Trader profile — historical activity, watch/restricted-list exposures, prior cases, peer comparisons, PAD history.
  • Reference data — issuer, sector, correlated instruments, news, corporate actions in the window.
  • GenAI assistants — emerging across vendors (Behavox Quantos AI, SteelEye, Symphony AI, Shield) to draft narratives, summarize evidence, and surface relevant prior cases.

The STOR itself

A STOR is filed with the national competent authority (FCA, BaFin, AMF, CONSOB, etc.) under MAR Article 16 when the firm has reasonable suspicion of insider dealing, market manipulation, or unlawful disclosure. The narrative must explain the activity, the basis of suspicion, the relevant trades and orders, the timing, and the parties — typically in tens of pages with attachments. Tipping off the subject is prohibited. Failure to file is itself a serious breach.

STOR narrative — key elements

Identity of suspected person/entity; description of the conduct; trades and orders that gave rise to suspicion (with exchange-side timestamps); other relevant activity (e-comms, voice, prior cases); the reasoning behind suspicion; supporting data and documents. The narrative must be specific enough that the regulator can act on it without re-doing the firm's analysis.

Chapter XV · Horizon

Emerging trends.

The dominant directions of change in trade surveillance as of 2024–2026.

§ 15 · Where TS is heading

Seven currents reshaping the function.

Holistic data, explainable ML, GenAI investigation, crypto maturity, off-channel discipline, real-time controls, and the slow convergence with conduct surveillance.

Holistic surveillance (trade + comms + voice on one data layer)

The defining direction. Tier-1 banks are consolidating onto a single data lake holding trades, orders, chats, emails, voice transcripts, market data, and trader hierarchies — then running detection across all of it. SteelEye, Symphony AI, Behavox, and tier-1 build-your-own programs all converge here.

Machine learning with explainability

ML is now mainstream in TS for alert prioritization, FP suppression, and e-comms classification — but only with SR 11-7-style validation and SHAP/LIME-style explainability. Black-box vendor models are no longer acceptable to model risk committees.

Generative AI for investigation

GenAI is being deployed not in detection (explainability constraints) but in investigation: drafting STOR narratives, summarizing trader history, surfacing similar prior cases, generating investigator briefs. Behavox Quantos AI, SteelEye, Symphony AI, Shield, and others all have GenAI copilots shipping or in pilot.

Crypto market surveillance maturing

Spot-crypto trading is now subject to MAR-equivalent expectations under the EU's MiCA regulation (2024). Crypto venues that previously operated with minimal surveillance must build TS programs; banks with digital-asset desks face the same expectations.

Off-channel comms enforcement

The 2022–2024 enforcement wave on personal-device messaging (WhatsApp, iMessage) has forced firms to invest heavily in (a) approved channels with full archiving, (b) device-level controls, and (c) detection of hints in monitored channels of off-channel migration.

Real-time and pre-trade surveillance

Historically T+1 (overnight). Increasingly, scenarios run intraday — and a small but growing share run pre-trade as risk-management controls (rejecting orders that look like spoofing before they reach the venue). Rule 15c3-5 (market access) drives this in the US.

Cross-firm consortium & federated approaches

Inter-firm collusion (the FX-fixing cartels) is invisible to any single firm. Vendor-led benchmarking and regulator-sponsored data-sharing pilots are exploring federated approaches — early, but the direction is clear.

Convergence with conduct surveillance

Increasingly, trade surveillance is one input into a wider conduct surveillance view of each trader and desk — combining market-abuse alerts, PAD violations, gifts/entertainment, regulatory attestations, complaint history, HR signals. The FCA's SM&CR regime and DOJ individual-accountability focus push firms in this direction.

Chapter XVI · Horizon

Common pitfalls & challenges.

Patterns recurring across enforcement actions, audit findings, and consulting engagements.

§ 16 · What goes wrong

Eight failure modes worth memorising.

Almost every supervisory finding lands in one of these eight buckets. Reading them is also the fastest way to set the priorities for a new program.

  • 1 · Data quality & time-sync

    Microsecond mis-synchronization between venue, OMS, and TS clocks breaks order-book reconstruction. Many programs build TS before solving this — and find their alerts are unprovable.

  • 2 · Coverage gaps to ESMA/CFTC catalogues

    A typology exists in the regulator's catalogue; no scenario implements it. Classic finding in supervisory reviews; usually triggers remediation orders.

  • 3 · Drowning in false positives

    Without segmentation and behavioral baselines, market-maker activity floods spoofing alerts. Investigator capacity becomes the binding constraint; real signals get buried.

  • 4 · Trade-comms isolation

    Trade and e-comms surveillance run on separate platforms with no join. Most major cases turn on the link between trade and message; isolation destroys it.

  • 5 · Off-channel comms blind spot

    Activity migrates to personal WhatsApp / iMessage and disappears from surveillance. Multi-billion-dollar enforcement category 2022–2024.

  • 6 · Watch/restricted-list hygiene

    Lists not maintained in real-time; instruments not removed; mappings stale. Insider-dealing surveillance is no better than the lists feeding it.

  • 7 · STOR-filing reluctance

    Firms either over-file (defensive) or under-file (institutional reluctance to escalate). Regulators publish STOR-volume statistics and notice outliers.

  • 8 · Model decay

    Scenario tuned years ago no longer fits current trading mix, venue mix, or typologies. Periodic re-tuning is not optional.
