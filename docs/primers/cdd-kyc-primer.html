Chapter I · Foundations

Introduction & purpose.

What CDD/KYC is, why it exists, and where it sits inside the broader financial-crime control framework.

§ 01 · Foundations

Customer Due Diligence (CDD) — colloquially called Know Your Customer (KYC) — is the discipline by which a regulated financial institution (FI) identifies who its customer is, verifies that identity, understands the nature and purpose of the relationship, assesses the risk that the customer poses for money laundering, terrorist financing, sanctions evasion, fraud, tax evasion or proliferation financing, and then keeps that picture current for as long as the relationship endures. It is the foundational layer of the AML/CFT/sanctions control framework — without reliable customer data, no transaction monitoring scenario, sanctions screening rule, or fraud model can work properly.

CDD is not a one-off onboarding step. FATF Recommendation 10 defines it as four activities performed on a risk-sensitive, ongoing basis: identify the customer; identify and take reasonable measures to verify the beneficial owner; understand the purpose and intended nature of the relationship; and conduct ongoing due diligence on the relationship. KYC, in popular usage, often refers only to the first two; CDD is the broader regulatory term that includes the whole lifecycle.

Mental model

The continuously refreshed customer dossier.

Think of CDD as the FI's continuously refreshed customer dossier. Each entry in the dossier is a hypothesis: this customer is who they say they are, doing what they said they would do, with funds that came from where they said. Every screening hit, transaction alert, news event, or refresh cycle is an opportunity to confirm or revise that hypothesis. When the hypothesis breaks badly enough — or cannot be re-confirmed at all — the relationship is exited.

Why it exists

  • Legal obligation. The US Bank Secrecy Act (1970) and its successor statutes — the USA PATRIOT Act §326 (the Customer Identification Program rule), the FinCEN CDD Rule (2018), and the Corporate Transparency Act (2021, operational 2024) — require every covered FI to establish a written CDD program. Comparable obligations exist under the EU AML Directives, the UK Money Laundering Regulations 2017, MAS Notices 626/824 in Singapore, HKMA AML/CFT guidance in Hong Kong, and RBI's Master Direction on KYC in India.
  • Sanctions enforcement. Without robust identity data, sanctions screening either over-blocks (false positives crippling operations) or under-blocks (sanctioned parties get through). CDD provides the identifying attributes — name, date of birth, nationality, registered address, UBO chain — that screening engines need to actually work.
  • Risk-based AML pillar. A risk-based AML program calibrates monitoring intensity to customer risk. The customer risk rating that drives that calibration comes from CDD. A misrated population leads directly to either under-monitoring of risky relationships (the TD Bank, Wachovia, Danske Bank pattern) or to excessive alert volumes against a low-risk population.
  • Reputational integrity. The bank that onboarded an autocrat's offshore vehicle, the trust company that papered a sanctioned oligarch's yacht, the crypto exchange that registered the North Korean Lazarus Group — these are CDD failures with consequences far beyond the regulatory fine. The Pandora, Panama, Paradise, FinCEN Files, and Suisse Secrets leaks all turn on CDD weaknesses.

Where it sits in the control framework

An AML/CFT/sanctions program rests on three operational pillars and a governance layer. CDD/KYC is the input layer that feeds the other two:

Pillar 1 — CDD / KYC

Identifies and risk-rates the customer; maintains the dossier; runs ongoing due diligence. Outputs feed every other AML control.

Pillar 2 — Sanctions screening

Real-time check of names, addresses, and beneficiaries against sanctions lists at onboarding, in payments, and on periodic re-screen. Requires CDD-grade identity data.

Pillar 3 — Transaction monitoring

Detects suspicious activity in transactions. Calibrates thresholds and scenario logic against the customer's expected behavioral profile — which is CDD output.

If a sanctions list is updated with a new name tomorrow, or a transaction monitoring scenario alerts on a customer today, does the CDD record contain enough trustworthy data to investigate that hit in minutes rather than days? A useful test

If not, CDD is the bottleneck of the entire AML program — irrespective of how sophisticated the screening engine or TM platform is.

Chapter II · Foundations

Regulatory landscape.

The global standard-setter, the major regional regimes, and the regulations you will be examined against.

§ 02 · Regulatory frame

CDD obligations are remarkably consistent across jurisdictions because most national regimes implement the same international standard. Understanding the standard first, then the national overlays, is the most efficient way to internalize the regulatory frame.

The global standard: FATF

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) is the inter-governmental body whose 40 Recommendations form the de facto global AML/CFT standard. Five Recommendations directly govern CDD:

  • Rec. 10 — CDD. Identify and verify the customer; identify and verify the beneficial owner; understand the nature and purpose of the relationship; conduct ongoing due diligence. Applies on a risk-sensitive basis. No anonymous accounts or accounts in fictitious names.
  • Rec. 11 — Record-keeping. Retain CDD records for at least five years after the relationship ends.
  • Rec. 12 — PEPs. Apply enhanced measures to foreign PEPs (and risk-sensitively to domestic PEPs and PEPs of international organizations), including senior management approval, source-of-wealth and source-of-funds inquiries, and enhanced ongoing monitoring.
  • Rec. 17 — Reliance on third parties. Permits limited reliance on regulated third parties for CDD, with conditions.
  • Rec. 22 — DNFBPs. Extends CDD obligations beyond banks to designated non-financial businesses and professions (casinos, real-estate agents, dealers in precious metals/stones, lawyers, accountants, trust and company service providers).
  • Rec. 24–25 — Beneficial ownership transparency. Jurisdictions must ensure adequate, accurate and timely beneficial ownership information is available for legal persons and arrangements.

Regulatory drift

Three structural shifts are reshaping the landscape.

(i) the EU is moving from Directive (transposed differently across Member States) to Regulation (uniform across the bloc) with a central supervisor (AMLA) from 2028; (ii) beneficial ownership data is moving from FI-collected to state-collected (US CTA, UK Register of Overseas Entities, EU Member State registers); (iii) perpetual KYC is supplanting periodic refresh in major banks. Any CDD operating model designed before 2022 should be re-tested against these three shifts.

Chapter III · Operating model

The CDD/KYC lifecycle.

The end-to-end flow from prospect identification through relationship exit, and where each stage tends to fail.

§ 03 · Operating model

Every CDD program — irrespective of size, jurisdiction, or line of business — implements some version of the same lifecycle. The names and tooling differ; the stages do not. Knowing the stages is the fastest way to read any vendor demo, regulatory exam finding, or internal audit report.

01
Pre-onboarding & intake
Channel intake (branch, digital, RM-led, broker-introduced); collect minimum identification data; preliminary risk screen.
02
Identification & verification
Identity proofed against documents, biometrics, electronic data sources. Address verified. For entities: incorporation documents, governance, signatories.
03
UBO & ownership chain
Beneficial owners and controllers identified (≥25% standard) and verified. Ownership chain documented; layered structures traced to natural persons.
04
Screening
Sanctions, PEP, adverse media checks on customer, UBOs, authorized signatories, related parties. True hits investigated and dispositioned.
05
Risk rating & profile
Customer risk rating computed across geography, product, channel, behavioral, and entity-type factors. Expected activity profile documented.
06
Onboarding decision
Approve, decline, or escalate to senior management/Compliance. For high-risk: EDD and senior approval required.
07
Ongoing monitoring
Activity monitored against expected profile (via TM). Continuous re-screening against updated sanctions/PEP/adverse-media lists. Event-driven triggers from TM, news, customer-initiated changes.
08
Refresh / pKYC / exit
Periodic refresh on risk-based cadence (or perpetual KYC for continuous refresh). Re-rate. If risk unacceptable: file SAR if warranted, exit relationship.

A useful observation

The mis-balanced operating model.

Most CDD programs are sized for stages 02 and 04 (verification, screening) and underweight stages 05 and 07 (risk rating and ongoing monitoring). Yet enforcement actions consistently turn on stages 05 and 07: a customer was correctly identified at onboarding and then under-monitored as risk evolved. Re-balance accordingly.

Chapter IV · Vocabulary

Core concepts & vocabulary.

The two-dozen concepts you'll see in every regulatory exam, vendor pitch, and consulting deck. Internalize these before reading scenarios.

§ 04 · Vocabulary you need

Customer-side concepts

KYC (Know Your Customer)
The colloquial term for the process of identifying, verifying, and risk-rating a customer at onboarding and on an ongoing basis. Often used loosely as a synonym for CDD but technically narrower (focused on identification and verification).
CDD (Customer Due Diligence)
The regulatory term defined in FATF Rec. 10. Broader than KYC: includes ongoing monitoring of the relationship, not just initial identification.
CIP (Customer Identification Program)
The US-specific obligation under Patriot Act §326 to verify identity at onboarding. The minimum identity data set: name, date of birth (for individuals), address, ID number.
EDD (Enhanced Due Diligence)
Heightened CDD applied to higher-risk customers — PEPs, customers in high-risk geographies, complex ownership structures, correspondent banking relationships, cash-intensive businesses. Requires senior management approval and deeper SoW/SoF inquiry.
SDD (Simplified Due Diligence)
Permitted in some jurisdictions for demonstrably low-risk relationships (e.g. listed companies on a major exchange, certain regulated financial institutions). Reduces but does not eliminate the CDD obligation.
Beneficial Owner / UBO
The natural person(s) who ultimately own or control a legal entity (typically ≥25% threshold) or on whose behalf a transaction is conducted. For trusts, includes settlor, trustee, protector, beneficiaries, and any other natural person exercising ultimate effective control.
Controlling Person
A natural person who exercises significant control over a legal-entity customer (in the US CDD Rule, one such individual is always required, regardless of ownership percentage). Senior managing officials are the usual default.
PEP (Politically Exposed Person)
A natural person entrusted with a prominent public function. Foreign PEPs — heads of state, senior politicians, senior judicial/military officials, senior executives of state-owned enterprises — always require EDD. Domestic PEPs and international organization PEPs require risk-sensitive treatment.
RCA (Relatives & Close Associates)
Family members and close business associates of PEPs. Carry equivalent risk in most jurisdictions and are screened alongside the PEP themselves.
Source of Wealth (SoW)
The origin of the customer's entire wealth — how they accumulated their net worth over their lifetime. Required for HNW, PEPs, and EDD-tier customers. "Inheritance from family business" or "founder of listed tech company X (IPO 2014)" are typical SoW statements.
Source of Funds (SoF)
The origin of the specific funds used in the relationship — proceeds of a property sale, salary, a particular business deal. SoF is transactional; SoW is aggregate.
Expected Activity Profile
The customer's expected transaction behavior — volumes, geographies, counterparties, product mix, channels — documented at onboarding and refreshed on an ongoing basis. The baseline against which TM scenarios calibrate.

Process-side concepts

Customer Risk Rating (CRR)
The composite risk score assigned to each customer, typically on a Low/Medium/High (or 1–5) scale, driven by attributes (geography, product, channel, customer type, PEP status, etc.) and refreshed periodically and on triggering events.
Risk-based approach (RBA)
The principle — embedded in FATF Rec. 1 — that AML resources should be allocated proportionally to assessed risk. The CRR is the operational mechanism for the RBA.
Periodic refresh
Re-performing CDD at fixed intervals based on risk: typically 5 years for Low, 3 years for Medium, 1 year for High. The historical default in most banks; increasingly being supplanted by perpetual KYC.
Event-driven refresh (trigger-based review)
Re-performing CDD when something material happens: an adverse-media hit, a TM alert, a sanctions list update affecting a related party, an authorized signatory change, a sudden product expansion.
Perpetual KYC (pKYC)
Continuous CDD: the customer profile is refreshed continuously from external data sources (registries, screening lists, news, behavioral signals) rather than at fixed intervals. Eliminates the refresh backlog and produces a real-time risk picture. Mainstream in tier-1 banks from ~2022 onward.
KYC remediation
A program to bring a back-book of customers up to current CDD standard — usually triggered by a regulatory finding, an acquisition, or migration to a new platform. Notoriously expensive and lengthy; often where consulting firms earn their AML revenue.
Reliance / introducer model
Under FATF Rec. 17 and equivalents, an FI may rely on another regulated FI or a designated third party to perform certain CDD steps. The relying FI remains accountable. Frequently abused historically — restricted in many regimes today.
Outsourcing
Distinct from reliance. The FI uses a vendor to perform CDD tasks on its behalf, with the FI retaining full accountability. Common for ID verification, screening, document collection. Vendor risk management applies (e.g. OCC Bulletin 2023-17, EBA Guidelines on Outsourcing).

Verification and screening concepts

Liveness detection
Biometric check that the person presenting a face is alive and physically present (not a photo, video replay, or deepfake). Liveness 2.0 uses challenge-response (e.g. follow a moving dot, speak a phrase, head movement) to defeat passive-attack vectors.
Electronic identity / eID
A government-backed digital identity used for identity proofing — Aadhaar (India), MyInfo (Singapore), BankID (Nordics), eIDAS notified schemes (EU), ID.me / Login.gov (US, limited), the UK "Brit Card" / GOV.UK One Login (rolling out).
Sanctions screening
Real-time and batch matching of customer/transaction names against sanctions lists (OFAC SDN, UN, EU, UK OFSI, etc.). True hits block onboarding or transactions.
PEP screening
Matching against PEP databases (commercial — Dow Jones, World-Check; or built in-house). Hits require senior approval and EDD measures, not automatic blocking.
Adverse media (negative news) screening
Matching against news databases for negative coverage relevant to financial crime: regulatory action, criminal investigation, asset seizure, leaks (Panama, Pandora, FinCEN Files). Hits trigger investigation and may justify CRR uplift or exit.
False positive rate
The proportion of screening matches that, on investigation, are not the named party (different person of the same name, transliteration coincidence, etc.). Sanctions screening rates of 95–99% false positives are not uncommon — the operational burden is enormous.

Chapter V · Customer types

Customer types & onboarding variants.

CDD requirements vary substantially across customer types. The variation is what makes CDD operationally complex.

§ 05 · Who you are onboarding

A retail bank onboarding a salaried individual through a digital channel and a private bank onboarding the family trust of a foreign PEP are performing technically the same regulatory activity, but operationally they are different sports. The matrix below sketches the substantive differences.

Individual customers

Retail / mass-market

Salaried individual, simple SoF (employer payroll). Document + database verification typically sufficient. Digital channel; high straight-through rate the operational priority. CRR usually Low.

Mass affluent / HNW

Wealth tier where SoW becomes material. Document verification supplemented by wealth-source attestation and credible-source corroboration (LinkedIn profile, prior-employer confirmation, sale agreements). CRR typically Medium.

Ultra-HNW / Private banking

Bespoke onboarding. Full SoW dossier, often supported by external due diligence (Diligent, K2, Kroll reports). Family office structures, trust arrangements, multi-jurisdictional. EDD by default; senior management approval; relationship manager personally accountable.

Legal entities

Entity typeSpecific CDD focus
Listed company (major exchange)SDD may be available. Verify listing status; check senior officials. Public-issuer disclosures are most of the dossier.
Private company / LLC / LPIncorporation docs, register filings, directors and authorized signatories, ≥25% UBOs identified and verified. Operating address verified independently.

Relationship-type variants

Correspondent banking
One bank holds an account for another bank. CDD is performed on the respondent institution, not its underlying customers. Wolfsberg CBDDQ is the de facto standard questionnaire. Nested correspondent relationships (the respondent's respondents) are a perennial weakness.
Trade finance counterparties
LCs, guarantees, and trade-credit involve counterparties (buyer, seller, shipper, insurer) on whom CDD must be performed even when they are not direct customers. Dual-use goods, deceptive shipping practices, and sanctioned port calls are recurring red flags.
Wealth management — trust & corporate service providers
When the customer is a trust or corporate-service vehicle administered by a regulated TCSP, the FI performs CDD on the vehicle and the underlying beneficial owners. The TCSP is itself a regulated entity in most jurisdictions and the relationship has its own risk profile.
Crypto / VASPs
Virtual Asset Service Providers (exchanges, custodians, certain OTC desks) are CDD subjects when they bank with a traditional FI. The Travel Rule (FATF Rec. 16) requires originator/beneficiary information to travel with the transaction across VASPs.
Non-resident / non-domestic customers
Customer resides in a different jurisdiction from the FI booking the account. Drives CRR uplift; requires verification techniques that work cross-border (remote ID, notarized documents, eID interoperability).

Chapter VI · Identity

Identity verification.

The methods by which an FI verifies that the person or entity presenting itself is who it claims to be — and how each method fails.

§ 06 · Proving who is who

Identity verification is the technically richest part of CDD. It is also where the AI-fraud wave (synthetic identities, deepfakes, AI-generated documents) has hit hardest in 2023–2026, forcing rapid evolution of historically static methods.

The 2024–2026 inflection

Generative AI has industrialized synthetic-identity attacks.

Before 2023, a document-plus-selfie onboarding flow was acceptable for retail. By 2025, generative AI had industrialized synthetic-identity attacks: AI-generated passports, AI-generated selfies, deepfake liveness defeats, fully autonomous agentic account-opening at machine speed. Sumsub and Fourthline both report 3–6× year-over-year increases in deepfake attacks against onboarding. The serious responses combine document forensics (Finovox, Onfido), challenge-response liveness, behavioral biometrics, device intelligence, and cross-FI consortium data on known synthetics. No single signal is sufficient.

Verification standards and frameworks

  • NIST SP 800-63-3 (Identity Assurance Levels — IAL1/2/3). The US technical standard. Used widely outside the US too.
  • eIDAS Regulation (EU). Establishes Low / Substantial / High assurance levels for cross-border digital identity.
  • FATF guidance on Digital Identity (2020). Confirms digital ID systems can satisfy CDD identification requirements where assurance is sufficient.
  • FFIEC BSA/AML Examination Manual — CIP testing procedures. Operational US standard.

Chapter VII · Ownership

Beneficial ownership (UBO).

The single hardest part of CDD: tracing the natural persons who ultimately own or control a legal entity, often through layers of jurisdictional opacity.

§ 07 · Who really owns this

UBO identification is where most enforcement actions land — because it is where determined bad actors invest the most effort to obscure themselves. A bank can do CIP perfectly, verify ID with the best biometric stack, run sanctions screening flawlessly, and still be exposed if it cannot see through to the natural person ultimately benefiting from the relationship.

The 25% rule and its limits

Most jurisdictions — FATF, EU AMLD, US FinCEN CDD Rule, UK MLR 2017 — set the UBO threshold at 25% direct or indirect ownership. A natural person owning 25% or more of a legal entity (directly, or via intermediate entities by multiplication of percentages) is a beneficial owner. The threshold has obvious limitations:

  • It is a floor, not a ceiling. A person owning <25% but exercising effective control (board majority, special voting rights, golden share) is still a beneficial owner.
  • Most rules require identifying at least one controlling person regardless of ownership, usually a senior managing official, ensuring no entity escapes UBO entirely.
  • Five 19% shareholders is the classic structuring move to defeat the 25% threshold. Most regulators expect FIs to identify all such "near-threshold" holdings on a risk-sensitive basis.

Common opacity techniques

01 — Multi-layer holding chains
High risk
Entity owned by entity owned by entity owned by entity…
An LP in jurisdiction A holds shares in a company in jurisdiction B which holds a trust in jurisdiction C, whose beneficiaries are entities in jurisdictions D and E. Without coordinated data, the natural person at the top is invisible.
ResponseExternal UBO graph databases (Sayari, Moody's Orbis, OpenCorporates), entity resolution, refusal to onboard where the chain cannot be traced to natural persons.
02 — Nominee directors and shareholders
High risk
Professional intermediary appears in registry, real owner does not.
A trust and company service provider supplies a nominee director and a nominee shareholder who hold the position on behalf of an undisclosed principal. Registries show the nominee; nothing shows the principal except a private declaration of trust.
ResponseProbe nominee arrangements explicitly at onboarding; require the principal to be disclosed; treat nominee-only structures as EDD by default; verify against industry intelligence on known nominee providers.
03 — Bearer shares
Declining
Ownership transfers by physical handover of the certificate.
Historically widespread (Panama, BVI, Switzerland). Whoever holds the certificate owns the company; ownership can transfer untraceably overnight. FATF pressure has eliminated most regimes by 2026 but residual exposure remains in some structures.
ResponseDecline to onboard entities with bearer-share capacity unless immobilized at a regulated custodian and verified.
04 — Trust within trust
High risk
Beneficiaries are themselves trusts, foundations, or discretionary classes.
A trust's beneficiaries are described as "the descendants of X for the time being" or "such persons as the trustee may from time to time appoint." The trustee has discretion; no current natural beneficial owner can be named.
ResponseIdentify settlor, trustee, protector, and the class of beneficiaries; document the trustee's discretionary powers; treat as EDD; require disclosure of current beneficiaries actually receiving distributions.
05 — Free-zone & offshore SPVs
Medium risk
Light-touch jurisdiction with low-substance company-formation regime.
DIFC, ADGM, Cayman, BVI, Delaware (historically), Wyoming, Nevis — paper SPVs incorporated for legitimate tax-neutrality reasons but indistinguishable in registry filings from concealment vehicles.
ResponseApply substance test (employees, premises, operations); look-through to UBO must succeed; corroborate ownership via secondary sources.
06 — Cross-jurisdictional disagreement
High risk
Registry in jurisdiction A says X owns Y; intelligence suggests Z actually does.
Discrepancies between registry filings and external intelligence (leaks, journalistic reporting, sanctions designations). The Panama, Paradise, Pandora, and FinCEN Files leaks are the canonical examples.
ResponseAdverse-media screening against leaks databases; discrepancy reporting (UK PSC discrepancy regime, EU AMLD5); refusal to onboard where intelligence contradicts filings and cannot be resolved.

Beneficial ownership registries

A major regulatory trend since AMLD4 (2015) has been the move from FI-collected to state-collected UBO data. The picture in 2026:

  • US — FinCEN BOI database (Corporate Transparency Act). Reporting companies file directly with FinCEN. FI access requires customer consent. Significantly narrower in 2024–2025 after various court rulings on the constitutionality of the CTA; situation evolving.
  • UK — Companies House Persons with Significant Control (PSC) register. Public. Subject to major data-quality reform under the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023.
  • UK — Register of Overseas Entities. Foreign entities owning UK property must register their beneficial owners.
  • EU — Member State central registers. Mandatory under AMLD5; public access restricted by ECJ ruling in WM and Sovim (Nov 2022). The 2024 AML Regulation re-frames access criteria.
  • Singapore — ACRA Register of Registrable Controllers. Limited access.
  • Commercial aggregators — Sayari, Moody's Orbis, OpenCorporates, Diligent, KYCnet. Combine registry data across jurisdictions with relationship-graph capabilities; in practice the operational source for most FI UBO work.

A realistic posture

Triangulate; don't reconcile away.

Registry data is improving but still incomplete and frequently stale. Treat any single source as a starting point, not ground truth. The professional standard is triangulation: registry filings + commercial aggregator + customer attestation + (where risk warrants) external due-diligence report. Disagreements between these are themselves a signal — not noise to be reconciled away.

Chapter VIII · Screening

Sanctions, PEP & adverse media screening.

The three screening dimensions, how they differ in disposition, and where the operational pain concentrates.

§ 08 · Who you are really dealing with

Screening is conceptually simple — match a name against a list — and operationally brutal. Every CDD program runs three distinct screening dimensions, each with different lists, different decisioning, and different failure modes.

The three dimensions

DimensionListsIf matched, what?
SanctionsOFAC SDN/SSI/sectoral (US), UN consolidated, EU consolidated, UK OFSI, AUSTRAC, national sanctions, secondary-sanction designations.Block. Strict liability in most regimes — cannot onboard, cannot transact. Investigation determines whether the customer is genuinely the sanctioned party.
PEPCommercial PEP databases (Dow Jones RiskCenter, LSEG World-Check, RDC/Moody's, Sayari); foreign + domestic + international-org categories; RCAs.EDD trigger. Not a block. Requires senior management approval, SoW/SoF, enhanced ongoing monitoring.
Adverse mediaCommercial adverse-media databases (LSEG World-Check, ComplyAdvantage, RDC), plus leaks (ICIJ databases — Panama, Pandora, FinCEN Files), regulatory enforcement registers.Investigation trigger. Disposition depends on the credibility, recency, and AML relevance of the news.

When screening runs

  • At onboarding. Customer name, UBOs, controlling persons, authorized signatories — all screened.
  • Continuously, against list updates. Every list update is rescreened against the customer book. OFAC SDN updates can occur intra-day.
  • At periodic refresh / on event triggers. Rescreen as part of refresh; rescreen on material change (e.g. new signatory added).
  • In payment flows. Sanctions screening on the originator, beneficiary, and intermediary banks of every cross-border payment.

Matching techniques

Exact matching
Character-for-character match. Useful for unique identifiers (passport number, ID number) but useless for names (transliteration, romanization, name-order, nicknames).
Fuzzy matching
Edit-distance or token-similarity matching: "John Smith" matches "Jon Smith". Most commercial engines use layered fuzzy approaches.
Phonetic matching
Soundex / Metaphone / NYSIIS — names that sound similar match. Important for transliterated Arabic, Russian, Chinese names. Now layered with other techniques.
Transliteration / romanization
"Mohammed" / "Muhammad" / "Mohamed" / "Mohammad" — all the same name. Mandatory for sanctions screening.
Address and DoB co-matching
Modern engines reduce false positives by requiring name plus address or name plus DoB match. Trade-off: missing data in either field can defeat the technique.
Entity-as-customer matching
Screening a legal entity against the SDN entity list. Often weaker than individual screening because entity naming is more idiosyncratic ("ABC Trading" matches many things).

The false-positive problem

Sanctions screening commonly generates 95–99% false positives. A bank with 10 million customers running daily OFAC rescreens may produce thousands of new "hits" per day, the overwhelming majority of which are different people with similar names. The operational pattern:

  • Layered matching with tunable thresholds. Tighten matching to reduce noise; risk-test the tuning to ensure true hits aren't lost (the "above-the-line / below-the-line" approach borrowed from TM).
  • L1 alert handling. First-line analysts dispose of obvious false positives based on secondary attributes (DoB doesn't match, country of residence doesn't match, occupation doesn't match).
  • L2 investigation. Genuine ambiguity escalated for deeper investigation.
  • QA sampling. Second line samples disposition decisions to detect over-clearing or bias-driven errors.
  • Model-tuning feedback loop. Disposition data feeds back into matching threshold and rule tuning over time.

Under-appreciated risk

Tight enough to be operable, loose enough that no true hit is missed.

Most enforcement actions and large fines stemming from screening failures are about missed true hits, not false positives. The fine costs $1bn; the false-positive operational burden costs $50m a year. The asymmetry should drive tuning: tight enough to be operable, loose enough that no true hit is plausibly missed. "Operable" is not the same as "comfortable for the L1 queue".

Chapter IX · Risk rating

Customer risk rating.

The composite score that drives downstream monitoring intensity, refresh cadence, EDD triggers, and exit decisions.

§ 09 · Ranking the risk

The CRR is the operational manifestation of the risk-based approach. Done well, it ensures that the riskiest 5–10% of the book gets the most attention. Done badly, it either under-monitors high-risk customers (a recurring enforcement pattern) or floods the alert queue with low-risk noise.

Standard risk-factor catalog

DimensionTypical factors
Customer attributesType (individual vs entity), PEP status (foreign vs domestic), occupation, industry, wealth tier, nationality, residence, age.
GeographyCountry of residence, country of business, countries of frequent transaction. FATF "increased monitoring" and "high-risk jurisdictions" lists drive the floors. EU high-risk third countries list. Sanctions exposure.
ProductCash-intensive products, private banking, correspondent banking, trade finance, prepaid cards, crypto on-ramps. Each has a default risk weighting.
ChannelBranch (face-to-face) vs digital vs broker-introduced vs RM-led. Non-face-to-face onboarding historically attracted a CDD weight; less so in 2026 with strong digital ID, but still applies in some regimes.
BehavioralTransaction patterns vs expected, geography mix, counterparty mix, velocity changes. The TM-feedback dimension.

Scoring approaches

Rule-based / matrix scoring
Each factor assigned a weight; weighted sum produces the score; bands map to Low/Medium/High. Most common approach. Transparent, easy to govern under SR 11-7, easy to defend to examiners. Fixed weights drift from true risk over time.
Statistical / ML-based scoring
Supervised models trained on historical SAR outcomes; or unsupervised anomaly detection across the customer book. More adaptive, harder to explain. Subject to full SR 11-7 model risk governance — independent validation, annual re-validation, monitoring of performance.
Hybrid
Rule-based floor (regulatory minima — e.g. all PEPs must be at least High) overlaid with statistical adjustments from behavioral signals. The mainstream 2026 architecture.

Standard rating scale

Low

Periodic refresh ~5 years. Standard TM thresholds. No EDD trigger. Standard onboarding pathway. Bulk of retail book sits here.

Medium

Periodic refresh ~3 years. Slightly tighter TM thresholds. No automatic EDD but elevated scrutiny on certain triggers.

High

Periodic refresh ~1 year. EDD required. Senior management approval at onboarding. Tightest TM thresholds. More frequent rescreening.

Some FIs add Prohibited (cannot onboard — sanctioned jurisdictions, certain customer types) and Critical (an additional tier above High for the very highest-risk relationships requiring board or chief risk officer approval).

Re-rating triggers

  • Periodic refresh comes due.
  • Material change in customer attributes (new authorized signatory, change of UBO, change of country of residence, change of industry).
  • Material change in transaction behavior (volume spike, new geographies, new product use).
  • Adverse media hit or PEP designation update.
  • Sanctions list update affecting related party.
  • SAR filed on the customer.
  • TM alert that the L1/L2 investigator deemed material.

SR 11-7 implications

The CRR is a model, not just a rule.

Under US Federal Reserve SR 11-7 (and its OCC and FDIC equivalents), the CRR is a "model" subject to model risk management. That means: documented methodology, independent validation before production, ongoing monitoring of performance, annual revalidation, governance over overrides, and an inventory entry in the model risk register. Treating the CRR as a "rule" outside model risk governance is a recurring exam finding.

Chapter X · EDD

Enhanced due diligence.

The escalated CDD regime applied to higher-risk relationships, what it requires, and the senior-approval discipline that surrounds it.

§ 10 · When standard CDD is not enough

EDD is not a different process from CDD — it is CDD with more depth, more sources, more senior involvement, and more frequent refresh. It is invoked when the risk picture warrants substantially heightened scrutiny.

When EDD is required

  • Foreign PEPs (FATF Rec. 12). Always EDD. Senior management approval at onboarding and at each refresh.
  • Customers from FATF "increased monitoring" or high-risk jurisdictions. EU equivalent: the EU high-risk third countries list. FATF "grey list" / "black list" countries.
  • Correspondent banking relationships, particularly cross-border. Wolfsberg CBDDQ-driven EDD; focus on the respondent's AML controls and customer base.
  • Complex ownership structures. Multi-jurisdictional, multi-layer, nominee arrangements.
  • Cash-intensive businesses (money service businesses, casinos, certain retail).
  • Wealth-management / private banking customers above defined thresholds.
  • Non-face-to-face onboarding in jurisdictions that still treat it as elevated.
  • Customers in industries with elevated ML/TF risk: precious metals/stones, art and antiquities, real estate, virtual assets, cannabis (in jurisdictions where legal).
  • Any customer whose CRR is High or whose risk picture has materially changed.

What EDD adds on top of standard CDD

The PEP regime in detail

PEPs are the most common EDD trigger and a recurring enforcement focal point. The regime distinguishes three categories with different obligations:

  • Foreign PEPs. FATF Rec. 12: always EDD. Heads of state, ministers, senior judicial, military, central bank, state-owned-enterprise senior executives, senior political-party officials. Treatment usually persists for some defined period after they leave office (the "once a PEP, always a PEP" debate is not resolved across jurisdictions).
  • Domestic PEPs. Risk-sensitive treatment. EDD applies where risk-rated as high — usually by seniority of office.
  • International organization PEPs. Heads and senior executives of intergovernmental bodies (UN, IMF, World Bank, regional development banks). Risk-sensitive.
  • RCAs. Spouses, children, parents, business partners. Treated with PEP-equivalent measures.

Frequent enforcement pattern

The PEP forgotten over time.

The recurring PEP-related finding is that a PEP was identified at onboarding, EDD was performed initially, and then the customer's heightened risk was forgotten. As the PEP rotated through political offices, accumulated wealth in unexplained ways, or became implicated in adverse media, the FI continued treating the relationship as if it were a normal HNW account. The control is not at onboarding; it is in the ongoing-monitoring discipline applied for years afterward.

Chapter XI · pKYC

Ongoing monitoring & perpetual KYC.

How CDD evolves from a periodic batch process into a continuous data discipline — and why the shift is the defining 2022–2026 trend in the field.

§ 11 · Keeping the picture current

The classical CDD operating model treats refresh as a batch event: customers' CDD files are re-performed every 1, 3, or 5 years based on CRR. In practice, this generates large backlogs (often years overdue), produces a stale risk picture, and consumes enormous L1 capacity. The mainstream alternative since ~2022 is perpetual KYC (pKYC): continuous, event-driven refresh, with the file in a permanent "in-review" state instead of moving through discrete review cycles.

Periodic vs perpetual — the operational shift

Event-driven triggers

A perpetual-KYC architecture identifies and acts on material changes in real time. Standard triggers:

  • Sanctions list update affecting the customer, UBO, or related party.
  • PEP designation update — new appointment, removal from office, RCA designation.
  • Adverse media hit — credible news of regulatory action, criminal proceeding, leak, asset seizure.
  • Registry update — change of directors, change of UBO, change of incorporation status, dissolution.
  • Customer-initiated change — new authorized signatory, new beneficial owner, change of registered address, new business line.
  • Transaction monitoring signal — a TM alert with sufficient confidence to warrant CDD re-assessment (not every alert; this is calibration-dependent).
  • Behavioral change — material drift from expected activity profile (volume, geography, product, counterparties).
  • Internal escalation — RM raises a concern; second-line review identifies an issue.
  • Group-level event — a related customer in the group has an event that materially affects the focal customer's risk picture.

Where pKYC architectures break

The architecture is conceptually clean. The 2022–2026 implementation experience has been less clean:

  • Trigger calibration. Tune triggers loose enough to catch material change; tight enough not to drown analysts in noise. The same threshold debate as TM.
  • External data quality. Perpetual KYC is only as good as the data feeds. Stale registries, coverage gaps, false PEP designations, leaks lifted into screening without disposition — all degrade the pipeline.
  • Customer experience. A perpetual-KYC pipeline that contacts the customer every time a registry attribute changes is intolerable. Most material changes should be resolved without customer outreach.
  • Decision audit trail. Continuous decisioning requires continuous documentation. Periodic refresh's "review packet" needs an event-stream equivalent.
  • Coexistence with periodic. Most banks run hybrid for years: pKYC for the segments where data feeds are richest, periodic for the rest. Cross-cutting governance gets complicated.

Reference architecture

A workable pKYC stack

  1. Canonical customer record as the single source of truth — customer attributes, UBO chain, screening status, CRR, expected activity profile.
  2. External data feeds ingested continuously: sanctions list updates, PEP database updates, adverse-media feed, registry change feed (Companies House, ACRA, FinCEN BOI, etc.), watchlist updates.
  3. Trigger engine that detects material change between the previous canonical state and the new external data. Configurable trigger logic; per-CRR tier sensitivity.
  4. Decisioning layer that routes trigger events: auto-resolve (low-materiality), L1 analyst (review), L2 investigation (escalation), CRR re-rating, SAR consideration, exit consideration.
  5. Customer outreach module that batches and minimizes customer-facing requests; uses digital channels where possible; documents the request and response.
  6. Audit and governance layer that records every state change, decision, and reviewer; feeds MI dashboards and exam preparation.

Early adopters

Where pKYC has been deployed in production.

ABN AMRO, ING, Standard Chartered, HSBC, and DBS have publicly described pKYC implementations from 2021–2024. Vendor platforms: Fenergo (KYC Refresh), Quantexa (Contextual Decision Intelligence), Encompass (KYC Now), Appian (Connected KYC), Pega (KYC). Implementations consistently take 12–24 months; consistently produce 30–60% reductions in L1 effort against equivalent periodic baseline.

Chapter XII · Specialized

Specialized domains.

CDD in correspondent banking, trade finance, virtual assets, and wealth management — each domain bends the standard model in domain-specific ways.

§ 12 · CDD at the edges

Correspondent banking

A correspondent banking relationship is a bank-to-bank account: the respondent bank holds an account with the correspondent (commonly to access a payment system or currency the respondent does not directly clear). The respondent's customers are not the correspondent's customers, but the correspondent is exposed to them through the respondent's controls.

  • Wolfsberg CBDDQ. The industry-standard correspondent banking due diligence questionnaire. A near-mandatory artifact for every correspondent relationship.
  • Nested relationships. The respondent's own respondents — sometimes opaque. The Riggs Bank and BCCI scandals turned partly on nested correspondent exposure.
  • Payable-Through Accounts (PTAs). Accounts where the respondent's customers can transact directly through the correspondent's account. Prohibited or sharply restricted in most regimes.
  • FATF Rec. 13. Specific EDD on cross-border correspondent relationships: gather information on the respondent's business and reputation, AML controls, supervision quality.

Trade finance

Letters of credit, guarantees, supply-chain finance, and documentary collections involve counterparties on whom CDD must be performed even when they are not direct customers of the FI.

  • Buyer / seller / shipper / insurer. All require some level of due diligence.
  • Dual-use goods. Goods with both civilian and military application. Heightened review. Reference: Wassenaar Arrangement, US Commerce Control List, EU dual-use Regulation.
  • Deceptive shipping practices. AIS-off vessel traffic, ship-to-ship transfers, false flag-of-convenience operations. Used to evade sanctions on Iran, North Korea, Russia.
  • Trade-based money laundering (TBML). Over/under-invoicing, phantom shipments, multiple invoicing — laundering value through trade flows. Specific to the trade-finance domain.
  • References: Wolfsberg Trade Finance Principles; ICC banking commission guidance; FATF TBML reports.

Virtual assets / VASPs

Crypto-asset service providers are CDD subjects when they bank with traditional FIs, and themselves perform CDD on their own users. The domain has its own regulatory architecture, evolving rapidly:

  • FATF Rec. 15 + 2019 VASP guidance. Brings VASPs within scope of AML/CFT.
  • Travel Rule (FATF Rec. 16). Originator and beneficiary information must travel with crypto transactions across VASPs above a threshold (USD 1,000 in most regimes).
  • EU MiCA Regulation (2023, fully eff. 2024). Comprehensive regulation of crypto-asset service providers; includes CDD requirements.
  • US — FinCEN MSB registration, OFAC sanctions enforcement (e.g. Tornado Cash, Lazarus Group designations).
  • Singapore — MAS DPT (Digital Payment Token) regime.
  • Risk dimensions: mixing services, privacy coins, cross-chain bridges, decentralized exchanges, NFT money-laundering.

Private banking and wealth management

The traditional EDD-by-default domain. CDD here is qualitatively different — relationship-manager-led, multi-jurisdictional, multi-generational, and dependent on heavy SoW investigation.

  • Source of Wealth dossier — the central artifact. May run to dozens of pages with corroborating documents and external due-diligence reports.
  • Family trust complexity — multi-generational structures with discretionary beneficiaries, protectors, and reserve powers. Trust deeds are read carefully.
  • Single-family offices and multi-family offices — themselves regulated in some jurisdictions (US RIA, Singapore VCC, Hong Kong OFC).
  • PEP and RCA concentration — wealth-management books have higher PEP density than retail.
  • Concierge / referral risk — wealthy customers are introduced through professional networks (lawyers, accountants, family advisers). Reliance is constrained but the introduction risk remains.

Charities and non-profit organizations

  • FATF Rec. 8. NPOs are not all high-risk; FATF specifically warns against blanket de-risking of the NPO sector.
  • CDD focus: mission, beneficiary geography, principal donors, principal grantees, governance. NPOs operating in conflict zones or fragile states attract heightened scrutiny.
  • Terrorist-financing exposure remains the principal concern; charity diversion has been used historically by terrorist organizations.

Three orders and a manual.

Outputs are bespoke human-authored reports that combine open-source intelligence, local-language press, and (where permitted) source interviews.

Chapter XV · Trends

Emerging trends.

The five or six structural shifts reshaping CDD between 2024 and 2028. Watch these closely.

§ 15 · Where the field is heading

1. Perpetual KYC becomes the default

Major banks have largely committed to pKYC architectures; tier-2 and tier-3 are following. By 2027–2028, expect periodic refresh to be the exception (smaller banks, specific segments) rather than the default. Implications: data-feed infrastructure becomes the constraint; L1 workforce reshapes around event-driven case handling rather than batch refresh cycles; vendor selection pivots toward orchestration and decisioning rather than data alone.

2. AI-generated synthetic identities at industrial scale

Sumsub and Fourthline both report deepfake-related attack volumes growing 3–6× year-on-year. AI-generated passports, AI-generated selfies that pass naive liveness, agentic AI that drives full account-opening flows at machine speed across many banks in parallel. The defensive response: liveness 2.0 (challenge-response), document forensics (Finovox), behavioral biometrics across the session, device intelligence, and consortium data on known synthetics. No single signal suffices.

3. Agentic AI in CDD investigation

Following the trajectory in AML transaction monitoring and fraud investigation, agentic AI is being deployed for CDD case work — automated UBO discovery and reconciliation, automated adverse-media review with disposition drafting, automated SAR-precursor narrative drafting. NICE Actimize's X-Sight ActOne agentic AI release (Apr 2025), Quantexa's Q Assist, FICO's analyst copilots, and tier-1 in-house implementations. Realistic 2026 expectation: 40–60% reduction in L1 hours on standard case types; analyst role shifts to oversight and exception handling.

4. State-collected beneficial ownership data

The structural trend since AMLD4: UBO data moves from FI-collected (attestation-based, hard to verify) to state-collected (registry-based, more authoritative). US CTA, UK PSC + Register of Overseas Entities, EU Member State central registers, Singapore RORC, Australian register (under consultation). Access rules remain contested (the ECJ Sovim ruling restricted public access in the EU). The practical implication: state registries are improving fast and will be the primary UBO source by ~2028.

5. Cross-FI information sharing

Pre-2018, AML information sharing between FIs was largely prohibited by privacy law. Since:

  • US — Section 314(b) of the USA PATRIOT Act permits voluntary information sharing among FIs on suspected ML/TF activity.
  • Singapore — COSMIC platform (MAS-led, launched 2024). Permits banks to share customer information on suspicious activity through a regulator-mediated platform.
  • UK — Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023 creates new information-sharing gateways.
  • Project Aurora (BIS Innovation Hub). Federated learning experiments to share AML intelligence without sharing underlying customer data.

Expect more of this — federated learning, regulator-mediated platforms, privacy-preserving collaboration.

6. Digital identity infrastructure

The gradual rollout of government-backed digital identity changes the substrate of CDD identification. eIDAS 2.0 in the EU (including the EU Digital Identity Wallet), India's Aadhaar, Singapore's MyInfo, the UK's GOV.UK One Login. Where they exist, they radically reduce identification cost and friction. The unevenness across geographies (the US has no national equivalent) means CDD operating models will need to handle eID-rich and eID-poor jurisdictions in parallel for the foreseeable future.

7. Convergence with fraud monitoring

Historically siloed (KYC + AML on one side; fraud on the other), the two functions are converging: account-opening fraud is a CDD problem in everything but org chart; first-party / synthetic-identity fraud requires both lifecycle CDD signals and transactional fraud detection. Quantexa, NICE Actimize, FICO, and SAS all market convergence platforms. Operationally, the merge is harder than the technology suggests (different data, different SLA, different second-line ownership).

8. AMLA and EU regulatory centralization

The 2024 EU AML package — directly applicable Regulation (AMLR), recast Directive (AMLD6), and the new Authority (AMLA, Frankfurt, operational 2028) — represents the most significant regulatory restructuring in EU AML history. AMLA will directly supervise the riskiest cross-border FIs. Expectation: harmonized rules across 27 Member States, fewer arbitrage opportunities, more consistent CDD standards.

Chapter XVI · Pitfalls

Common pitfalls & challenges.

Operational anti-patterns that recur across CDD programs regardless of size or jurisdiction. If your program shows any of these, address them before the regulator finds them.

§ 16 · What goes wrong in practice

1 · Onboarding data debt

Customer data captured inconsistently across channels and LOBs; canonical record never built; refresh starts with a data-quality remediation. Mitigation: front-to-back data model with channel-agnostic enforcement.

2 · CRR drift

CRR computed once at onboarding and rarely recomputed. Geography weights drift from current FATF list; product weightings outdated. Mitigation: annual model validation; CRR recomputed on every refresh; rule version control.

3 · Refresh backlog

The classic. Periodic refresh cadence exceeded; sometimes by years. Risk picture stale; regulatory exposure high. Mitigation: pKYC migration; if interim, a remediation program with clear targets.

4 · Screening over-clearing

L1 productivity targets create incentive to clear false-positive backlog quickly; in doing so, some true hits are missed. Mitigation: QA sampling; structured disposition templates; calibration sessions; clear escalation discipline.

5 · UBO self-attestation taken on trust

Customer fills in UBO form; FI files the form; no independent verification. The chain is taken at face value. Mitigation: external UBO data; registry triangulation; substance tests on entities; risk-sensitive deep dives.

6 · PEP regime forgotten over time

Foreign PEP onboarded with full EDD; over years the PEP status fades from operational view and the relationship is handled as a standard HNW account. Mitigation: ongoing PEP screening; EDD discipline maintained for the relationship lifetime.

7 · Sales pressure on Compliance

Revenue-pressure overrides Compliance hesitancy at onboarding; "soft-no" customers re-onboarded through other LOBs. Mitigation: centralized customer view; declination register; independent senior approval for marginal cases.

8 · Documentation expiry

Passport expires; the FI does not notice; identification is technically lapsed. Mitigation: automatic expiry-tracking; customer-facing renewal flows; risk-sensitive grace periods.

9 · New-product CDD gap

A new product launches without the full CDD framework extended to it. The product becomes a back-door for the rest of the bank's risk taxonomy. Mitigation: new-product approval discipline that includes a Compliance sign-off on CDD applicability.

10 · Cross-jurisdictional under-coverage

Branch in a peripheral jurisdiction runs lower standards than the home market; group-level oversight is weak. The Danske Estonia pattern. Mitigation: group-level CDD standards mandatory; second-line testing of peripheral entities; whistleblower channels.

11 · Reliance-on-introducer abuse

Customers onboarded through introducers (brokers, professional intermediaries, group entities) with light CDD on the FI's own side; introducer's quality not independently tested. Mitigation: restrict Rec. 17 reliance; document introducer arrangements; ongoing testing of introducer effectiveness.

12 · Customer-friction over-correction

Following an enforcement event, the FI tightens CDD aggressively; new-customer onboarding times balloon; drop-off rates spike; financial inclusion suffers. Mitigation: targeted tightening informed by typology; process redesign for low-friction high-assurance flows; ongoing measurement.
