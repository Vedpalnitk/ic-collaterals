Chapter I · Foundations

Introduction & purpose.

What screening is, why it sits beside Transaction Monitoring and KYC as the third pillar of the financial-crime control framework, and what makes it operationally distinct.

§ 01 · Foundations

Names, lists, and the law of the land.

Screening is the comparison of names, identifiers, and other attributes — of customers, counterparties, beneficial owners, payment parties, vessels, and aircraft — against curated lists of concern: sanctions, embargoed jurisdictions, politically exposed persons (PEPs), adverse media, and internal blacklists. The goal is to prevent prohibited business from being onboarded or transacted, and to identify elevated-risk business for enhanced due diligence.

Unlike Transaction Monitoring — which is suspicion-based and post-event — screening is largely rule-based and preventative. A sanctions hit is not "suspicious activity"; it is, in the first instance, a legal prohibition on transacting. The bank cannot proceed until the hit is cleared as a false positive. This makes screening one of the only compliance functions that blocks business in real time.

Mental model

A name-matching problem wrapped in a regulatory framework.

Screening is a name-matching problem at industrial scale, wrapped in a regulatory framework. Most of the engineering effort goes into making the match robust enough to catch obfuscated names without drowning the operation in false positives. Most of the regulatory effort goes into showing the matching is complete, current, and properly governed.

Why it exists

  • Legal prohibitions. Sanctions are not guidance — they are binding law. Transacting with a sanctioned party (knowingly or otherwise, in many regimes) exposes the institution to civil penalties, criminal prosecution, and revocation of operating licences.
  • Enforcement consequence. The largest single fines in banking history have been sanctions cases: BNP Paribas (USD 8.9bn, 2014), Standard Chartered (multiple, totalling ~USD 1.7bn), HSBC, Credit Suisse, ING. Sanctions exposure is also the leading reason for de-risking and correspondent-banking exits.
  • Geopolitical immediacy. Sanctions regimes change in hours, not years — see the rapid Russia-related expansions of 2022 onwards. The screening function is the operational lever a bank pulls to react.

What makes screening operationally different from TM

  • Volume. A tier-1 bank screens hundreds of millions of names and tens of millions of payments per day. The platform must perform at industrial throughput.
  • Latency. Payment screening is real-time, sub-second. A SWIFT payment that fails screening cannot leave the bank.
  • Determinism. Same input + same list version = same hit, every time. Auditors and regulators expect this; ML-led screening introduces an explainability challenge.
  • Zero-tolerance. A missed sanctions hit is a strict-liability violation in most regimes, regardless of intent. There is no "false negative is acceptable" tolerance.

Chapter II · Foundations

The regulatory landscape.

The sanctions regimes that mandate screening, the supervisors that enforce it, and the conceptual rules (50% rule, secondary sanctions) that shape implementation.

§ 02 · Why this exists

Multiple regimes, one platform.

Sanctions are made and enforced by national governments and supranational bodies. A bank operating across borders is subject to all of them simultaneously — and they do not always agree. This section walks the major regimes a screening function must contend with, then surfaces the implementation consequences for platform design.

United States

  • OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control), US Treasury — administers and enforces US sanctions. Publishes the SDN List (Specially Designated Nationals), the SSI List (Sectoral Sanctions Identifications, for Russia/Ukraine), the NS-MBS List (Non-SDN Menu-Based Sanctions, for China), and program-specific lists for Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria, Venezuela, etc.
  • The 50% Rule. Any entity owned 50% or more — directly or indirectly, individually or in the aggregate — by one or more SDNs is itself sanctioned, even if not listed. This single rule is the most consequential interpretive principle in screening: it forces banks to screen beneficial ownership, not just names on the list.
  • Secondary sanctions. Sanctions on non-US persons for transacting with listed parties — extending the reach of US sanctions globally and making US-dollar correspondent banking the chokepoint for enforcement.
  • Other US lists: DPL (Denied Persons List, Commerce/BIS), Entity List (BIS), Unverified List, Military End User (MEU) List, SDGT designations, FBI Most Wanted.

European Union

  • EU Consolidated Financial Sanctions List — maintained by the European Commission; consolidates restrictive measures adopted under the Common Foreign and Security Policy (CFSP). Implemented via Council Regulations directly binding on EU persons.
  • EU Anti-Money Laundering Regulation (AMLR), 2024 — formalizes the screening obligation and introduces the EU AML Authority (AMLA) for direct supervision from 2028.
  • Member-state competent authorities (BaFin, AMF, DNB, etc.) enforce locally; national lists may supplement EU-wide measures.

United Kingdom

  • OFSI (Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation), HM Treasury — administers UK financial sanctions; publishes the UK Sanctions List; enforces breaches via civil penalties and (since 2022) strict-liability standard.
  • Sanctions and Anti-Money Laundering Act 2018 — the post-Brexit legal foundation.
  • Russia (Sanctions) Regulations 2019 and dozens of country/thematic regimes.

United Nations

  • UN Consolidated List — Security Council sanctions implemented under UN Charter Chapter VII; binding on all member states. Covers Al-Qaida/ISIL/Da'esh (1267 regime), Taliban (1988 regime), DPRK, Iran, Libya, etc.

Jurisdictional overlap

"Most stringent applicable" is the safe default — until it isn't.

A multinational bank is subject to multiple sanctions regimes simultaneously — and they don't always agree. An entity sanctioned by OFAC but not by the EU presents a complex compliance decision for an EU bank with US-dollar correspondent relationships. "Most stringent applicable" is the safe default, but the conflicts regime (e.g. EU Blocking Statute) can prohibit complying with extraterritorial US measures, creating genuine legal dilemmas. The screening platform must operate per business unit and per booking entity, not bank-wide.

Why this matters for screening design

Screening platforms must support: (a) multiple lists simultaneously, with per-list enable/disable per booking entity; (b) list version control, so any historical hit can be traced to the list as it stood at the moment of screening; (c) real-time list refresh, because new designations are immediately legally effective; (d) governance of internal lists, because internally-maintained blacklists carry the same operational weight as regulator lists.

Chapter III · Foundations

Three flavors of screening.

The screening function delivers three different controls against three different risks. They share infrastructure but differ fundamentally in legal nature.

§ 03 · What to screen against

One engine, three decisions.

Although "screening" is often discussed as a single discipline, three distinct controls live under that banner. They are run on the same platform, against overlapping data, by overlapping investigators — but they answer fundamentally different regulatory questions. Confusing them is one of the most common cultural failure modes in compliance functions.

Flavor 01

Sanctions screening

A binary, strict-liability legal control. A confirmed hit is a prohibition on doing business — the bank cannot onboard the customer, cannot release the payment, and (depending on regime) must freeze any property in its possession. Decision: proceed or block.

Flavor 02

PEP screening

A risk-rating control. A confirmed PEP match is not prohibited; it requires the bank to apply enhanced due diligence, source-of-wealth review, and senior management approval. Decision: onboard with EDD or decline as risk-policy choice.

Flavor 03

Adverse media screening

A reputational and risk control. Hits indicate negative news, criminal allegations, regulatory action, or controversy. Decision: investigate; outcomes range from no action to EDD to exit. Less binary, more judgement-led.

A useful frame

Must not / must more carefully / should know about.

Sanctions tells you what you must not do. PEP tells you what you must do more carefully. Adverse media tells you what you should know about. Different decision rules, different escalation paths, different documentation standards — but one screening engine, one data lake, and increasingly one investigator workflow.

Chapter IV · Lists & lifecycle

The lists landscape.

A surprising amount of operational complexity comes from the lists themselves — what they are, how often they change, how they're structured, and how they're consumed.

§ 04 · The data you screen against

What is actually in the lists.

The lists are not a fixed corpus of known villains. They are dynamic, sprawling, multi-vendor, multi-format data assets — issued daily by regulators in some cases and curated continuously by commercial vendors in others. Understanding them as data is the precondition for everything else in this primer.

PEP lists

Unlike sanctions lists, PEP lists are not published by regulators. They are commercial datasets compiled by vendors (Refinitiv World-Check, Dow Jones, LexisNexis, ComplyAdvantage, Moody's GRID) from public records, gazettes, news, and proprietary research. The vendor's taxonomy of "PEP" is consequential: definitions of domestic vs foreign PEP, close associate, family member, and the once-a-PEP-always-a-PEP question all vary by vendor and by jurisdiction. The FATF Recommendations and EU AMLD set the conceptual baseline; the vendor implements.

Adverse media data sources

Curated and aggregated negative news, regulatory enforcement records, court filings, sanctions analyses, bribery/corruption databases. Sources: Refinitiv World-Check, Dow Jones Risk & Compliance, LexisNexis WorldCompliance, ComplyAdvantage, Moody's GRID, public-records databases. Increasingly enriched with NLP classification (relevance scoring, risk-category tagging) to suppress noise.

Internal lists

Every institution maintains its own internal lists alongside external ones: declined customers, exited customers, watch list (active investigations), fraud blacklist, litigation hold. Internal lists carry the same operational weight as external ones — and the same governance expectations.

List structure — what's actually in a record

A single SDN record (or its equivalent) typically includes:

  • Primary name and one or more aliases (a.k.a., f.k.a., n.k.a.) — often transliterated multiple ways
  • Date(s) of birth, and place of birth
  • Nationality / citizenship
  • Identifiers — passport numbers, national IDs, tax IDs, IMO numbers for vessels, tail numbers for aircraft, SWIFT BICs for entities
  • Addresses
  • Designation reference and program code (e.g. SDGT, UKRAINE-EO13662, CYBER2)
  • Position — for sectoral sanctions, the specific restriction
  • Last updated date

In practice

Lists are living data.

The SDN list changes most business days. New designations are legally effective immediately; banks must ingest, propagate, and re-screen against new entries within hours, not days. Sophisticated programs run portfolio rescreening automatically every time the list changes — not on a fixed cycle.

Chapter V · Lists & lifecycle

The screening lifecycle.

Seven stages, common across all flavors and across both customer and payment screening.

§ 05 · How screening actually works

From list ingestion to blocking report.

However many vendors, however many lists, however many flavors of screening — the operating model collapses to a single seven-stage pipeline. Each stage has its own well-documented failure modes, its own well-documented mitigations, and its own well-documented audit expectations. Mastering screening operationally is, in large part, mastering this pipeline.

01
List ingestion
Fetch list updates from regulator and vendor feeds; version, normalize, validate.
02
Indexing
Build search structures: phonetic codes, n-grams, transliteration variants.
03
Input capture
Customer record at onboarding, or payment message at SWIFT/ACH/SEPA gateway.
04
Matching
Run the candidate input against indexed list entries; emit candidate hits.
05
Hit generation
Score, prioritize, dedupe, attach context (which list, why, what attributes matched).
06
Investigation
Analyst clears (false positive) or confirms (true match) using secondary attributes.
07
Block / release / report
Block payment, freeze assets, file blocking report; or release with documented rationale.

Chapter VI · Vocabulary & matching

Core concepts & vocabulary.

The terms that recur across regulation, vendor pitches, and consulting decks. Internalize these before reading the matching section.

§ 06 · Vocabulary you need

The terms that anchor every screening conversation.

The screening world has a dense vocabulary that recurs across regulators, vendors, lawyers, and engineers — often with subtle but consequential definitional differences. This section is the glossary you need to internalize before walking into a vendor demo, reading an OFAC settlement, or sitting in a model-governance committee.

Sanctions concepts

SDN (Specially Designated National)
An individual or entity on the OFAC SDN List; US persons are prohibited from transacting with them and must block any property in their possession.
Blocked property
Any asset of a sanctioned party that comes into the institution's possession or control. Must be frozen, reported to OFAC within 10 days, and held until the sanction is lifted or licensed.
OFAC licence
An authorization — general or specific — that permits an otherwise-prohibited transaction. General licences apply broadly; specific licences are applied for case-by-case.
The 50% Rule
OFAC interpretive rule that any entity owned 50%+ by one or more SDNs is itself sanctioned, by operation of law, regardless of whether it appears on the list. Forces UBO screening.
Secondary sanctions
Sanctions imposed on non-US persons for dealing with sanctioned parties — extends the reach of US sanctions and is the principal lever of US sanctions diplomacy.
Designation reference / Program code
The legal authority for a listing (e.g. SDGT, IRAN-EO13599, RUSSIA-EO14024). Determines which restrictions apply.
Sectoral sanctions
Targeted restrictions on specific activities (e.g. financing > 14 days for SSI Russian entities) rather than full blocking. More technical to implement; the platform must understand the rule, not just flag the name.
Strict liability
Most sanctions regimes impose liability without proof of intent or knowledge. A bank that processes a payment to a sanctioned party is in breach even if it didn't know.

PEP concepts

PEP
A natural person entrusted with prominent public functions (head of state, senior politician, judge, senior military, senior executive of a state-owned enterprise).
Domestic vs Foreign PEP
FATF and EU definitions distinguish PEPs in the home country (domestic) from those abroad (foreign). Foreign PEPs are always high-risk; domestic PEPs are risk-rated case-by-case.
RCA (Relative or Close Associate)
Family members and close associates of a PEP. Required to be screened and risk-rated as PEPs in most regimes.
Once-a-PEP
How long after leaving the public role someone remains a PEP. The 5th and 6th EU AMLDs treat someone as PEP for at least 12 months after leaving office, with risk-based extension; some banks operate once-a-PEP-always-a-PEP.

Adverse media concepts

Risk category
Taxonomy of adverse signals: financial crime, terrorism, narcotics, corruption, sanctions evasion, fraud, sexual offences, environmental crime, etc. Drives investigator triage.
Relevance / Recency
Article relevance (is it really about this person?) and recency (is it current?). NLP scoring of both is now standard with vendors.

Matching concepts

Hit
The output of a screening match — a candidate identification of an input against a listed party.
True positive / False positive
A confirmed match is a TP; a name-coincidence that turns out not to be the listed party is an FP. Payment screening typically produces FP rates of 95–99%.
False negative
A listed party not detected. The unforgiving metric; even rare false negatives are regulatory catastrophes.
Match score / Confidence
A numerical score the matching engine assigns to a candidate match. Threshold-based gating decides what becomes a hit for investigation.
Whitelist (Good-Guy list)
Curated list of confirmed false positives that should not re-alert. Tightly governed because a bad entry on the whitelist creates an undetected hole.
Secondary attributes
Non-name attributes used to confirm or refute a name match — DOB, nationality, passport number, address, identifier. The single most powerful FP-reduction lever.

Chapter VII · Vocabulary & matching

Name-matching deep dive.

The single most consequential engineering choice in screening. Most vendor differentiation lives here.

§ 07 · The technical heart

Tolerant enough to catch obfuscation, strict enough not to drown.

Names of sanctioned parties and PEPs are routinely obfuscated, mis-transliterated, abbreviated, or reordered. "Vladimir Putin", "V. Putin", "Vlad Pootin", "путин", "Putin Vladimir Vladimirovich", "بوتين", and "Putin V V" must all match a single underlying record. The matching engine must be tolerant enough to catch real obfuscation and strict enough not to drown in spurious matches against, say, "Vladimir Petrov" or "John Smith".

Stop-word and obfuscation patterns to know

Beyond pure algorithms, screening platforms maintain pattern libraries for common obfuscations:

  • Embedded special characters ("V.l.a.d.i.m.i.r"; "Vlad!mir") — strip and re-match.
  • Numeric substitution (leet) ("Vl4d1m1r") — normalize digits to letters.
  • Inserted whitespace ("Vla dimir") — collapse before matching.
  • Concatenated names ("VladimirPutin") — token segmentation.
  • Honorifics and titles ("Mr Vladimir Putin", "President Putin") — strip before matching.
  • Stripped vowels common in payment-message free-text — use phonetic and consonantal matching as backstop.

Secondary attribute scoring

Once a candidate hit fires on name similarity, the platform applies secondary attributes to raise or lower the score:

  • DOB match — exact +confidence, year-only +partial, mismatch −confidence, missing on either side = no signal
  • Nationality / Country of residence — match +confidence, mismatch −confidence (but cautiously, since residency changes)
  • Identifier match — passport / national ID exact match is near-determinative
  • Address proximity — same country/city raises confidence
  • Gender — only when independently established for the listed party

The trade-off every program lives with

Recall vs. precision is the regulator's question, not yours.

Looser matching → higher recall, higher FP rate, larger investigation team. Tighter matching → lower FP rate, but the regulator's question after any miss will be: "Why didn't your algorithm catch this obvious obfuscation?" The defensible posture is documented, evidence-based threshold setting with ongoing performance monitoring — not optimization for either pole.

Chapter VIII · Screening types

Customer screening.

Screening of the people and entities the bank knowingly transacts with — at onboarding and on an ongoing basis throughout the relationship.

§ 08 · Screening the customer

From the named entity to every party in its orbit.

Customer screening is the older of the two big screening disciplines — it predates payment screening by decades and grew out of KYC. Its scope, though, has steadily expanded: today, screening the customer means screening not just the named entity but every individual and entity in its ownership and control orbit, and re-running that screen every time anything material changes — the customer, the lists, or the relationship.

What gets screened

  • The customer — individual or entity name, DOB, nationality, address, identifiers.
  • Beneficial owners — natural persons owning ≥ 25% (or controlling) the entity. Critical for the 50% rule.
  • Directors, officers, authorized signatories — anyone with control over the account.
  • Related parties — joint account holders, guarantors, trustees, settlors, beneficiaries.
  • For trusts — trustee, settlor, protector, beneficiary, beneficial owner.
  • For correspondent banking — the respondent bank and its own AML programme.

When screening fires

EventWhat is screenedLatency

Customer screening operational flow

At onboarding: capture KYC → extract every name + identifier → screen against sanctions, PEP, adverse media → hits route to investigator → cleared as FP or escalated → onboarding proceeds, is delayed, or is declined. Throughout the relationship: any change in customer data, any list update, any periodic-refresh event re-runs the screen and re-routes any new hits.

The UBO challenge

The 50% rule means screening the named entity alone is insufficient. You must walk the ownership chain to identify the natural persons who ultimately control the entity, then screen each one. Where ownership structures involve multiple corporate layers across jurisdictions — common in trade finance, shipping, private wealth — the UBO chain may have a dozen entities and require enrichment from corporate registries (Companies House, Bureau van Dijk Orbis, OpenCorporates), public filings, and the customer's own disclosures. Many sanctions breaches turn on a UBO failure rather than a name-match failure.

Chapter IX · Screening types

Payment screening.

Real-time screening of every party in every cross-border (and increasingly domestic) payment message. Most volume-intensive part of the screening estate.

§ 09 · Screening the payment

Every party, in real time, at industrial volume.

If customer screening is the slower, more deliberate side of screening, payment screening is the high-velocity one — sub-second, transactional, every party in every message, all day, every day. It is where the bulk of false positives are generated, where the most consequential historical enforcement actions have landed, and where the ISO 20022 transition is currently rewriting expectations.

What gets screened in a payment

Every party named in a payment message:

  • Ordering customer / Debtor (the originator)
  • Ordering institution / Debtor agent (the originator's bank)
  • Intermediary institution(s) (correspondent banks in the chain)
  • Beneficiary institution / Creditor agent (the beneficiary's bank)
  • Beneficiary / Creditor (the recipient)
  • Remittance information (free-text reference field — often contains additional party names)
  • Trade-finance attachments — invoices, bills of lading, shipper, consignee, vessel name, port of loading/discharge, goods description

The ISO 20022 transition

The global SWIFT migration to ISO 20022 (in force end of 2025 for cross-border payments) replaces the legacy MT free-text fields with structured XML that explicitly identifies the originator, originator address, ultimate debtor, ultimate creditor, etc. This is the most significant change to payment screening in two decades:

  • Structured party data — addresses, identifiers, and roles are now in defined fields, not concatenated free text. Matching accuracy improves dramatically when used properly.
  • Ultimate debtor / ultimate creditor — explicit fields for the parties behind the ordering and beneficiary names; previously buried in remittance info or invisible.
  • Truncation no longer mandatory — MT had 4×35-char address blocks; ISO 20022 allows much richer addresses, removing a major source of screening ambiguity.
  • BICs and LEIs — structured machine-readable identifiers reduce reliance on name matching for entities.

Stripping & cover-payment evasion

Historically, sanctioned parties (and their facilitators) used wire stripping — deliberately removing or obfuscating originator/beneficiary information to defeat screening at intermediary banks. Several major US enforcement actions (Lloyds 2009, Credit Suisse 2009, Barclays 2010, ING 2012, Standard Chartered 2012/2019, BNP Paribas 2014) involved stripping. Modern screening platforms must inspect the cover payment chain (MT202 COV) and the remittance free-text field, and re-construct full party information from related messages. ISO 20022's structured fields are designed to make stripping much harder to disguise.

Latency & volume

Payment screening must complete in milliseconds — typically < 200ms end-to-end for the screening step — to avoid material delay in payment processing. A tier-1 international bank processes tens of millions of payments per day, each requiring screening of 3–10 parties; the platform throughput requirement is industrial.

Trade finance & vessel screening

Trade-finance payments carry additional screening dimensions: goods descriptions against controlled/dual-use lists; port and country against sanctioned destinations; vessel name and IMO number against vessel-specific sanctions lists (the practice of identity-laundering — repainting names, switching off AIS — has made dedicated vessel-tracking integration essential, e.g. with Pole Star, Windward, Lloyd's List Intelligence).

P3
Typology
A common-name customer ("Mohammed Ali", "John Smith", "Wang Wei") fires a match against a listed party with the same or similar name but different identifiers, DOB, nationality, or jurisdiction.
Signature
Name similarity high; secondary attributes (DOB, nationality, identifier) mismatch.
Disposition
Cleared as false positive with documented secondary-attribute analysis; whitelisted for future matches.

Confirmed sanctions hit

True positive — listed party
P1
Typology
Name and secondary attributes align convincingly with a listed party on OFAC/UN/EU/UK list.
Signature
High name score + matching DOB/identifier/nationality + plausible role.
Disposition
Block the transaction; freeze any property; file blocking report (OFAC within 10 days of blocking); apply for OFAC licence if permitted activity; do not onboard; document fully.

50% rule indirect hit

UBO-derived sanction
P2
Typology
The named entity is not itself on the list, but ownership analysis reveals 50%+ ownership by one or more SDNs.
Signature
Direct screening returns no hit; UBO chain screening returns SDN match; aggregate ownership ≥ 50%.
Disposition
Treat as blocked party; same handling as direct SDN match.

Sectoral / menu-based sanction

Activity-restricted, not blocked
P2
Typology
Listed party is subject to specific activity restrictions (e.g. debt > 14 days, equity issuance, specific sectors) rather than full blocking.
Signature
Hit on SSI or NS-MBS lists; restriction-type field on list record.
Disposition
Evaluate the specific activity against the menu of restrictions; some transactions permissible, others not. Granular and detail-sensitive.

PEP match

Risk-rating, not prohibition
P2
Typology
Customer or beneficial owner identified as PEP or RCA.
Signature
PEP-list match; vendor-categorized role, country, status (current/former).
Disposition
Apply EDD: source-of-wealth, senior management approval, enhanced ongoing monitoring. Not a block.

Adverse media hit

Negative public record
P3
Typology
Customer linked to financial crime, corruption, fraud, regulatory action, sexual offence, or major controversy via curated media database.
Signature
Vendor-classified risk category; relevance and recency scores; supporting article excerpts.
Disposition
Assess severity and recency; range from no action to EDD to relationship exit. Document reasoning.

Payment hit — counterparty

Beneficiary or originator match
P2
Typology
A name in the payment message (typically the beneficiary or ordering customer) matches a listed party.
Signature
Real-time alert in the payment gateway; payment placed on hold pre-execution.
Disposition
Investigate within payment SLA; release, reject, or block-and-report. Time-critical operation.

Payment hit — free text

Remittance-info match
P3
Typology
A listed name appears in the remittance information / payment reference field (e.g. "Payment for shipment to [sanctioned port]") even though the named parties are clean.
Signature
Match in free-text field; party names independently clear.
Disposition
Investigate context; possible stripping evasion; escalate if suspicious. ISO 20022 transition has reduced the prevalence but not eliminated it.

Vessel / aircraft hit

Asset-level sanction
P2
Typology
A trade-finance or shipping payment references a vessel (by name or IMO) or aircraft (by tail number) that is itself sanctioned.
Signature
Match on IMO/tail number; flag-of-convenience signal; AIS-darkness signal from vessel-tracking provider.
Disposition
Block; consider sanctions evasion typology; check related parties and broader trade-finance chain.

Whitelist-driven suppression

Previously-cleared FP
P4
Typology
A customer or counterparty previously investigated and confirmed as FP triggers a match again on subsequent screening.
Signature
Match on a named-entity-list-entry pair previously dispositioned as FP and added to the good-guy list.
Disposition
Auto-suppress with audit trail; periodic review of whitelist hygiene; unwind suppression if list entry materially changes.

Priority code

An operational, not regulatory, classification.

P1 blocking action required  ·  P2 material risk decision  ·  P3 investigate, often resolved as FP  ·  P4 automated handling. Priority is operational, not regulatory.

Chapter XI · Operations

Worked walkthroughs.

Three concrete examples showing how a hit looks, how it's analyzed, and how an investigator resolves it.

§ 11 · From hit to disposition

Three end-to-end resolutions.

The scenarios in the previous section described shapes. This section walks three of them all the way through: what the hit looked like on screen, what the investigator saw, what they did, and why. These are composites, not real cases — but they reflect the texture of the work as it is genuinely practised in tier-1 institutions.

Walkthrough 01 · false-positive common-name collision

Wang Wei, a software engineer in Shenzhen, fires against an SDN Wang Wei.

Setup. Customer Wang Wei is onboarding for a personal account. Name is captured exactly as printed on national ID.

Hit:

  1. Sanctions screen returns one candidate match: SDN "WANG, Wei" listed under a cyber program
  2. Name similarity score: 0.95 (very high)
  3. Customer DOB: 1987-03-12 · SDN DOB: 1971-08-04 (mismatch)
  4. Customer nationality: Chinese (PRC) · SDN nationality: Chinese (PRC) (match)
  5. Customer ID: PRC national ID 4403... · SDN identifier: PRC passport E12... (different document; no overlap)
  6. Customer address: Shenzhen · SDN known address: Beijing

Investigator analysis. Name similarity alone is not enough — "Wang" is among the most common surnames globally and "Wei" is a common given name. The DOB mismatch alone is decisive; the identifier non-overlap and address mismatch confirm. The match is logged as a confirmed false positive, the rationale documented, and the customer/listed-entry pair added to the good-guy list to suppress future re-alerting on this specific pair (but the customer remains in scope for all future list updates).

Why it works. Secondary attributes did the heavy lifting; name matching alone produces unmanageable volumes on common names. The whitelist entry is per-pair (this customer to this SDN entry) — not a blanket exemption for the customer. If a different SDN listing later matches this customer, it will alert anew.
Walkthrough 02 · confirmed payment hit, block and report

Outgoing wire to a beneficiary linked to a sanctioned entity.

Setup. US-domiciled corporate customer Acme Trading Inc instructs an MT103 of USD 4.2m to beneficiary "Trans-Caspian Logistics LLC" at a Turkish correspondent.

Hit signals:

  1. Beneficiary name matches OFAC SDN entry "Trans-Caspian Logistics LLC" (Russia-related EO14024 program)
  2. SDN entry includes the same registered address (Istanbul branch) cited in the payment
  3. Identifier (Turkish tax number) matches the SDN entry
  4. Match score: 0.99; secondary attribute alignment: 3 of 3

Investigator actions. (a) Payment is held pre-execution at the SWIFT gateway. (b) Investigator confirms the match against OFAC listing details. (c) Compliance officer signs off as confirmed true positive. (d) Funds are blocked — placed in an interest-bearing blocked account in Acme Trading's name. (e) OFAC blocking report is filed within 10 business days. (f) Annual blocked-property report submitted as required. (g) Acme Trading is contacted (limited communication permitted) to explain that the funds are blocked and that they may apply to OFAC for a specific licence if applicable.

Why it works. This is the textbook outcome of payment screening — the control achieved its purpose. Note the asymmetry with AML SAR filing: in screening, the customer must be informed that their payment was blocked (they cannot be left to assume it went through), even though tipping off about the broader investigation may still be restricted.
Walkthrough 03 · 50% rule indirect hit via UBO chain

A clean-looking entity owned 60% by an SDN through nested holdings.

Setup. A new corporate customer "Bluegrass Investments Ltd" is onboarding. The entity itself appears nowhere on any list.

UBO analysis:

  1. Bluegrass Investments Ltd (BVI) is owned 100% by "Coastal Holdings PCC" (Guernsey)
  2. Coastal Holdings PCC is owned 60% by "Mr Aleksandr Ivanov" (individual)
  3. Direct screening of Bluegrass: no hit
  4. Screening of Coastal Holdings: no hit
  5. Screening of Aleksandr Ivanov: hit on SDN list, Russia-EO14024 program, with matching DOB and passport number

Disposition. Aleksandr Ivanov's 60% ownership of Coastal Holdings exceeds the 50% threshold. Coastal Holdings is therefore itself sanctioned by operation of law. Coastal's 100% ownership of Bluegrass means Bluegrass is also sanctioned by operation of law. Onboarding is declined; any pre-existing funds received are blocked; OFAC reporting follows.

Why it works (and where it usually fails). The customer record alone gave no hit. The protection lived entirely in the UBO chain. Programs that screen only the named customer — without walking ownership through every layer — fail this case. UBO data quality (from the customer's disclosures plus enrichment via corporate registry data) is the operational bottleneck. The 50% rule applies to aggregate ownership across multiple SDNs, so the calculation can be more complex than this example.

Chapter XII · Operations

False-positive reduction.

Most screening programs run FP rates of 95–99%. Reducing this — without missing true positives — is where most of the engineering and tuning effort goes.

§ 12 · The central operational problem

Cutting noise without cutting signal.

A typical payment-screening program flags one alert per 50–200 messages and clears more than 95% of them as false positives. That ratio defines the cost structure of the function — the size of the L1 team, the latency at the payment gateway, the percentage of alerts the regulator sampling sees. Reducing it, defensibly, without trading away true positives, is the central engineering and tuning problem of screening.

Secondary-attribute scoring

The largest single FP-reduction lever. Configure the engine to require not just a name-similarity threshold, but corroboration from DOB / nationality / identifier / address before generating a high-priority hit. Many modern vendors expose this as a multi-factor scoring framework with bank-tunable weights.

Whitelisting (Good-Guy lists)

Once a name-pair has been investigated and dispositioned as FP, suppress future re-alerting on that pair. Critical caveats: per-pair, not per-customer; reviewed periodically and unwound if either side changes; documented with the investigation rationale that justified suppression. Bad whitelist hygiene is a regulatory finding hot-spot — a misconfigured "always clear" entry can create a permanent blind spot.

List enrichment & cleanup

Some vendor lists carry weak entries with sparse identifiers. Enrich your local copy of the list with additional data (gazette references, public-domain identifiers, court documents) to give the matching engine more secondary attributes to work with. Drop or flag duplicate entries.

Per-program scoring

Match thresholds calibrated by sanctions program: the threshold for a confirmed-hit alert on an SDGT (terrorism) entry can be looser than for a sectoral SSI entry where the cost of an over-alert is high and the underlying party is more easily disambiguated.

Cultural / linguistic tuning

Recognize that some name conventions produce structurally more FPs (common Chinese, Korean, Arabic, Slavic names with short forms or limited identifier penetration). Apply context-aware thresholds and secondary-attribute weights, rather than a single global setting.

Machine learning

Supervised ML trained on dispositioned hits predicts FP likelihood and routes high-confidence FPs to auto-clear queues (with sampled human QA) or to lower-priority investigation. Vendors increasingly ship these as bolt-on layers (ComplyAdvantage, Hawk AI, Quantexa, NICE Actimize WL-X, Lucinity). All such models require SR 11-7-style validation and ongoing performance monitoring.

Above-the-line / Below-the-line testing

Standard regulatory expectation: periodically sample alerts just above and just below the matching threshold, manually review, confirm the threshold is calibrated correctly. The same discipline used in TM applies to screening — perhaps even more so given the strict-liability standard for sanctions hits.

A regulatory trap

"We were drowning in FPs" is not a defence.

Loosening match thresholds solely to reduce alert volume is the most-common screening finding in OFAC and OFSI enforcement matters. Any change in match parameters must be supported by ATL/BTL testing, documented in the model inventory, approved by model governance, and re-tested afterwards. "We were drowning in FPs" is not a defence.

Chapter XIII · Operations

Investigation & disposition.

The human side of screening. Latency-critical for payment screening, judgement-critical for sanctions and PEP investigations.

§ 13 · What happens after a hit

Where matching ends and judgement begins.

When the matching engine raises a hit, the locus of the work shifts from algorithms to humans. The investigator becomes the bank's final line of defence — and, in payment screening, the source of the SLA that determines whether the bank's wire can leave on time. The structure of the investigation function, the toolkit it uses, and the documentation it produces all determine whether screening succeeds operationally and survives audit.

Investigator hierarchy

L1 — Screening analyst

First touch on every hit. Validates inputs, applies secondary attributes, checks public sources, closes obvious FPs with documented rationale, escalates the rest. Often a 24×7 operation for payment screening.

L2 — Sanctions investigator

Handles non-trivial cases — 50% rule analyses, sectoral sanctions complexity, multi-jurisdictional issues. May liaise with the customer's relationship manager and with internal legal.

L3 — Sanctions / OFAC officer

Senior compliance officer; signs off on blocking decisions; manages OFAC reporting; coordinates with external counsel on licence applications and ambiguous cases; manages regulator-facing communications.

The investigator's toolkit

  • Hit detail panel — input vs list entry side-by-side; algorithm contributions to score; all secondary attributes.
  • List entry detail — full SDN record, program reference, date listed, links to source documents.
  • Customer 360 — KYC, transactions, prior screening history, prior hits and dispositions.
  • UBO & corporate structure — ownership chain visualization, often from Orbis, OpenCorporates, registries.
  • Vessel / aircraft tracking — AIS data, ownership, voyage history (Pole Star, Windward, Lloyd's List Intelligence).
  • Adverse media context — articles and risk-category tagging from vendor data.
  • GenAI assistants — emerging from Lucinity, Saifr, ComplyAdvantage, Hawk AI for narrative drafting, summarization, and source-of-wealth analysis.

Disposition documentation

Disposition rationale — example

What "good" looks like on the page.

"Hit on customer Wang Wei vs SDN WANG, Wei (Cyber2). Name similarity 0.95. DOB mismatch (customer 1987-03-12 vs SDN 1971-08-04); identifier non-overlap (customer PRC ID 4403... vs SDN PRC passport E12...); address mismatch (Shenzhen vs Beijing). Three secondary attributes contradict; identifier non-overlap decisive. Disposition: false positive, customer cleared, pair whitelisted with 12-month review. Investigator: [name]. Reviewer: [name]. Date: [date]."

Reporting outputs

  • OFAC blocking report — within 10 business days of blocking property; annual blocked-property report.
  • OFAC reject report — for transactions rejected (not blocked) due to sanctions.
  • OFSI compliance reporting — including the UK annual review process.
  • SAR/STR — where the hit also raises money-laundering suspicion (e.g. sanctions evasion typology).
  • Voluntary self-disclosure — to OFAC or OFSI where the bank discovers a past breach; materially reduces penalty exposure.

Chapter XV · Landscape & trends

Emerging trends.

The dominant directions of change in screening as of 2024–2026.

§ 15 · Where screening is heading

What is changing, and what is not.

Screening is one of the slower-moving disciplines in financial crime — by design, since predictability and audit-defensibility are part of its core value. Even so, the period 2022–2026 has produced more change than any equivalent span in the previous decade. The themes below are the ones that are reshaping platform architecture, vendor positioning, and regulator expectations in real time.

ISO 20022 fully landed

The SWIFT MT → ISO 20022 migration completed end of 2025 for cross-border payments. Structured payment fields dramatically improve screening accuracy when fully consumed — but require the screening platform to be re-architected to consume XML, parse structured parties, and use Ultimate Debtor / Ultimate Creditor fields properly. Many banks are still in transition; legacy MT consumption co-exists with ISO 20022 in hybrid configurations.

AI for hit reduction with explainability

Machine-learning hit-scoring layers are now mainstream on top of deterministic matching — provided each decision is explainable to a regulator. The deterministic match still fires; ML triages and prioritizes. The black-box-only vendor model is no longer viable.

Perpetual screening

Traditional cycle-based rescreening (annual, every-three-years) is being replaced by perpetual screening: every list update triggers automatic portfolio rescreen, and any change in customer data triggers a fresh screen. Pairs naturally with perpetual KYC.

Crypto wallet screening

Banks with any VASP exposure now embed Chainalysis / TRM / Elliptic into their screening flow — checking whether a wallet involved in a transaction has historical exposure to sanctioned addresses, mixers, or darknet markets. The OFAC sanctioning of Tornado Cash (2022) established that protocols and addresses themselves can be sanctioned, not just legal persons.

Beneficial-ownership transparency expansion

Public UBO registers — UK Companies House PSC register, EU Beneficial Ownership Registers (where accessible post-2022 CJEU ruling), US Corporate Transparency Act FinCEN reporting — make UBO data more available, and raise regulator expectations. Programs that previously took customer-provided UBO at face value increasingly must verify against public registers.

Russia-sanctions expansion and dark-fleet evasion

The 2022 expansion of Russia sanctions, the price-cap regime for Russian oil, and the rise of the "dark fleet" (vessels using flag-of-convenience, AIS spoofing, identity laundering) have made trade-finance screening dramatically more complex. Vessel tracking integration is no longer optional for institutions with shipping exposure.

Sanctions complexity and policy fragmentation

Sectoral and menu-based sanctions (NS-MBS, SSI) and divergence between US/EU/UK programs are creating ever-more granular sanctions logic. Screening platforms increasingly need rule engines beyond pure list-matching — to evaluate not "is this name listed?" but "does this specific activity, with this party, under this program, in this booking entity, breach the restriction menu?"

GenAI for investigation

As in TM and TS, GenAI is being deployed not in the matching layer (explainability constraint) but in the investigation layer: drafting dispositions, summarizing public-source research, structuring source-of-wealth analyses, walking ownership chains. Lucinity, Saifr, ComplyAdvantage, Hawk AI, and SymphonyAI all have shipping or piloted copilots.

Chapter XVI · Landscape & trends

Common pitfalls & challenges.

Patterns repeating across OFAC enforcement orders, OFSI penalties, audit findings, and consulting engagements.

§ 16 · What goes wrong

Ten failure modes the regulator sees most often.

If you read enough OFAC and OFSI enforcement orders, the same ten patterns repeat with unsettling consistency. They are documented here not because any of them is exotic — none of them is — but because the test of a screening program's maturity is whether each of them has been actively engineered out, not merely "considered".

1 · UBO blind spots

Direct screening passes; the SDN owner sits two corporate layers deep, in a different jurisdiction. The 50% rule applies anyway. This pattern is in nearly every modern sanctions enforcement.

2 · List ingestion lag

A new designation goes live on the list at 14:00 local time; the bank's screening engine doesn't refresh until overnight. Hours of unblocked exposure.

3 · Cover-payment stripping

MT202 COV chain is not fully screened; underlying payment parties slip through. Multiple US enforcement actions against major banks turned on this.

4 · Free-text remittance gaps

Sanctioned name appears in the unstructured payment reference field, never reaches the matching engine. ISO 20022 reduces but does not eliminate.

5 · Whitelist contamination

A bad good-guy entry permanently suppresses a real hit. Hygiene failures here are devastating.

6 · Threshold drift

Quietly raising match thresholds to reduce FPs without ATL/BTL evidence. Classic regulator finding.

7 · Transliteration weakness

Cyrillic, Arabic, or Chinese names entered in script; matching engine handles only Latin. Real exposure during the Russia sanctions expansion.

8 · List version vs decision mismatch

When investigating a historical screening pass, the bank cannot reproduce the list as it stood at the time. Auditors and regulators ask for exactly this.

9 · Sectoral-sanctions misapplication

SSI entries treated as full blocks (over-restrictive) or as no-restriction (under-restrictive). Both are findings.

10 · Vessel and trade-finance gaps

No screening of IMO numbers, no integration with vessel-tracking data, no ability to detect AIS-darkness signal on dark-fleet shipping.

Major enforcement actions worth reading the orders on

The cases that define modern screening expectations.

  1. BNP Paribas — DOJ / OFAC, 2014 — USD 8.9bn — Sudan/Iran/Cuba payment stripping. The largest single sanctions penalty in history; defining case for cover-payment stripping risk.
  2. Standard Chartered — NYDFS / OFAC, 2012 and 2019 — Iran-related sanctions; second order arose from inadequate remediation post first order.
  3. Credit Suisse — 2009; Iran/Sudan stripping; ~USD 536m.
  4. Lloyds TSB — 2009; Iran/Sudan/Libya stripping; ~USD 350m. Earliest of the modern stripping cases.
  5. ING Bank — 2012; Cuba/Iran stripping; ~USD 619m.
  6. Société Générale — 2018; Cuba/Iran/Libya/Sudan; ~USD 1.34bn.
  7. Standard Chartered — NYDFS / OFAC, 2019 — Iran-related sanctions follow-up.
  8. British Arab Commercial Bank — OFAC, 2019; Sudan-related.
  9. Binance — DOJ / OFAC / FinCEN / CFTC, 2023 — USD 4.3bn — sanctions and AML failures in crypto; the largest crypto-side action and a defining case for digital-asset sanctions exposure.
  10. OFAC Tornado Cash designation, 2022 — the precedent for sanctioning a protocol / smart-contract address rather than a legal person.
  11. BAE Systems / NCA / SFO regimes — UK and US guidance on systemic sanctions and bribery overlaps.

Suggested reading order

Eighty percent of the working knowledge in four documents.

Start with the OFAC Framework for Compliance Commitments (concise; sets the conceptual baseline), the Wolfsberg Guidance on Sanctions Screening (industry view), the OFAC 50% Rule guidance (the single most consequential interpretive principle), then read the BNP Paribas 2014 settlement agreement and OFAC Tornado Cash 2022 for the breadth of what is being enforced. That sequence gives you 80% of the working knowledge for any vendor demo or consulting deck on screening.
