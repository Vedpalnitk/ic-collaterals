Chapter I · Foundations

Introduction & purpose.

What transaction monitoring is, why it exists, and where it sits inside the broader financial-crime control framework.

§ 01 · Foundations

From raw payments to reasonable suspicion.

Transaction monitoring (TM) is the continuous, automated review of customer activity — deposits, withdrawals, wires, ACH, card, securities trades, journal entries, internal book transfers — against pre-defined detection logic to identify activity that may indicate money laundering, terrorist financing, sanctions evasion, fraud, or market abuse. It is a regulatory obligation for virtually every regulated financial institution (FI) in the world and is one of the three pillars of an AML program, alongside Customer Due Diligence (CDD/KYC) and Sanctions Screening.

The TM control does not try to prove a crime. It tries to detect a reasonable suspicion that warrants human investigation and, if appropriate, a regulatory filing (a Suspicious Activity Report in the US, a Suspicious Transaction Report in most other jurisdictions). The output of TM is therefore an alert — not a conclusion.

Mental model

A two-stage funnel.

Think of TM as a two-stage funnel. Stage 1 is detection logic (rules, scenarios, models) that converts raw transactions into alerts. Stage 2 is human investigation that converts alerts into either cleared, escalated, or reported outcomes. Most regulatory criticism — and most vendor competition — targets the conversion ratios between these stages.

Why it exists

  • Legal obligation. The Bank Secrecy Act (1970) in the US, and equivalents globally, require regulated FIs to report suspicious activity. Failure to monitor is a violation in itself — independent of whether laundering actually occurred.
  • Societal harm. The UN Office on Drugs and Crime estimates global money laundering at 2–5% of world GDP annually — roughly USD 800bn–2tn. TM is the front-line technical control.
  • Bank protection. Effective TM protects FIs from being used as conduits for proceeds of crime — which carries fines (multi-billion-dollar in extreme cases), consent orders, and reputational loss.

Scope & adjacent controls

TM does not operate in isolation. The full financial-crime stack typically includes:

Chapter II · Foundations

Regulatory landscape.

The rules that mandate transaction monitoring — international standards, regional frameworks, national regulators, and the reporting regimes they enforce.

§ 02 · Why this exists

FATF sets the floor; national regulators do the enforcing.

International standards

The global AML/CFT framework is set by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF), an inter-governmental body whose 40 Recommendations are the universal reference for national laws. FATF doesn't regulate FIs directly — it evaluates countries. But every national regulator implements FATF requirements, so understanding the 40 Recommendations gives you the conceptual ground floor for every jurisdiction.

Other international bodies worth knowing:

  • Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) — issues supervisory guidance on sound AML/CFT risk management for banks.
  • Wolfsberg Group — a private association of 12 global banks that publishes industry guidance on KYC, correspondent banking, payment transparency, and the use of AI in financial crime. Often cited in regulatory examinations.
  • Egmont Group — global network of Financial Intelligence Units (FIUs); coordinates cross-border SAR exchange.

The reporting obligations TM feeds

SAR / STR

Suspicious Activity Report (US) / Suspicious Transaction Report (rest of world). Filed when an FI has a reasonable suspicion that activity relates to money laundering, terrorist financing, or other predicate offences. Typically due within 30 days of detection in the US; 24 hours to a few days in many other jurisdictions. The FI cannot tip off the customer.

CTR

Currency Transaction Report (US). Mandatory filing for any cash transaction exceeding USD 10,000 in a single business day per customer. Not a suspicion-based report — it is mechanical and threshold-driven. Drives the structuring typology (see §7).

Other

Cross-border wire reports (FBAR, FATCA, CRS), monetary instrument logs (≥ USD 3,000), Section 314(a)/(b) information-sharing requests in the US, and country-specific large-value transaction reports (e.g. AUSTRAC's TTR threshold of AUD 10,000).

Why this matters for TM design

Detection logic must cover the typologies the regulator expects you to cover. Examiners will ask: "Show us your scenarios for structuring. For rapid movement of funds. For high-risk geography activity. For PEP exposure. For pass-through behavior." If a typology is documented in FATF or FinCEN guidance and your platform does not detect it, the gap is a finding. Most enforcement actions over the last decade — HSBC 2012, Standard Chartered 2012/2019, Danske Bank 2018, Westpac 2020, TD Bank 2024 — turned on missing or mis-tuned scenarios, not on missing CDD.

Chapter III · Foundations

Three stages of money laundering.

The canonical model used in every FATF document and AML training. Different TM scenarios target different stages.

§ 03 · The problem TM is detecting

Placement, layering, integration.

STAGE 01

Placement

Illicit cash enters the financial system — cash deposits, prepaid cards, money services businesses, casinos, real-estate down-payments. Most vulnerable stage for the launderer; most-detectable stage for the bank because cash is involved. Targeted by: structuring, cash-intensive business, smurfing scenarios.

STAGE 02

Layering

Funds are moved through complex sequences of transfers to obscure origin — wire chains, shell companies, trade-based laundering, securities trades, cross-border flows, crypto on/off-ramps. Targeted by: rapid movement of funds, pass-through, journal entries, network/graph scenarios, offsetting trades.

STAGE 03

Integration

Now-cleaned funds re-enter the legitimate economy as apparently legal income — real estate, luxury goods, business investments, loans repaid from offshore accounts. Hardest to detect because the trail is broken. Targeted by: anticipatory profiling, change-in-behavior, source-of-wealth review scenarios.

Note

Terrorist financing reverses the model.

Terrorist financing (TF) often reverses the model: small amounts of legitimate money are funnelled toward an illicit end. Classic TF typologies — donations to charities of concern, hawala-style transfers, small repeated wires — therefore look nothing like classical ML and require their own scenarios.

Chapter V · Lifecycle & method

Core concepts & vocabulary.

The fifteen-or-so concepts you'll see in every regulatory exam, vendor pitch, and consulting deck. Internalize these before reading scenarios.

§ 05 · Vocabulary you need

Customer-side and detection-side language.

Customer-side concepts

KYC (Know Your Customer)
The process of identifying, verifying, and risk-rating a customer at onboarding and on an ongoing basis.
CDD (Customer Due Diligence)
Same as KYC but emphasized as a continuous process, not a one-off event. Required by FATF Rec. 10.
EDD (Enhanced Due Diligence)
Heightened CDD applied to higher-risk customers — PEPs, customers in high-risk geographies, complex ownership structures, correspondent banking relationships. Includes source-of-wealth and source-of-funds analysis.
UBO (Ultimate Beneficial Owner)
The natural person who ultimately owns or controls a legal entity (typically defined as ≥25% ownership, but stricter in some jurisdictions). The lynchpin for detecting shell-company misuse.
PEP (Politically Exposed Person)
Individuals entrusted with prominent public functions and their close associates/family. Not inherently suspicious, but warrant EDD due to elevated bribery / corruption / sanctions risk.
CRR (Customer Risk Rating)
A composite score (typically L / M / H) used to drive monitoring intensity, threshold calibration, and refresh cadence. Inputs: customer type, geography, product, channel, history.
Peer group / segmentation
Customers grouped by similar risk profile and activity expectation, so that scenarios can compare each customer to "people like them" rather than to a global average. Critical for behavioral and anomaly-based detection.

Detection-side concepts

Typology
A pattern of behavior associated with a known laundering or financing technique (e.g. structuring, funnel accounts, trade-based laundering). Typologies are catalogued by FATF, Wolfsberg, FinCEN, etc.
Scenario
An implemented detection logic that targets one or more typologies. Most TM platforms ship with 25–100 out-of-the-box scenarios that institutions tune to their data.
Rule
The most granular piece of detection logic — a single condition or threshold ("cash deposit > $9,500 within 24 hours of a wire out"). Scenarios are composed of rules.
Threshold
A numeric value that controls when a scenario fires. The most-tuned parameter in any TM platform — too low and you drown in false positives, too high and you miss real activity.
Alert
The output of detection logic. A package of: customer ID, scenario name, time period, transaction set, a calculated value, and any risk attributes. Becomes a case in the investigation workflow.
False positive (FP) / True positive (TP)
An alert that, after investigation, turns out not to be (FP) or to be (TP) genuinely suspicious. In most production TM platforms, the FP rate runs at 90–99%. Reducing FP rate without reducing TP recall is the central engineering problem.
SAR conversion rate
The proportion of alerts that result in a filed SAR. Industry-typical figures sit at 1–5%.
Above-the-line / Below-the-line (ATL / BTL) testing
A regulatory expectation: periodically test the scenarios just above (ATL) and just below (BTL) the threshold to confirm the threshold is set at the right point. Above-the-line should be productive; below-the-line should be clean of obvious typology activity.
Disposition
The investigator's recorded decision on an alert — typically "Closed — No SAR", "Closed — SAR Filed", "Escalated", or "Continue Monitoring". The disposition history is gold-dust for model tuning and ML.
Model risk / Model governance
The regulatory framework (US: SR 11-7) requiring documented validation, ongoing performance monitoring, and independent challenge for every model — including TM scenarios. Driven by the Model Risk Management function inside the bank.

Chapter VI · Lifecycle & method

Detection approaches.

The six families of techniques that produce TM alerts today, ordered roughly by adoption maturity. Most production platforms blend several.

§ 06 · How detection is built

Rules to ML, with graph in the middle.

1 · Rules / Threshold-based detection

The oldest and still-most-common approach. A scenario fires when one or more numeric conditions are met (e.g. cash_in_24h >= $9,500 AND wire_out_72h >= $9,000 AND wire_dest_country IN high_risk_list). Outputs are deterministic, auditable, and easy to explain to a regulator — but rigid, prone to false positives, and easily gamed by anyone who learns the threshold.

2 · Statistical / scenario-based detection

Adds statistical aggregation to rules: standard-deviation bands, ratio analysis, peer-group comparisons, time-windowed deltas. The Oracle/Mantas scenario library is the canonical example — most banks operate on this approach today, often with hundreds of tuned scenarios.

3 · Behavioral profiling

Establishes a per-customer baseline of expected activity (volume, geography, counterparties, channels) from KYC and historical data, then alerts on material deviation. Sometimes called anticipatory profiling. Strong for catching evolving behavior; weak when the baseline itself is compromised (e.g. customer was a mule from day one).

4 · Network / Graph analytics

Treats parties, accounts, devices, IPs, addresses, and transactions as nodes-and-edges in a graph, and runs multi-hop traversals or community-detection algorithms to surface schemes that would be invisible to single-account scenarios. Mule networks, hub-and-spoke structuring, and round-tripping are the canonical use cases. Vendors: Quantexa, TigerGraph, Neo4j, SymphonyAI Sensa. Now the dominant differentiator in new TM platform builds.

5 · Machine Learning — Supervised

Trains models on labelled historical alerts (TP vs FP, or SAR-filed vs not) to predict alert quality ("alert risk scoring"), to suppress likely-FPs, or to triage cases. Common architectures: gradient-boosted trees, neural nets, graph neural nets. Heavily regulated under model-risk frameworks — requires explainability, validation, and ongoing performance monitoring. Watch for the label leakage problem: yesterday's false negatives don't appear in your training labels.

6 · Machine Learning — Unsupervised & anomaly detection

Used when labels are scarce: isolation forests, autoencoders, clustering, density-based anomaly detection. Surfaces customers or transactions that don't resemble their peer group or their own history. Strong for novel typologies; weak on explainability. Often deployed as a scenario complement, not a replacement.

In practice

How these combine in production.

A mature TM stack typically runs (a) a base layer of statistical/threshold scenarios for regulatory coverage, (b) a graph layer for network and multi-hop typologies, (c) a behavioral profiling layer for change-detection, and (d) an ML scoring layer on top of all generated alerts to drive risk-based investigator prioritization. Each layer's output is auditable and tied to a documented typology.

Chapter VII · Typologies

Scenarios & typologies.

The catalogue of laundering / financing patterns most TM programs must cover. Each card carries the typology, the detection pattern that targets it, and the primary risk it addresses. Priority bars reflect typical regulatory and impact weighting.

§ 07 · The work itself

What a regulator expects you to detect.

Placement-stage scenarios

Structuring

CTR threshold evasion
P1
Typology
Cash deposits or withdrawals deliberately broken below the USD 10,000 CTR threshold (or USD 3,000 monetary-instrument log threshold) to avoid reporting.
Detection
Same-customer, same-day aggregation across branches and account numbers; cluster on amounts just below threshold bands ($9,000–$9,999, $4,500–$4,999).
Primary risk
31 USC §5324 BSA reporting evasion; placement of illicit cash.

Microstructuring (Smurfing)

Coordinated multi-actor structuring
P1
Typology
Multiple individuals ("smurfs") each depositing small amounts that aggregate to a large sum at a common beneficiary. Defeats single-customer aggregation.
Detection
Many-to-one beneficiary detection across distinct depositors; link via shared device, IP, branch, address, or coordinated timing windows.
Primary risk
Coordinated placement of illicit cash; mule recruitment networks.

Cash-intensive Business Misuse

Front-business laundering
P2
Typology
Legitimate cash-handling business (laundromat, car wash, restaurant) used to commingle illicit cash with real revenue.
Detection
Cash deposits inconsistent with declared business turnover; volume/seasonality mismatches; comparison to industry peer benchmarks.
Primary risk
Classic placement laundering; revenue inflation for tax fraud.

Funnel Accounts

Multi-state cash collection
P3
Typology
Cash deposited in multiple geographies into a single account and withdrawn from a single distant location — classic for narcotics proceeds and human trafficking.
Detection
Geographic dispersion of cash deposits over short windows; deposit-state vs withdrawal-state mismatch; high velocity through a single low-balance account.
Primary risk
Drug trafficking proceeds; human trafficking proceeds.

Layering-stage scenarios

Rapid Movement of Funds

Pass-through / funnel activity
P1
Typology
Funds in and out within a short window with no economic purpose — a classic layering signature.
Detection
Time-windowed in/out edge analysis at the account level; net position ≈ 0 with high throughput; low day-end balance vs high daily flow.
Primary risk
Layering; pass-through laundering through a custodial account.

Network of Accounts / Customers

Mule rings & coordinated schemes
P1
Typology
Interconnected accounts sharing identifiers (address, phone, IP, device) acting in concert despite no documented relationship.
Detection
Graph community detection on shared-attribute and transaction edges; hub-and-spoke and round-tripping motifs.
Primary risk
Mule networks; organized fraud and laundering rings.

External Pattern of Funds Transfer

Customer ↔ external entity
P3
Typology
Unusual flows to/from an external counterparty — incomplete originator/beneficiary data, repetitive transfers via different accounts to the same destination.
Detection
Multi-hop traversal across wire/ACH edges; aggregate by external counterparty across all customer accounts.
Primary risk
Layering; sanctions evasion via intermediaries.

Journal Entries — Unrelated Accounts

Internal transfers without purpose
P4
Typology
Internal book transfers between accounts with no documented relationship or economic rationale.
Detection
Internal-transfer edges enriched with CDD-relationship signal; flag where graph distance is high but flow is large.
Primary risk
Internal layering; insider fraud.

Movement Without Trade Purpose

Conduit / shell-style flows
P3
Typology
Transactions inconsistent with stated business — shell-style accounts acting as conduits with no commercial substance.
Detection
Business-profile mismatch on the customer node combined with high pass-through ratio across counterparty edges.
Primary risk
Shell company misuse; tax evasion; sanctions evasion.

Trade-Based Money Laundering (TBML)

Over/under-invoicing in trade finance
P3
Typology
Mis-pricing, phantom shipments, or multiple-invoicing of goods to move value across borders disguised as legitimate trade.
Detection
Price-vs-market-reference deviation; container-volume vs invoice-value mismatch; counterparty country vs commodity type mismatch.
Primary risk
Cross-border laundering; sanctions evasion; trade fraud. Estimated to be one of the largest ML channels globally.

Offsetting Trades

Wash / mirror / pre-arranged trades
P3
Typology
Trades that cancel out across accounts or jurisdictions — value transfer disguised as market activity (the technique exposed by Deutsche Bank's Russian mirror-trade scandal).
Detection
Cross-account trade-pair detection on instrument + time + price; UBO-clustered traversal across related accounts.
Primary risk
Cross-border value transfer; market manipulation.

Currency Exchange — Multi-Listed

Cross-market value transfer
P3
Typology
Buying/selling ADRs, GDRs, or dual-listed shares across markets and currencies primarily to convert currency or move value.
Detection
Link instrument nodes across exchanges; correlate cross-currency leg pairs by UBO and time.
Primary risk
Value transfer; capital control evasion.

Integration-stage & behavioral scenarios

Anticipatory Profiling

Deviation from expected behavior
P2
Typology
Activity diverging from the CDD-derived baseline of expected volume, geography, products, or counterparties.
Detection
Per-customer baseline computed on graph-aggregated features; alert on statistical drift versus baseline + peer group.
Primary risk
Novel typologies; evolving risk; integration-stage activity.

Significant Change in Behavior

Unexplained pattern shift
P2
Typology
Material, unexplained shift in volume, geography, product mix, or interaction channel versus history.
Detection
Temporal feature comparison on the customer node — current window vs trailing baseline, peer-group adjusted.
Primary risk
Account compromise; evolving misuse; mule recruitment after a clean onboarding period.

High-Risk Transaction Monitoring

Entity & counterparty
P4
Typology
Scrutiny of activity by or with parties carrying elevated ML/TF risk — high-risk geos, PEPs, opaque ownership, adverse media.
Detection
Risk-attributed nodes for parties + jurisdictions; alert on edges that cross from low-risk to high-risk subgraphs.
Primary risk
Sanctions exposure; PEP misuse; corruption proceeds.

Dormant Account Reactivation

Unused account suddenly active
P3
Typology
An account that has been inactive for months or years suddenly shows large or rapid activity — a hallmark of account takeover, identity theft, or mule reactivation.
Detection
Dormancy-window state machine on account; alert on first transaction above peer-threshold after defined inactivity period.
Primary risk
Account takeover; mule reactivation; identity fraud.

Specialized & emerging scenarios

Cuckoo Smurfing

Hijacking legitimate remittances
P3
Typology
Launderer's cash is paid into the account of an unwitting third party who was expecting a legitimate inbound remittance, while the launderer collects the real overseas funds in cash. Particularly seen in Australian and South Asian corridors.
Detection
Inbound cash deposit timing matches an expected international remittance; deposit channel/branch does not match remitter profile.
Primary risk
Cross-border laundering; victim is often unaware until investigated.

Black-Market Peso Exchange

Parallel-market currency conversion
P3
Typology
Drug proceeds in USD are sold to Latin-American businesses needing dollars, in exchange for pesos delivered in country — laundering and currency conversion in one move.
Detection
US-dollar payments from unconnected US sources to Latin-American businesses inconsistent with declared trade flow.
Primary risk
Drug-trafficking proceeds laundering; trade-finance abuse.

Crypto On/Off-Ramp Activity

Bank ↔ VASP flows
P2
Typology
Frequent, structured, or large flows between bank accounts and virtual-asset service providers (VASPs) — exchanges, OTC desks, mixers, peer-to-peer markets — used as on/off-ramps in layering.
Detection
VASP-counterparty edge enrichment; pair with blockchain-analytics signal (Chainalysis, TRM, Elliptic) to identify sanctioned or high-risk wallets at the other end.
Primary risk
Crypto-enabled laundering; sanctions evasion via privacy tools.

APP Scam / Authorized Push Payment Fraud

Social-engineered transfers
P2
Typology
Customer is socially engineered into authorizing a payment to a fraudster (romance scam, investment scam, impersonation). The transaction looks legitimate at the rail because the customer authorized it.
Detection
Behavioral biometrics (hesitation, navigation patterns); new payee + large amount + uncharacteristic destination; mule-account intelligence at the beneficiary side.
Primary risk
Consumer harm; reimbursement liability under regimes like the UK PSR mandatory reimbursement scheme (2024).

Terrorist Financing Indicators

Low-value, high-purpose flows
P3
Typology
Small, repeated transfers to or from individuals/charities in conflict-zone jurisdictions; donations clustering around known event dates; flows to NGO-fronts under sanction.
Detection
Geography + counterparty + amount fingerprinting against FATF/national TF typology lists; time-clustering on known dates.
Primary risk
Terrorist financing — criminally severe and the most regulator-sensitive of all typologies.

Round-Tripping

Funds return via foreign loop
P3
Typology
Funds leave the country via a customer/related entity and return as foreign direct investment or "loan" from an offshore vehicle — disguising the source and gaining tax/regulatory benefit.
Detection
Outbound + inbound leg pairing through related parties; offshore-jurisdiction concentration; corporate-structure overlap.
Primary risk
Tax evasion; concealment of beneficial ownership; capital control evasion.

Priority code

How to read the P1–P4 pills.

P1 highest regulatory weight and operational impact  ·  P2 high  ·  P3 medium  ·  P4 coverage / context. Specific weighting will vary by institution, regulator, and product mix.

Chapter VIII · Typologies

Worked scenario walkthroughs.

Three concrete examples showing how typology maps to detection logic, what an alert looks like, and how the investigator should think about it.

§ 08 · From signal to alert

Three traces, three lessons in detection.

Walkthrough 01 · Structuring

Customer Maria deposits cash at three branches in one week

Setup. Maria has a personal checking account, low-risk CRR, declared occupation "retail clerk", typical monthly inflow ~$3,000.

Activity over 5 business days:

  1. Monday — $9,500 cash deposit at Branch A
  2. Wednesday — $9,200 cash deposit at Branch B
  3. Thursday — $9,800 cash deposit at Branch C
  4. Friday — $9,300 cash deposit at Branch A

Detection logic that fires. Scenario: Same-customer cash deposit aggregation. Aggregates all cash deposits across branches within a 7-day rolling window, alerts if total ≥ 2× declared monthly inflow AND average individual deposit is > 85% of the $10,000 CTR threshold AND no CTRs were triggered.

Why it works. Each individual deposit was below the CTR threshold (so no automatic CTR), but the aggregation pattern is a textbook smurf signature: every amount in the 90–98% band of the threshold, multiple branches (to defeat single-teller recognition), and dramatically inconsistent with the declared income profile. The investigator should pull cash-source documentation, request EDD, and consider whether a SAR is appropriate even before EDD completes.
Walkthrough 02 · Rapid movement of funds

The pass-through account — $0 balance, $4M throughput

Setup. ABC Trading Ltd, business account, declared activity "import-export consultancy", typical monthly turnover ~$200k.

Activity over 30 days:

  1. 28 inbound wires totaling $4.1M, from 11 distinct counterparties across 6 jurisdictions
  2. 26 outbound wires totaling $4.08M, to 9 distinct counterparties across 5 jurisdictions
  3. Day-end balance never exceeded $30k; ended the month at $18k
  4. No commercial documentation (invoices, bills of lading) referenced in payment messages

Detection logic that fires. Scenario: High-turnover / low-balance pass-through. Alerts when (monthly throughput / average daily balance) exceeds peer-group threshold AND net position is within ±5% of zero AND payment-message commercial reference is empty.

Why it works. A genuine consultancy holds working capital. A pass-through layering vehicle does not — funds enter, route, exit, and the account never "rests". The huge throughput-to-balance ratio is the smoking gun, and the missing trade documentation rules out trade-finance explanations. A graph view of counterparties typically reveals a hub-and-spoke topology with shared UBO at the spoke ends.
Walkthrough 03 · Network of accounts (graph)

Mule ring detected via shared device fingerprint

Setup. Bank operates a graph layer enriched with login device fingerprints, IPs, and shared static identifiers (phone, address).

Signal extracted by graph traversal:

  1. 17 personal accounts, opened across 4 months at 6 branches, with no documented relationship in KYC
  2. All 17 logged in from the same 3 mobile devices (device-fingerprint match)
  3. Each account receives a single inbound P2P transfer of $1,800–$2,300 within 24 hours of opening
  4. Each account then sends a single outbound transfer to one of two destination accounts within 48 hours
  5. Average lifetime activity per account: 3 transactions, ~$2,000 net throughput, then dormant

Detection logic that fires. Graph community-detection scenario alerts when ≥ 5 accounts share a device fingerprint AND share no KYC-documented relationship AND each account exhibits a single high-velocity in/out pattern AND outbound destinations cluster to ≤ 3 endpoints.

Why it works. No single account would have triggered a rules-based scenario — each one transacted small amounts, briefly. The signal lives at the network level, not the account level. This is the canonical case where graph analytics finds patterns that flat-rule scenarios cannot, and why most new TM builds in 2024–2026 emphasize a graph layer.

Chapter IX · Operations

Fraud monitoring vs AML TM.

Often run on separate platforms, separate teams, and separate regulatory drivers — but the underlying transaction data is the same, and convergence is the dominant industry trend.

§ 09 · Adjacent but different

Same data, different question.

Why convergence is happening

Three forces are pushing fraud and AML TM together. (1) The same transaction often has both signals — APP scam victims are paying mules, and the mule side is an AML problem. (2) Customer-level risk cuts across both — a mule account is risky for fraud detection on inbound flows and risky for AML on outbound flows. (3) Cost. Banks operate two separate stacks today; consolidating yields shared data, shared modeling, and reduced TCO. Vendors like Featurespace, SymphonyAI Sensa, and Quantexa are explicitly positioning as fraud + AML platforms.

Chapter X · Operations

Mitigation & tuning.

The repertoire of methods used to reduce false positives, raise true-positive recall, and stay defensible in front of a regulator. Every TM program is in a permanent tuning loop on these levers.

§ 10 · Making TM actually work

The permanent tuning loop.

Threshold tuning

Scenarios fire on numeric thresholds (amount, count, ratio, time window). Tuning means choosing thresholds that are sensitive enough to catch real activity but specific enough to suppress noise. The regulator-accepted method is Above-the-Line / Below-the-Line (ATL/BTL) testing: take a sample of activity just above and just below the threshold, manually review, and confirm the threshold sits at the right productive-vs-noise transition.

Segmentation

Apply different thresholds to different customer segments based on expected behavior. A "small-business retail" segment will tolerate higher cash-deposit thresholds than a "personal-account low-income" segment. Without segmentation, you either over-alert on retail or under-alert on small business.

Suppression and hibernation

Suppression rules — automatic close-out of alert patterns the institution has previously dispositioned as benign (e.g. "scheduled monthly payroll inbound > threshold"). Alert hibernation — temporarily suspend a scenario for a customer after a certain number of recent investigated-and-cleared alerts. Both must be governed: a regulator will ask how suppression is approved, reviewed, and unwound.

Risk-based prioritization

Score every alert by predicted suspicion likelihood, customer risk, amount, and counterparty risk; route high-score alerts to senior investigators and let low-score alerts auto-close with sampling. ML alert triage is the most common production application of supervised learning in TM today.

Coverage assessment vs typology library

Map every implemented scenario to one or more FATF / FinCEN / national typologies. If a typology has no scenario, that gap is documented, risk-rated, and remediated on a plan. This is the single most common regulator-facing artifact in TM model risk management.

Lookbacks

When a coverage gap is discovered or a scenario is added, run it historically over 12–24 months of past transactions to find activity that would have been alerted. Lookbacks are sometimes mandated by consent orders (the famous HSBC and Standard Chartered lookbacks ran into tens of millions of transactions and hundreds of millions of dollars in cost).

Model validation & governance

Under model-risk frameworks (US: SR 11-7), every scenario and model must be independently validated at implementation and re-validated periodically. Validation tests: design soundness, data quality, threshold appropriateness, performance vs benchmark, ongoing monitoring. The validation function reports to the Chief Risk Officer and is structurally independent from development.

Feedback loop from disposition

The investigator's disposition (cleared / SAR-filed) is the highest-quality label data the institution will ever produce. Feed it back into ML triage, scenario tuning, threshold review, and analyst training. Programs without this loop tune scenarios on the basis of opinion rather than evidence.

Parallel running ("shadow alerts")

When replacing a scenario or migrating from one platform to another (e.g. Mantas → in-house graph platform), run the new logic in parallel with the legacy for 4–12 weeks. Compare alerts case-by-case. A new scenario should achieve ≥ 100% recall of legacy true positives before being allowed to retire the legacy scenario.

A regulatory trap

"We were drowning in alerts" is not a defence.

Tuning that raises thresholds (and so reduces alert volume) without documented justification is one of the most-common findings in regulatory exams. Any threshold movement must be supported by ATL/BTL testing, documented in the model inventory, and approved by model governance. "We were drowning in alerts" is not a defensible reason.

Chapter XI · Operations

Investigation & case management.

The human side of TM. Most of the cost in a TM program is in this stage, not in detection.

§ 11 · What happens after an alert

L1, L2, MLRO.

Investigator hierarchy

L1 — Triage analyst

First touch on every alert. Validates the data, checks recent history, reviews customer profile, runs basic web/adverse-media checks. Most L1 alerts close at this stage with a documented rationale. Productivity often measured in alerts/day (50–150 typical).

L2 — Investigator

Handles escalations from L1. Performs deep-dive: full transaction history, EDD review, source-of-funds analysis, counterparty review, 314(b) requests where applicable. Often produces the draft SAR narrative.

L3 — SAR officer / MLRO

Senior officer (in many jurisdictions, the Money Laundering Reporting Officer) who reviews and approves SAR filings, manages regulator relationships, owns program-level metrics, and signs off on policy.

The investigator's toolkit

  • Case management system — single workspace for the alert, customer, transactions, prior history, and disposition record. Common platforms: Actimize, Pega, Oracle FCCM Case Manager, Bottomline, Verafin, Lucinity Studio.
  • Investigator workbench / "360 view" — pulls KYC, transactions, sanctions hits, prior alerts, related parties into one screen.
  • Graph view — visualizes the customer's network of counterparties, shared identifiers, and prior alert history. Most powerful when the underlying detection layer is also graph-native.
  • Adverse media & web search — increasingly automated via vendors like ComplyAdvantage, Refinitiv World-Check, Dow Jones, LexisNexis Bridger.
  • 314(b) channel (US-specific) — voluntary information-sharing between FIs on suspected ML/TF. Hugely under-used.
  • GenAI assistants — emerging in 2024–2026 from vendors like Lucinity, Hawk AI, SAS, drafting narratives, summarizing customer history, surfacing relevant typologies.

What a good disposition looks like

Disposition rationale — example

The pattern of a defensible close-out.

"Alert triggered by Same-Customer Cash Deposit Aggregation scenario covering 5 deposits totaling $36,700 over 7 days. Customer is a self-employed contractor; deposits align with three documented invoices provided on EDD request (attached). Source of funds verified against contractor's bank statements from prior payer. No structuring intent indicated — individual deposits range $4,800 to $8,500 with no clustering near threshold band. Chapter XII · Operations

SAR / CTR / STR reporting.

The three primary forms that flow from a TM program into the financial intelligence ecosystem, and the rules that govern each.

§ 12 · The regulatory outputs

Suspicion-based and threshold-based.

SAR / STR — the suspicion-based filing

In the US, a Suspicious Activity Report is filed with FinCEN within 30 calendar days of detecting suspicious activity (extendable to 60 days if no suspect has been identified). The filing is strictly confidential — tipping off the customer is itself a criminal offense. In the UK, the equivalent is a Suspicious Activity Report filed with the NCA via UKFIU; in most other jurisdictions, the equivalent is a Suspicious Transaction Report (STR) filed with the national FIU. Filing thresholds and timing vary, but the principle — reasonable suspicion, confidential, timely — is universal.

The quality of the SAR narrative matters enormously. Regulators have been increasingly explicit that "checkbox" SARs with no useful narrative do not satisfy the obligation. A good SAR narrative explains who, what, when, where, why, and how, with specific transaction details and the basis of suspicion.

CTR — the threshold-based filing

In the US, a Currency Transaction Report must be filed for any cash transaction (or aggregated same-day transactions by/for one customer) exceeding USD 10,000. CTRs are not suspicion-based — they are mechanical. The threshold is the entire reason structuring is a typology: a launderer who breaks deposits below $10,000 is specifically trying to avoid CTR generation.

Other notable reports

  • Section 314(a) requests — FinCEN-issued requests asking FIs to search records for named subjects in active investigations. Mandatory response.
  • Section 314(b) sharing — voluntary inter-FI sharing on suspected ML/TF subjects, with a safe harbor from liability.
  • FBAR / FATCA / CRS — cross-border account and tax-information reporting (not strictly TM but draws on the same data).
  • OFAC blocking reports — when a sanctions hit blocks a payment, the FI files within 10 days.
  • Country-specific large-value transaction reports — e.g. AUSTRAC TTR (AUD 10,000), FINTRAC LCTR (CAD 10,000), India CTR/CCR.

Choosing a vendor

Hybrid is the default.

The right vendor depends on (a) bank size and complexity, (b) existing tech stack and data architecture, (c) regulatory geography, (d) build vs buy philosophy. Most tier-1 banks today run a hybrid: a legacy enterprise platform (Mantas/Actimize/SAS) for regulatory coverage, plus a modern graph or AI layer (Quantexa/SymphonyAI/in-house) for differentiated detection. Pure rip-and-replace migrations are rare and expensive; most programs migrate scenario-by-scenario under parallel running.

Chapter XIV · Ecosystem & pitfalls

Emerging trends.

The dominant directions of change in TM as of 2024–2026. None of these are speculative — all are visible in regulatory papers, vendor roadmaps, and tier-1 bank programs today.

§ 14 · Where TM is heading

Graph, ML, federation, real-time.

Graph & network analytics as a first-class layer

Single-account scenarios miss schemes that live at the network level. Graph-native TM (TigerGraph, Neo4j, Quantexa) is now the default for new builds, with statistical scenarios still doing the regulatory-coverage work underneath. Mule networks, hub-and-spoke layering, and round-tripping are the canonical wins.

Machine learning, explainability, and model governance

ML in TM is increasingly mainstream — but only with full explainability and SR 11-7-style validation. Black-box vendor models are no longer defensible. SHAP values, decision trees on top of neural networks, and rule-extraction from ML models are common patterns.

Federated learning & consortium models

Most laundering crosses bank boundaries — but no single bank sees the full network. Federated models (where banks contribute encrypted signals to a shared model without sharing raw data) and regulator-sponsored consortium models (e.g. the UK's Future of Payments work, MAS's COSMIC platform in Singapore) are the next frontier.

Perpetual KYC (pKYC)

Traditional KYC refresh cycles (1/3/5 years by risk tier) are too slow. Perpetual KYC uses ongoing data signals — transaction patterns, public information, screening hits — to refresh customer risk continuously. Strong implication for TM: scenarios fire on a customer with a always-current risk rating rather than a stale one.

Real-time TM

Increasing share of payments moves on real-time rails (FedNow, FPS, UPI, Pix, SEPA Instant). Batch-overnight TM is too slow to prevent fund movement, so banks are pushing detection logic upstream to pre-authorization windows of milliseconds — converging with fraud monitoring architecturally.

Generative AI for investigation

GenAI is being deployed not in detection (where explainability is a hard constraint) but in investigator productivity: drafting SAR narratives, summarizing customer history, surfacing relevant typologies, generating EDD checklists. Lucinity, SAS, Hawk AI, and SymphonyAI all have announced or shipping GenAI copilots.

Crypto, stablecoins, and VASP integration

Bank exposure to virtual-asset service providers is growing — both as customers and counterparties. Integrating blockchain analytics (Chainalysis, TRM, Elliptic) into bank-side TM lets the bank evaluate the off-chain risk of an on-chain counterparty's wallet history.

EU AML Authority (AMLA)

From 2028, AMLA will directly supervise a designated set of cross-border EU FIs and will set the EU-wide single rulebook. This will materially reshape TM practice in Europe — moving away from fragmented national implementation toward a single, examiner-uniform standard.

Chapter XV · Ecosystem & pitfalls

Common pitfalls & challenges.

Patterns visible across enforcement actions, audit findings, and consulting engagements. Most are operational, not technical.

§ 15 · What goes wrong

Eight failure modes you will recognise.

1 · Drowning in false positives

FP rates of 95–99% are common. Investigator capacity becomes the binding constraint; real signals get lost in the noise; threshold-tuning becomes a continuous fire-fight. Mitigation: ML triage, segmentation, ATL/BTL discipline, suppression governance.

2 · Coverage gaps to typology library

A typology exists in FATF guidance, no scenario implements it. The gap becomes a regulator finding, then a consent order, then a multi-year remediation. Mitigation: live typology-to-scenario coverage map, quarterly review.

3 · Data quality & fragmentation

Wires in one system, deposits in another, customer profiles in a third, no unified ID. Scenarios miss multi-system patterns. Mitigation: a true customer-360 layer, enterprise data lineage.

4 · Stale CDD / KYC

Customer's documented activity profile is 4 years old; the scenario compares today's behavior to a baseline that no longer applies. Mitigation: pKYC, event-driven refresh.

5 · Model decay

A scenario tuned in 2019 is no longer optimal in 2026 — customer mix changed, payment rails changed, typologies evolved. Mitigation: ongoing performance monitoring with predefined re-tune triggers.

6 · Investigator turnover

L1 attrition is high; disposition quality degrades; case backlog grows. Mitigation: GenAI copilots, structured rationale templates, training calibration, automation of low-risk closures.

7 · Vendor lock-in

Legacy platform is expensive and inflexible but rip-and-replace is risky and migration takes years. Mitigation: parallel-run discipline, scenario-by-scenario migration, API-fronted detection layer.

8 · Weak feedback loop

Disposition data doesn't make it back to tuning or ML. The program improves slowly or not at all. Mitigation: disposition data pipeline, model governance forum attended by detection and investigations together.
